Climate and energy policy measures in the budget are a mixed bag with experts lamenting many missed opportunities for reform amid the energy crisis
By [Ben Potter](
The budget provides no new direct funding for clean energy projects but funds a new federal Environment Protection Agency with a brief to streamline and speed up project approvals — an obstacle to faster deployment — via bilateral deals with states. Picture: Goyder wind farm in South Australia.
Climate and energy policy measures in the [federal budget]( are a mixed bag that just passes muster overall — with conspicuous “fails” in the area of fossil fuel taxes — and draws brickbats from more activist groups.
The budget’s failure to reform light-as-a-feather taxation of oil & gas and the $11 billion fuel tax credit used mainly by big miners and — perversely — heavy freight operators stand out as the most glaring omissions.
Given the desperate need for more revenue to boost decarbonisation, meet pressing social needs such as housing and — one day — even balance the budget, it seems inevitable these omissions will dog the government until the next election.
“The direction outlined in the Budget supports many of the foundational elements required for a more electrified and consumer-driven energy system but we know there is always more work to do,” says Lisa Zembrodt, principal and senior director of advisory services at Schneider Electric.
All in all net new funding of about $10 billion has been provided for lowering emissions across the economy and enabling energy transition in this budget and the mid-year economic and fiscal outlook, mostly through the $7.2 billion cheaper home batteries scheme but also via agencies such as the Clean Energy Finance Corporation, National Reconstruction and Net Zero funds.
This is “a good start, but we need to be more ambitious yet, the cost of climate inaction is simply too extreme,” says Tim Buckley, director of Climate Energy Finance.
Federal incentives for clean energy such as the 40 gigawatt Capacity Investment Scheme, renewable energy targets and cheaper home batteries remain unchanged (the latter was trimmed back prior to the budget).
The new federal Environment Protection Agency — overseer of bilateral agreements with the states to streamline and speed up stalled clean energy project approvals — is expected to cost $250 million over the next two years.
But the changes to capital gains taxation create a “cloud of uncertainty” for foreign investment in renewables at a time when more capital is vital to meet climate targets and bring down costs, says Blair Palese, founder of 
Continuing industrial decarbonisation programs include $2 billion in capital grants for green aluminium production credits, $1 billion for green iron investment, $1.1 billion for “cleaner fuels” and Future Made in Australia (grants for clean tech manufacturing, production tax credits for critical minerals and “hydrogen headstart”).
The government will investigate a “new market measure to help drive demand” for low-carbon liquid fuels. At the same time, just over $2 billion has been cut from (mostly) hydrogen production credits and subsidies for solar panel and battery manufacturing.
Alison Reeve, climate and energy program director at the Grattan Institute, gives the government a “credit” here.
“While it’s great to see the government sharing risk with industry to transform the industrial sector, some of the previously announced funding is poorly structured and not being spent to best advantage. This has now been clawed back and programs like Hydrogen Headstart made more realistic,” Reeve tells _The Energy._
The budget provides $25.3 million to fund a new National Technical Regulator to streamline standards and help smoothly integrate consumer energy resources (CER) rooftop solar, home batteries, two-way EV charging and other consumer energy resource into the grid.
This is in addition to cheaper home batteries and EV incentives, and part of a $97 million CER roadmap.
It will be helpful at a time when home batteries and EVs are changing the role consumers play in the energy system, “coordination matters as much as generation capacity” and “the challenge now is making those technologies work cohesively at scale”, Zembrodt says.
“The next phase of energy technology will depend less on adding capacity and more on how effectively distributed technologies integrate into the grid. Continued focus on interoperability standards and grid capability will be essential as these systems scale nationally.”
The budget has confirmed changes that phase down the fringe benefits tax exemption to a 25 per cent discount — just above the 20 per cent discount for all non-EVs — for EVs above $75,000 initially and for all EVs after April 2029.
Reeve says this is a pass, because the savings seem predicated on the rich not buying cheap cars — “and $75k is still a pretty expensive car”.
“However, it is good to see the support being gradually phased down rather than cut all at once. The forward estimates show fuel excise holding up for a few years yet — not clear if this is expected high fuel prices or the EV uptake rate not yet moving the needle.”
EV sales have jumped since the Iran war began, and Reeve expects the FBT discount and the untouched issue of federal road user charging to be revisited pretty soon.
“Pressure will only increase as EVs become a larger share of the fleet. The government should speed up progress.”
There’s also $40.5 million to accelerate the rollout of EV chargers in regional black spots and on kerbsides.
Aside from the EV charging support and another $40.5 million to accelerate electrification of Australia Post’s fleet, there are no new measures to directly support the take up of electric heavy vehicles.
But there is an important reference to using National Competition Policy (NCP) — prodigal son of the 1990s Keating government cast into the wilderness by subsequent coalition governments — to “increase transport productivity and support the uptake of heavy zero emissions vehicles”.
NCP’s main role is to cut red tape, which is laid on thick and fast for heavy vehicles, severely restricting the supply of electric trucks coming into Australia, which badly lags Germany and China in take-up of the technology.
If it can achieve this, more global manufacturers of heavy electric vehicles will be attracted to Australia’s relatively small market.
Still, more direct assistance is still required to encourage freight operators to electrify — especially as the availability of fuel tax credits for heavy freight operators (see below) and their free pass under the Safeguard Mechanism continue to make diesel attractive.
There are no new incentives for oil, & gas production, but the budget confirmed $10 million for feasibility studies on expanding domestic refining capacity, a $7.5 billion fuel and fertiliser facility and an initial $3.2 billion to boost minimum reserves of diesel and jet fuel.
Reeve rated these efforts a “pass”.
“While feasibility studies are a better idea than rushing into commitments to new refineries that don’t have feedstock, I’d like to see this funding include refining capabilities for low carbon liquid fuels too.”
Ongoing light taxation of gas production and exemptions for fossil fuels drew criticism from many quarters.
Expected petroleum resource rent tax (PRRT) receipts have been boosted by $400 million a year by the Iran war but still raises a paltry $1.9 billion in 2026-2027 and just $6.45 billion over the four budget outlook years.
Fuel tax credits (FTC) — reduced by temporary crisis measures — are still expected to cost the budget nearly $11 billion in 2026-27 and a colossal $47 billion over the four years.
Perversely, fuel tax credits discourage large miners and heavy freight operators from electrifying their vehicle fleets by making diesel cheaper to use, at the same time as the Safeguard Mechanism encourages them to cut their carbon emissions.
Reeve labels both of these misses “fail”. “FTC reform is one of the few remaining “hollow logs” in Canberra that can and should be tapped for budget savings,” she says.
“The PRRT is fundamentally broken, and the Australian public regard the current taxation regime as unfair. We have a budget that’s urgently in need of extra revenue and the LNG industry won’t be around forever.
“If big gas export projects were viable under “normal” gas prices, the idea that a change to taxation makes them unviable doesn’t stack up.”
