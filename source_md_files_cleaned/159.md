Will the Middle East conflict provide an opportunity for Australian LNG producers to step up in place of QatarEnergy?
By [Kate Burgess](

European and Asian LNG prices skyrocketed on news that an Iranian drone struck QatarEnergy’s Ras Laffan hub, effectively halting supply from the region.
Europe's Title Transfer Facility shot up 40 per cent to €44.5/MWh while the Japan-Korea marker rose 68.52 per cent to $25.39 per million British thermal units.
The production shutdown may provide an opportunity for seaborne cargoes from other LNG producing nations to step in and plug Qatar’s 77 million tonnes per annum (mtpa) gap, which accounted for one-fifth of global LNG shipments in 2025.
Australia and Papua New Guinea together provide 89mtpa of global supply, but analysts argued that key producers including Woodside and Santos have little spare capacity in their projects that could be quickly mobilised to meet demand from Asian buyers.
UBS analyst Tom Allen estimated that Australian and PNG producers could only supply an extra 1-2 million tonnes. In a note, he wrote that most of QatarEnergy’s tankers were already en route to buyers in Asia and Europe when the Strait of Hormuz, through which most cargoes transit, effectively shut on Monday.
“Even if the Strait is blocked from LNG trade for days to a week, we do not expect it to have a material, enduring market impact as demand can be met via arriving cargoes (currently on the water) and storage.”
Institute of Energy Economics and Financial Analysis Kevin Morrison said that while Santos had recently begun ramping up production at its Barossa gas field that feeds Darwin LNG, progress at the newly commissioned site had been historically slow.
Meanwhile, Woodside is due to start producing from its Pluto 2 LNG train later in 2026, but in the interim overall production will be lower due to a scheduled outage at Pluto 1 in May.
Queensland’s three LNG projects operate at or near their nameplate capacities, so Origin, Shell and Santos would be unlikely to lift production volumes there, Morrison added.
INPEX and Total’s Ichthys LNG in the Northern Territory is producing slightly below full capacity, at around 8mtpa rather than 9.3, so production could theoretically be ramped up there.
Morrison believes that US LNG producers would be most likely to step up production because several projects there are due to expand their capacities in coming months.
“This year, an extra 46mtpa is expected to come online globally. This is the biggest net increase ever and most of that is coming from the US,” he said.
Analysts also believe that some Asian buyers such as Pakistan and Bangladesh are price-sensitive and would likely scale back their LNG purchases if prices jump too high.
