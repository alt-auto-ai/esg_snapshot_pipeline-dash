The Energy Made Easy site may be in for a funding boost but some say a lot more is needed to inform consumers as energy products evolve
By [Charis Palmer](

The Australian Energy Regulator’s Energy Made Easy website, which some consumer advocates have taken to calling “energy made hard”, could be in for a funding boost if a recommendation from Australia’s energy rule maker is taken up by the government.
Energy retailers have gotten behind the Australian Energy Market Commission’s pricing review recommendation that the site receive more funding, but many have questioned whether a functionality upgrade is enough to make it easier for consumers to compare retail offers.
“With smart meters coming through, it's enabling a whole bunch of new tariffs that are fundamentally changing consumers’ relationship with their biller,” Seniors Australia policy director Dr Brendon Radford told  _The Energy._
“And we’ve got a choice — do we keep going with the status quo, the winners and losers approach, or do we want to do something different and make sure that we're actually looking after people?”
Radford pointed to negative feedback his organisation receives from its membership of pensioners and self-funded retirees about smart meters.
“They get rolled onto a tariff they don’t understand, and then they get a bill shock. And their first reaction to that is ‘well that’s because of the transition’.
“And so the industry is at risk of creating more mistrust and undermining the transition,” Radford said.
“ Energy Made Easy, or energy made hard as we like to call it, is pretty abysmal really. It's very difficult to know what you're going to pay.”
The AER 
The regulator says it’s “currently working with government to explore opportunities for funding to upgrade the Energy Made Easy website, calculation engine and retailer portal to uplift accessibility and cybersecurity, increase the accuracy of comparison results, and update functionality to keep pace with the changing energy market and better support customers to switch plans”.
Asked specifically about what that updated functionality might entail, a spokesperson for the AER said improvements under consideration include “evolving how consumers compare energy plans to keep pace with the market, for example by looking at ways to compare Virtual Power Plant products”.
Energy Consumers Australia supports additional funding for the site, but in its submission to the pricing review says comparison tools alone have inherent limits. It says any new funding should be used to deliver “a true ‘onestop-shop’ for energy information and advice consumers can trust”.
The group’s consumer research has found only 12% of consumers report having used Energy Made Easy.
It points to ASIC’s MoneySmart initiative as an example of a service evolving beyond product comparisons:
“MoneySmart included content designed to help people navigate the system, avoid scams, understand product details, compare offers, understand their rights, and direct people to the ombudsman services. ASIC reports 1 in 2 Australians visited the site in the 2022/23 financial year – significantly more than the 12% of consumers visiting EME.”
Meanwhile, battery innovator Tesla says Australia’s current government-run comparison sites “do not yet compare offers based on quality metrics” and comparison “is no longer simply a price ranking exercise”.
It says consumers need to understand what level of automation is offered, what they retain control over, what risk is borne by the provider, and what rewards may be expected.
“This requires the AER to have both the funding and the governance process to keep pace with market innovation.”
In a veiled swipe at the regulator, AGLs submission to the pricing review says the AER already has a large budget and “whether this upgrade should be funded by a new allocation from Government or reprioritisation of existing funds is a matter for the AER board and Govt to consider in the context of overall budget and cost of living pressures”.
For now, Energy Made Easy and Victoria’s Energy Compare are the sites politicians and consumer groups point to as the answer for when prices spike or news surfaces of the loyalty tax inherent in Australian energy retailing.
But the AEMC’s pricing review goes well beyond retail competition and switching.
Consumer group CHOICE says it has seen many examples from consumers who received a ‘Better Offer’ or ‘Best Offer’ message referring to a plan not listed on a retailer’s website or Energy Made Easy at all.
“Consumers may be misled by these representations if they want to switch to the recommended plan but cannot in practice, or are falsely led to believe that they are unable to switch to it,” it says in its submission to the pricing review.
Seniors Australia’s Radford says consumers shouldn’t be bombarded with hundreds of options and the group is instead advocating for consumers on a cost-reflective tariff to pay the lower of two prices.
“You get the DMO and you get the bill for the price for the market offer that you chose. Whichever one is cheaper, is the one you pay. And if the one that is cheaper is the DMO, then you get some information on your bill, which says under your market offer, you could have done better if you'd done X, Y, and Z, because people need information at the billing point.”
