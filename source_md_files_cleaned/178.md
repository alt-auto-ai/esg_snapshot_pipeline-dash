Labor is backing local projects in regional Victoria to make sure even the smallest towns can thrive.
Minister Settle announced more than 100 projects have now been delivered through $20 million Tiny Towns Fund. 
The completed projects span across regional Victoria and range from town hall upgrades, new public spaces, arts and cultural activities to town activations and tourism attractions.
Across Gippsland Tiny Towns Fund has invested $517,723 for 27 projects, supporting local facilities, boosting community connections and helping small towns thrive.
This includes $25,000 to renew the Loch Memorial Reserve Playground in Loch and $11,076 for new male and female umpires changerooms in Nyora.
Upgrades across Gippsland are improving community facilities and accessibility, with new roofs and refurbishments at halls, pavilions and sporting clubs in towns like Boolarra, Briagolong and Ellinbank.
Near Ballarat, a $50,000 grant is helping the Inverleigh Mechanics Institute Reserve Committee of Management expand the capacity of the Inverleigh Public Hall through a new fully sealed storage shed. 
Projects are also strengthening inclusion and participation, with accessible upgrades in Hill End and Newhaven, community spaces in Neerim Junction and Omeo, and local programs in Neerim South.
At the same time, investments in sport and community life from Meeniyan and Outtrim to Tarwin Lower and Toongabbie – alongside events and tourism improvements in places like Paynesville and Sandy Point, are helping bring communities together and keep small towns active and connected.
Under the program, 24 town halls have been renovated and improved across the state, including Bolinda Hall in the Macedon Ranges, where the facility’s walking track received an upgrade to connect and keep people active.
Arts and culture have also been celebrated, with new lighting and signage installed at the Lake Boga Silo Art, helping attract visitors and bring in an economic boost. 
In an extra boost, regional businesses and workers are being hired to bring our Tiny Towns Fund projects to life, helping boost local employment and business demand. 
Labor has invested more than $50 billion in regional Victoria, in new schools, new TAFEs, new hospitals and better roads. 
Jess Wilson’s $40 billion in cuts can’t happen without cutting regional hospitals and schools — because that’s where most of the state budget goes.

Australia has an energy system being reshaped by overlapping decisions on infrastructure, industrial policy, land use, housing and transport
By [Matthew Benetti](
Australia's regulatory and policy settings date from a time before renewable energy began to dominate the grid - Supplied
Australia's energy transition is one of the largest coordination challenges the country has ever attempted, and aligning the many institutions responsible for it remains a work in progress.
Responsibility for planning the grid, designing the market, siting energy infrastructure, shaping industrial policy and securing social licence is spread across federal and state governments, different market bodies and regulators, network businesses, investors and communities. Their roles overlap, the lines between them blur, and their decisions don’t always pull in the same direction. Each is working hard, but they are not yet fully in sync.
The Integrated System Plan (ISP) is a case in point. Produced by AEMO, the ISP is the blueprint for the National Electricity Market. To produce its Optimal Development Path, which guides tens of billions of dollars in investment decisions on transmission, generation and storage, AEMO applies a ‘likelihood test’ that weights planning scenarios by their probability of occurring. The ISP reflects what is ‘most likely’ to happen, rather than the goals federal and state governments are working towards.
That distinction matters more than you might think.
Take industrial decarbonisation. Before industries can electrify, investors must have confidence that the renewable energy needed will be sufficiently available, affordable and reliable. Without that confidence, the scaled deployment of renewable energy projects is not treated as the 'most likely' scenario, which leads the ISP to plan more conservatively than necessary. That conservatism then becomes self-reinforcing. Cautious planning weakens confidence in future infrastructure, and weak confidence holds back the industrial commitment needed to justify it. This is the consequence of a governance framework designed decades ago to manage a largely static energy system, now being asked to lead its transformation.
The same disconnect plays out across the system. Demand-side resources are treated as secondary to new supply. Electricity and gas planning remain siloed, with no coordinated pathway for gas phase-out, even though electrification drives it. Transmission and generation siting proceeds without proper integration with government land-use and planning agencies. Industrial regions lack energy plans that connect local decarbonisation to national grid investment.
What’s at stake is Australia’s bid to establish itself as a 'renewable energy superpower'. In 2024 the Australian Government committed $22.7 billion over 10 years to build a Future Made in Australia (FMA). The FMA framework was designed to grow Australia's renewable energy and export industries by drawing on the country's unique endowment of wind, solar, critical minerals, infrastructure and skilled workforces. Delivering on that ambition means reforming how energy system actors coordinate and prioritise, and aligning markets and infrastructure rollout with Australia’s economic direction. But currently, no single body has the mandate nor the relationships to coordinate that work.
The May 2026 budget made efforts to acknowledge this, funding work to modernise electricity market settings, accelerate uptake of demand-side resources, and establish an investor front door to help significant projects navigate approvals across government.
These are useful steps, but tools for navigating fragmentation are not the same as resolving it. Building the conditions for energy planning to succeed means assembling a shared evidence base, aligning stakeholders across jurisdictions and sectors, and earning the social licence that infrastructure requires.
A forward focused policy-alignment test for the ISP would be a material improvement on the status quo. What matters more is that governments, governance bodies and market participants share a clear view of what they are working towards, and where the gaps remain.
That means coordinated land-use planning, a managed pathway for gas phase-out, industrial area energy plans that connect regional decarbonisation to national grid investment, transparent data sharing, and putting to work the full potential of the demand-side.
The energy transition will not be won by any single institution acting alone. It will be won by institutions that are clear on their purpose and limits, and willing to work across boundaries to seize economic opportunities while meeting the National Energy Objectives.
