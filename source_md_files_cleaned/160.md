The Energy Security Corporation is talking to battery developers to finance the next slate of battery projects to shore up the grid's post-Eraring future
By [Kate Burgess](
[Get The Energy in your inbox](
New South Wales will face a 500MW firmed capacity gap after Origin Energy's Eraring Power Station is shuttered in 2029, according to the head of its $1 billion storage financing agency.
The shortage will most acutely be felt in the Sydney-Newcastle-Wollongong region. There is enough generation capacity, but because the load comes mostly from solar generated during the middle of the day, batteries will be required to “time shift” it for use during the evening peak, Paul Peters argues.
The Energy Security Corporation is actively exploring novel financing structures to help bring needed projects to final investment decision (FID) faster so they can be brought online in time for Eraring’s closure.
“Projects face sequencing and timing challenges in reaching FID,” Peters told  _The Energy_.
“The ESC is seeking to address this by providing flexible capital, including subordinated debt, early draw facilities or accepting higher merchant exposure, enabling projects to proceed sooner and crowd in additional private investment.”
The agency began operating last July after being seeded with a $1 billion investment, however while Peters maintains its staff have been engaging with market participants across the state, it is yet to publicly announce its first deal.
“The ESC is working with a number of project proponents to reach FID as soon as possible,” Peters said.
Peters said some projects would need capital at an earlier stage to help get them moving. In project finance, debt is not usually provided to developers until after a final investment decision has been made. Under the ESC's model, a layer of mezzanine equity could be provided to encourage commercial lenders to back the project.
In its draft 2026 Integrated System Plan, which forecasts the electricity generation that will be required to meet the National Electricity Market's forecast demand within state and federal emissions targets, AEMO revised 2035 electricity demand upwards to 75TWh from 65TWh.
The anticipated demand explosion comes after 15 years of stagnant demand in the NEM between 2009 and 2024.
“We have a growth trajectory that is very real, and it’s driven by the electrification of transport and heat,” Peters told a CEDA gathering last week.
There were 100,000 EVs registered in NSW as of December 2025, and this is forecast to grow to 1 million by 2030.
Peters identified three gaps in commercially available financing for storage projects. “The first is the capital gap, where projects do not yet meet the risk-return requirements of banks or investors.
“The second is a timing and sequencing gap, where projects require flexible capital to allow it to reach FID sooner. The third relates to system needs, such as long-duration storage, where projects are critical to system reliability but may face greater financing constraints.”
NSW is aiming to have installed 2GW/16GWh of storage capacity by 2030 under its Electricity Infrastructure Roadmap. Given the typical two-year lead time to build and commission a battery, projects will need to be announced by early 2028.
Australian Energy Market Operator figures show that 272MW of battery capacity is in operation in the state, with a further 2.2GW committed. [Six eight-hour battery projects]( were offered long-term electricity supply agreements (LTESA) in February, bringing to 13 the number of battery projects that have been awarded these contracts.
Under an LTESA, battery operators receive the option to receive an annuity payment as a top-up to their revenue, but can elect to forego the payment if they expect particularly strong merchant revenue in a given period. The state now has a total of 200 battery projects either under construction or seeking planning approval.
The state’s latest Energy Security Target Monitor report points to a 274MW capacity shortfall in 2027-2028, but it was prepared on the basis of an Eraring Energy closure in 2027, before Origin Energy announced the shutdown would be delayed until 2029.
A further shortfall is expected around 2033-34, when the Bayswater power station is due to close.
