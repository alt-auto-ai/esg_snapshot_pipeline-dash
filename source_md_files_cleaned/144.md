Market operator seeks new data as consumers vote against orchestration
By [Charis Palmer](
The average home battery is size is now above 32Kwh - Charis Palmer
Lacklustre uptake of virtual power plant plans by Australian home battery owners will be reflected in the market operator’s revised 2026 forecasts as the sector comes to terms with the spike in supersized batteries.
”From a residential VPP participation point of view, the assumptions have been revised downwards, reflecting ongoing low levels of uptake and stakeholder feedback,” Australian Energy Market Operator forecasters today told a meeting of the Forecasting Reference Group.
The new forecast across the NEM sees less than 25% uptake by 2030, even under the Accelerated Transition scenario, and less than 15% under the Slower Growth scenario.
The market operator confirmed it, like the rest of the sector, doesn't have great visibility of the level of participation in VPPs, currently relying on an almost 12-month old report from the Australian Competition and Consumer Commission.
“We're currently in the process of reviewing and looking for better data sources with regards to VPPs, as well as reviewing how they're modelled going forward,” an AEMO modeller told the meeting.
VPPs are the main tool by which the operator could orchestrate rapidly growing consumer energy resources, but the lack of interest from consumers led battery storage and grid integration expert Lachlan Blackhall to recently [suggest]( "the age of the VPP might be over”.
Energy Consumers Australia will soon release new survey data showing what consumers say is holding them back from being part of a VPP.
“We know orchestrating CERs for solar and home batteries promises lower costs for everyone, and by unlocking behind-the-meter capacity. But for all their promise of public good, consumers seem to be rejecting or at least skeptical of the proposition of virtual power plants,” Energy Consumers Australia executive manager of advocacy and policy Lotte Wolff told the Smart Energy Council conference last week.
Wolff said the consumer group’s regular surveys were showing the risk-reward calculation was off for consumers.
“ Our survey shows that it's likely because VPP business models don't match why consumers purchase batteries in the first place.
“What we see in our six-monthly survey of over 4,500 consumers is that they buy batteries because they're interested in saving money on their electricity first and foremost, and they want to reduce their reliance on the grid and have access to backup power. So that energy independence piece is actually really important,” Wolff said.
They added that there was still skepticism from consumers about giving up control.
Worse than the current 15 per cent uptake figure for VPPs currently accepted in the market (Western Australia helps bring up the number due to its advanced VPP pilots and incentives), is the number of all consumer devices that can be seen by grid operators.
“Only about two and a half per cent of those systems are actually connected in a way that is grid aware,” Smart Connect initiative director Bruce Thompson said.
He added that while the technology was VPP-ready, there was still a crucial role for systems integration.
“There is a level of infrastructure that we don't have. This stuff won't integrate itself. Just like telecommunication systems, we need some level of infrastructure that will allow this to be like the telephone exchange. And once we do that, we start to make it more commercially viable, we solve the technical problem, and I'd like to think that we actually start to take some of that pain point away from a customer trust and value perspective.”
With consumers taking up larger batteries than initially anticipated by the designers of the cheaper home batteries scheme, some experts say VPPs may have become less important for time shifting demand.
“Actually, we may not need as many consumers to participate in VPPs if they're installing larger batteries,” Wolff said. “The ISP projections assume much smaller average battery sizes than what's currently being installed.”
Other products have also emerged, such as evening only feed-in tariffs that encourage battery owners to support the grid without signing up for a VPP.
Still, it’s not all downside for VPPs, said Wolff, with the ECA’s survey showing of those still considering whether to purchase a home battery, 80 per cent are somewhat or very interested in a VPP.
> #### Expert view
> “It's very difficult to compare if you join one VPP over another. How much are they going to use your battery? Will you have access to backup power? What's the payoff?
> It would be great if there was some sort of standardised way or a mandate that VPP providers give some minimum information to customers when they're comparing offers.
> A ‘no worse off guarantee’ is something that some VPP providers do, so you know you're not going to be left without power, in a blackout or financially, you're not going to be any worse off for (the VPP) operating your battery. I think those sorts of things would go a long way in terms of building trust and giving consumers the information that they can make a smart decision about on that large investment that they've just made.”
