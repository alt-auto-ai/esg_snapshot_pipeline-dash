The [EU Biodiversity Strategy for 2030]( aims to protect and restore nature and degraded ecosystems, with benefits for people, nature and the climate.
Research looking at links between biodiversity and socio-economic wellbeing in central and eastern European villages compared rural landscape use, the quality of life for people, and ecosystem health. They explored the trade-offs between biodiversity and human well-being, with closeness to cities and the quality of surrounding landscapes being key factors. 
Despite decades of intensified farming, many villages within farmed landscapes remain important refuges for wildlife, to varying degrees, but the type of rural land use affects rural villages in different ways. Socio-economic wellbeing was found to be correlated with village closeness to a city and the type of surrounding landscape. 
This research looked at 64 villages in Hungary and Romania, located around 16 mid-sized cities. Half of them were closer to cities, and half were farther away. They were categorised by landscape type and complexity – either agricultural landscapes (arable, pasture, fruit crops) or forest areas (broadleaved woodland, mixed forest, transitional scrub). The villages were broadly similar in population size, village size and soil quality.

They found biodiversity was on average 15% lower within villages with more homogenised agricultural landscapes, compared to forest-dominated landscapes. A village’s proximity to cities did not significantly affect biodiversity scores; overall, it was higher at village edges and declined towards village centres. This reduction was more pronounced in forest-dominated landscapes (20% decline) than in agricultural landscapes (15%). 
Wellbeing was 27% higher in villages near cities, compared with those farther away, and 14% higher in villages within forest-dominated landscapes, compared to villages in other farmed landscapes. Human pressures were similarly higher in villages closer to cities, regardless of whether they were farm or forest landscapes.
Researchers found that human pressures (the aforementioned measure of HFI) had an overall negative effect on biodiversity, and saw evidence of a trade-off between the HFI and biodiversity. Forested-dominated areas appeared to show a buffering effect, where there was still the highest biodiversity, and higher human wellbeing, even where there were increased human pressures.
To promote sustainable land management in villages, care for biodiversity and human wellbeing, the authors noted a need to consider different priorities depending on where the village is located and the type of surrounding landscape.
These variations should be incorporated into [rural development plans]( and policies. For example, in villages near cities, building development should minimise the practice of sealing-in soil with impermeable artificial surfaces such as concrete or asphalt, and reduce the intensity of green space management. Residents should adopt low-intensity, nature-friendly management of gardens and the areas in front of houses. 
By contrast, in forested areas, efforts and policies should focus on limiting agricultural expansion and intensification to preserve biodiversity and the forested landscapes. For villages near to cities and surrounded by forestry, conservation strategies should maximise the natural buffering quality that exists by preserving semi-natural habitats.
Conservation strategies should also preserve semi natural habitats and protect biodiversity surrounding the villages. They noted that villages edges had higher biodiversity than centres, particularly in forested areas. In agricultural landscapes, village green spaces should be improved via habitat creation and restoration, led by local residents and local authorities, whilst in surrounding landscapes the responsibility for biodiversity conservation lies crucially with landowners such as farmers and foresters. 
To enact these positive changes in public and private green areas, a combination of top-down (policy-driven) and bottom-up (community-driven) initiatives are needed. There could be incentives for sustainable agriculture, restoration projects for native habitats and village greening programmes, while bottom-up efforts might involve local non-governmental organisations engaging communities to promote biodiversity-friendly practices, such as using locally-native seed mixtures to enhance green spaces, and outreach to raise awareness of biodiversity needs.
Enhancing landscape connectivity is also recommended, particularly in forest-dominated landscapes, with potential for greater biodiversity benefits. Conservation strategies such as the use of uncut grassland strips and planting wildflowers applied in urban areas could also help counteract biodiversity loss in villages.
This focus on eastern and central European villages fills a key knowledge gap, with insights that can inform policy and community action to provide balanced support for ecological and human health. In particular, policies such as the [EU Rural Development Strategy]( could shift towards a stronger focus on biodiversity management using these findings to ensure more effective conservation actions and landscape improvements both in and around villages.
