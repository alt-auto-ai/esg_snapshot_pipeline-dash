Inventors of kerbside charging solutions for electric vehicle owners without off-street parking are finally breaking through with a handful of local councils
By [Ben Potter](
A Kerb Charge EV charger installed inside Kevin English's fence in Melbourne's inner bayside suburbs connects to a kerbside terminal for his EV to plug in - Supplied
Inventors of home-based kerbside electric vehicle charging solutions for households and businesses without off-street parking have battled years of indifference from local councils and policymakers.
But now they’re getting a boost from a handful of Melbourne and Sydney councils — just as to the US-Israeli war on Iran kickstarts EV sales. EVs and hybrids took 48 per cent of national sales in April, triple their February share, 
Mosman Council on Sydney’s lower North Shore on Tuesday last week adopted a “resident-led” EV charging policy allowing privately owned and operated EV chargers to be installed kerbside and connected via underground cable to residents’ premises.
“I think we will comply with that,” Williamstown-based accountant Rod Walker tells _The Energy._
Walkers’s company Kerb Charge has developed a pop-up kerbside charging outlet encased in stainless steel that drops down to sit flush with the footpath or nature strip when not in use, and connects via cable buried 500mm underground to a charger on the resident’s premises. A video 
Kerb Charge is already authorised by the City of Port Phillip to install its chargers in the inner bayside Melbourne local government area as part of a trial of up to 100 units — 22 are installed now — that Walker expects will be a precursor to a wider rollout.
Port Phillip Council CEO Chris Carroll says the council is now widening its nation-leading trial of resident-led EV kerbside charging to embrace two other prototypes that Mosman Council’s policy does not expressly (but in practice may) authorise.
These are:
—A gulley cut in the footpath — with a stiff rubber seal over the top for pedestrian safety — through which a charging cable can be run to the kerb to charge a vehicle and retracted into the vehicle owner’s premises when not in use. This was developed by the UK’s 
—A pole-and-boom-mounted elevated charging cable that swings over the footpath and nature strip when a vehicle needs to be charged, and retracts inside the vehicle owner’s premises when not in use, developed by Melbourne engineer Ross De Rango 
Carroll says 22 in-kerb units have been installed across Port Phillip by a local electrician. The council added Kerbo Charge's "gulley recessed unit" to the program last month, and is in the process of adding VCSA's elevated boom model, which is not yet available to residents. Like many councils it is also working to install more public EV pole-mounted and fast chargers.

The market for resident-led kerbside EV charging is small — De Rango estimates there are about 25,000 EV owners who lack off-street parking — but it’s growing at around 1000 a month and may accelerate further after the jolt from the war on Iran.
Carroll estimates about 90 per cent of Port Phillip residents live in multi-unit dwellings or lack off-street parking, and may face challenges with home charging. Just over 3.2 per cent — above the national average — of Port Phillip residents already own EVs or plug-in hybrids.
These residents are likely to want to benefit from the compelling economics of home charging just as much as EV owners with off-street parking (De Rango's estimate is based on about 500,000 EVs on the roads, and one in 20 car-owning households without off-street parking).
Walker, whose Hobson’s Bay local council has not authorised Kerb Charge’s model, says it costs him about $2700 to fill the petrol tank of his Honda Civic and drive 15,000 kilometres — a typical annual distance. That's almost twice the $1500 he estimates it would cost to charge an EV of the same size to drive the same distance, or more than 13 times the cost of charging at home on the 8c/kilowatt hour overnight EV rate offered by some retailers.
For Walker, an adventurous 72 year-old who sailed a 30 foot yacht from the US to Australia via Panama Canal in his mid-20s, the thaw in the attitude of a few local councils is the culmination of seven years spent developing his “Kerb Charger” and battling council bureaucracy to get it accepted.
He gets hundreds of calls from EV owners wanting kerbside charging but “the thing that’s really frustrating is that councils will not give us a run.” They’ll turn a blind eye to the “extension lead over the fence — there are literally thousands of those”. He suspects it might take an accident for them to take action.
A consultant hired by Port Phillip a few years ago even recommended the council kill off resident-led kerbside charging and concentrate on public charging, but the council decided to ignore this advice and back Kerb Charge’s trial deployment.
“I always worked on good ideas winning and bad ideas losing. And you know, right now, we're winning.”
Retired telecommunications engineer Kevin English was the first customer to be connected to Kerb Charge’s Port Phillip trial some three years ago, only to face the possibility of it being removed when the council temporarily got cold feet.
English had no objections from neighbours, and has only had to resort to commercial charging a handful of times when other vehicles blocked access to his charger.
Walker even made him a mechanical aid to pull the charger up and connect a cable to it when his left arm was injured. “He’s done a great job of engineering it,” English says.
Meanwhile, De Rango’s Vehicle Charging Solutions Australia (VCSA) already has approval for a trial deployment of up to 30 of the company’s boom mounted chargers in the Merri-bek local government area, which will be completed in June. Merri-bek is centred on Brunswick, Coburg and Pascoe Vale in Melbourne’s inner north.
The former robotics engineer is also eyeing a wider rollout, as a handful of councils in Melbourne and Sydney prepare to encourage more resident-led kerbside charging.
Based on feedback from the Merri-bek trial, it has refined its product, dispensing with a counterweight to slash the weight of the apparatus and responding to trial participants’ desire to be able to store it out of sight when not in use.
VCSA is in the process of launching the new version — VCS2 — across Melbourne and anticipates launching in Sydney in the not too distant future. This 
“We're currently working towards rolling out VCS2 across Melbourne with a particular focus on the inner suburbs,” De Rango says.
“We are heartened by the positive engagement that we have had from Merri-Bek and from Port Phillip in particular.”
A Mosman Council spokesperson says it wants to streamline its approvals under its “technology agnostic” policy, allowing for a variety of solutions to meet the criteria as the technology evolves.
It will not approve cables or similar infrastructure running across the footpath, which pose trip and other pedestrian hazards — or approaches that “negatively affect visual amenity in the public domain”.
Solutions that avoid these pitfalls and can be shown to be safe, feasible and consistent with the council’s policy — such as trenching, under-footpath solutions or other emerging designs — will be considered on their merits.
