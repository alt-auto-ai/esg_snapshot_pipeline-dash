The justification given by Australia’s energy rule maker for recommending higher fixed network charges has been questioned by the Australian Energy Regulator for failing to reflect the rapidly declining cost of electricity self-sufficiency.
Warning of a growing proportion of customers deciding whether to stay connected to the grid at all, the AER has used its submission to the AEMC’s draft pricing review to call for further analysis and suggest an alternative approach to future pricing.

The AER submission also points out the challenge of capturing the value delivered by customers with batteries using marginal pricing.
“Marginal export pricing cannot fully capture the network value of these flexible customers because the value lies primarily in the availability of the resource to respond when needed, not in the energy actually exported. However, a marginal price only compensates for realised actions, not for the standing capability (as network support) that allows network planners to defer investment,” the submission points out.
“For these customers, the network’s avoidable cost may be zero or even negative once this value is taken into consideration – that is, this customer disconnecting from the grid may increase network costs, rather than avoiding them. This raises the question of whether a uniform fixed charge can remain efficient when the value of remaining connected varies materially across customers – and, if not, what principle should guide differentiation of network charges.”
The contribution comes as the AEMC fields thousands of submissions from consumers responding to a [campaign]( against higher fixed charges by community-based lobby group Solar Citizens.
Solar and storage lobby groups and most consumer advocates, with the exception of Energy Consumers Australia, have warned against higher fixed charges, arguing it could add to the bills of people who don’t use much energy and create large cross-subsidies from efficient and lower-income households to high consumption homes.
The AER suggests customers could instead be grouped into a small number of broad tiers (such as low, medium and high peak demand) based on their observed capacity usage patterns over time.
“This approach could better align what customers pay for accessing the network with the network capacity they actually use during network peaks. In comparison to a more granular approach with individual price differentiation, this approach would be stable enough to support reliable residual recovery and avoid large and unpredictable changes in customer behaviour.”
The AER says this approach would limit disproportionate impacts on low-use customers, many of whom don’t have access to consumer energy resources.

It flags a disproportionate impact on small business and regional energy users in its network.
“Essential Energy’s tariffs already include one of the highest proportions of fixed charges in the National Electricity Market (NEM). This is largely a product of the characteristics of Essential Energy’s network, with the lowest customer density in the NEM and many thousands of kilometres of assets across remote, bushfire-prone and challenging terrain.
“If the share of fixed charges within network tariffs increases further, it is likely that many of Essential Energy’s regional customers will feel this most acutely,” the submission states.
Both the AER and the energy giants have suggested network tariff design could be better addressed through the AEMC’s broader Electricity Network Regulation Review.
The AEMC continues to consult and says any changes could be expected to be rolled out over a period as long as a decade.
