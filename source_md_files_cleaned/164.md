Investment in new renewable energy projects is running laps around the targets required for Australia to hit its net zero targets, RenewMap has found in a new analysis showing the gap between ambition and delivery.
Some 450GW of renewables are currently under development in the NEM – more than twice as much as the 200GW previously said to be required by 2050 for Australia to meet its targets and replace its coal fleet.
Looking nationally, there are more than 670GW of utility-scale projects under development – with onshore wind (155GW) outpacing utility solar (90GW) and offshore wind (26GW), all topped by 185GW/530GWh of BESS capacity.
The numbers belie the constant criticism that renewables can’t provide a viable alternative to legacy generation methods, said Alex Thompson, co-founder and director of RenewMap as he prepared to front today’s 
The obstacles facing renewables lie not in “ambition or origination,” Thompson said, but “the friction between stages, particularly approvals and grid connections, and how that affects capital’s confidence in delivery timelines.”
Government investment policies have had different effects in different states, with Western Australia’s SWIS Transmission Plan accelerating investments in that state because it’s unlocking projects that were previously harder to finance.
The Victorian pipeline, by contrast, is being “driven strongly“ by large BESS projects and the more than 1GW of new Victorian wind developments approved last year. Queensland, by contrast, has seen onshore wind paused since late 2024 even as solar and BESS continue strongly.
Thompson flagged the growing backlog of EPBC assessments that is slowly extending approval timeframes, with that pipeline growing from around 65 projects in 2024 to around 140 this year.
Despite investor enthusiasm, Thompson said, blowouts in approval timeframes were creating drag on the industry - with onshore wind developments taking a median of 30 months for approval in NSW, four times longer than in Queensland and five times longer than in WA.
BESS approvals were relatively quick overall, being approved faster in most states than even solar PV installations – a strength for Australia, Thompson said in calling the BESS pipeline “a genuine competitive advantage on the global stage".
