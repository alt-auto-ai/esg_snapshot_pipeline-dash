Large inverter based loads would be forced to adhere to new grid access standards under a proposed rule change responding to the unprecedented level of growth being seen in data centres connecting to the grid.
The Australian Energy Market Commission has gone further than outlined in an initial consultation 
The proposed changes would enable this, but also require data centres of 30MW or greater to ride through certain frequency or voltage disturbances in order to prevent cascading failures and potential blackouts. The standards would not be applied retrospectively, but instead to those who have not yet received an offer to connect.
It comes as energy regulators globally [assess]( the risks of data centre connections to system security, and on the back of a now widely reported [event]( in Virginia’s ‘Data Centre Alley’ where 60 data centres disconnected from the grid after they detected voltage fluctuations and enabled backup power.
The classification of a large load as 30MW is significantly higher than the current threshold of 5MW or greater in AEMO’s System Strength Impact Assessment Guidelines, responding to industry concerns that onerous compliance requirements could deter investment.
AEMC Chair Anna Collyer said the standards were “designed to enable investment with certainty, not block it”.
“Data centre operators would know exactly what’s required upfront. Network service providers would be able to apply technical connection standards consistently,” Collyer said.
The new standards would introduce three tiers for loads at the distribution level to address current inconsistencies in how large loads are being treated:
  * Tier 1 connection for inverter based loads with a nameplate rating of up to 30MW, and non-inverter based loads regardless of its MW nameplate rating.
  * Tier 2 connection for inverter based loads with a nameplate rating of at least 30MW but less than 100MW.
  * Tier 3 connection for inverter based loads with a nameplate rating of 100MW or greater.
The AEMC says current inconsistencies are reducing AEMO’s visibility and ability to plan for system security needs.
It also says the approach reflects what’s already happening in other jurisdictions.
“By largely matching the standards proposed or used in Texas, Ireland, and Finland, data centre operators could use the same equipment and feasibility studies as they do elsewhere. This standardised approach would mean faster deployment, lower costs, and better investment certainty,” the rule maker said in a statement.
Feedback on the draft rule change is due by May 7.
