The AER has relented to pressure by Victorian distributors, approving revenue increases of around 12% on the promise more broadly distributed demand will drop consumer bills at the same time
By [David Braue](
Victoria’s DNSPs have claimed a victory in their negotiations with the Australian Energy Regulator (AER), successfully clawing back revenue increases they say are necessary to ensure network stability and performance in the face of surging electricity demand as electrification initiatives take hold and Australians swarm to EVs.
The AER’s draft decisions on revenues for the 2026-2031 shaved $1.57 billion from the initial revenue requests of AusNet, Jemena, CitiPower, Powercor and United Energy – which [howled]( that the reduced revenues would stymie planned network investments.
This, for example, included $86 million of proposed Powercor investments that the company argued would unlock over $178 million in customer value and limit customer participation in the energy transition “at a time where their participation is required most.”

This includes accepting some DNSP arguments for revenue increases, rejecting others, and in some cases “identifying other solutions to address consumers’ concerns and deliver the outcomes they were seeking at a lower cost.”
The decisions include a combined $119 million for network and community resilience initiatives during and after extreme weather events, and over $112 million in new investment “to support network reliability”.
Data centre operators will be steered towards “paying their own way when connecting to the distribution network,” Gallagher said, with those operators paying the cost of direct grid connections and a “fair" portion of shared distribution network costs.

Despite increased revenue recovery, the AER estimates that Victorian power customers are “likely” to see annual electricity bills decrease by $6 to $38 through 2031.
That’s because higher demand would spread the revenue increases across more customers, the AER said, “leading to lower bills than in the current period” – although it qualified the estimates by pointing out that bill reductions “depend on whether the increases in demand are realised.”
The regulator also approved a combined $199 million “to better integrate consumer energy resources” (CER) and provide pricing options to encourage customers to reduce their impact on the network.
Although pricing and delivery of CER related programs “is a new and uncertain area where the quantification of costs and benefits is not straightforward,” the AER wrote of its decision to provide United Energy with $35.2 million for CER integration, “on balance we see merit in strengthening United Energy’s CER capability in the immediate term given the longer-term benefits to consumers.”
Those benefits may also involve increased availability of kerbside EV chargers along DNSP networks – an area about which the Victorian government recently [drew a line in the sand]( as DNSPs and commercial EV charging providers debate the best model to deliver expanded charging infrastructure for consumers.
The AER has supported exploration of those issues with a previous ring-fencing exemption to facilitate kerbside charging trials, but in its new final determination it cautioned that Victorian DNSPs’ demand for “significant uplifts in expenditure” needed to be cost justified, with new investment needing to “clearly target where and how demand on the network is changing.”
“It’s critical that network businesses get the most out of the network we already have,” Gallagher said, “and look at alternative, non-network solutions where possible so that consumers only pay for targeted investment where and when it’s needed.”
The final decisions come into effect from July 1.
