Many companies have, in fact, set and achieved “100% renewable” targets. This includes the four big tech players — Amazon, Meta, Alphabet and Microsoft — with Australian players not far behind. 
While these achievements are positive, it's important not to conflate “100% renewables” with "zero emissions", or use it uncritically as evidence data centres are enabling the transition.
The reason for this is that there is a wide difference between the electricity companies purchase on paper and the electricity that actually powers their operations.
Under market-based accounting systems, companies can buy renewable energy certificates or other instruments that are disconnected from the electricity they consume. A data centre drawing power from a coal-heavy grid at midnight can still claim to be operating on renewable energy if it owns certificates generated by a solar farm hundreds of kilometres away during the middle of the day.
Location-based emissions accounting tells a very different story. Emissions are measured according to the actual carbon intensity of the grid supplying electricity at the time it is consumed. Geography and timing are both important here, the latter especially due to the fact that data centre electricity demand is relatively flat, while most renewable generation remains intermittent.
“100% renewables” claims are usually market-based, with companies still reporting significant location-based emissions. 
For these reasons, it's important that data centre developers aren’t scrutinised on their headlines, but on their underlying procurement practices.
Right now, it’s difficult to know. Procurement disclosures by the hyperscalers are often patchy, or opaque. Amazon, for example, publishes a list of its renewable projects, but not of its emitting ones.
What we do know is that all four have grid-connected data centres (which incur location-based emissions) as well as off-grid fossil fuel generation assets. Their “100% renewables” claims mean that, annually, they purchase enough renewables to offset these emissions. But, due to disclosure limitations, it's hard to ascertain how credible and additive these renewables are.

In Australia, the story has, historically, been simpler. Big players like NextDC and AirTrunk have connected their data centres to the grid, with location- and market-based emissions mostly being equal. But, this is changing.
The disconnect between renewable claims and emissions is evident in the emissions progress of the large tech companies. Despite all achieving 100 per cent renewables, Microsoft, Meta and Alphabet are struggling to meet their 2030 emissions targets and there’s a risk that Microsoft’s time-matched renewable target isn’t the only one rolled back. Between 2021 and 2024, their location-based emissions increased by 72 per cent or more.
In the coming years, data centre operators in Australia are likely to achieve their renewables and market-based emissions targets, while driving investment in renewable projects through PPAs and other mechanisms. But, if current approaches don’t change, data centres will also continue to drive the generation of fossil-fuelled electricity, producing higher emissions, even after they achieve “100% renewables”. In this case, there is a high risk that data centres will delay the energy transition, rather than enable it.
While headlines like “100% renewables” are memorable, there isn’t a single benchmark to meet. The details of a renewable strategy matter. For this reason, improving disclosure does, too.
The next port of call is maximising renewables in a way that is physically and temporally connected to demand. This means prioritising projects that contribute to the decarbonisation of the local grid, rather than relying on certificates or distant assets.
