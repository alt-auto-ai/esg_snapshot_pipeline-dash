The pace of permanent gas disconnections is likely to surprise, with electricity filling the demand, yet distributors currently have no systematic access to gas disconnection data
By [John Godfrey](
Electricity distributors do not have sufficiently granular data on gas disconnections - Rawpixel
Victoria has 2.2 million gas customers spread across 34,000km of pipeline. On peak winter days, residential heating accounts for more than half of total gas consumption, more than 550 terajoules a day. In summer, it falls to near zero. That enormous seasonal swing is about to land on the electricity distribution network.
Heat pumps are far more efficient than the gas heaters they replace. Even so, converting that winter gas peak to electricity means something in the order of 30GWh per day of additional demand, roughly a 20 to 25 per cent increment on Victoria's average daily electricity consumption. It will arrive on feeders and transformers that were never sized for it, concentrated in the months when rooftop solar output is at its lowest.
Victorian distributors are already reporting undervoltage in suburbs with high electrification uptake, and two have projected that 10 per cent of their zone substations will flip from summer-peaking to winter-peaking within the current regulatory period. Yet electricity distributors currently have no systematic access to gas disconnection data. They cannot see what is coming, or where it will hit at the substation and transformer level.
This isn't a data problem that better modelling can fix on its own. It's a coordination gap. No institution has the authority to match where and when gas networks shrink with where and when electricity networks need to expand. And the timeline is shorter than the regulatory language suggests.
The Australian Energy Market Commission's review of a proposed rule change, "Gas Networks in Transition," outlines sensible reforms to gas network regulation: accelerated depreciation, tighter capital expenditure tests, a 20-year planning outlook. But the AEMC has been unusually candid about what it cannot do. A decommissioning framework? Beyond its scope. Coordinating gas retirement with electricity network investment? Not its job.

At the same time, the Australian Energy Market Operator is scoping its inaugural Distribution Statement of Opportunities, which will attempt to forecast distribution-level demand growth. But state-level or even zone-level averages won't help a distributor decide which transformers need upgrading next winter. That requires spatially granular gas disconnection data, matched to electricity network geography, that currently doesn't exist in any systematic form.
Three institutions, three proceedings, one gap. Even the Energy Consumers Association, the proponent of one rule change and a major submitter to the other, has been forced to make the same coordination argument twice in two separate silos, assessed against different statutory objectives.
Victoria has legislated net zero emissions by 2045. The 20-year planning horizons being introduced in both rule change requests arrive at almost exactly that date. The logical endpoint for gas distribution networks under Victoria's own legislation is not managed decline but complete closure. Yet the proceedings consistently frame the future in the language of "uncertain demand outlook." Nobody is openly naming what the legislation already implies.
And the decline won't be gradual. As fixed network costs are spread across fewer remaining customers, rising bills accelerate further departures, producing an S-curve, not a straight line. The pace of closure is likely to surprise.
Ministerial direction through the Energy and Climate Change Ministerial Council remains essential for the larger questions: who authorises geographically targeted gas network retirement, and who funds the transition for households that can't self-fund.
But the AEMC's draft determination on “Enhancing distribution network planning & reporting”, with submissions closing on 4 June, offers an immediate opportunity. Three rule-level changes would make a material difference, none requiring legislation.
The new Distribution Network Development Plan requires scenario analysis, but the scenarios are framed entirely around consumer energy resources: solar, batteries, electric vehicles. Gas-to-electric load transfer is a fundamentally different demand driver, spatially concentrated and overwhelmingly winter-peaking. The final rule should require electricity distributors to account for fuel-switching demand as a distinct planning input.
The draft rule's joint planning provisions require coordination with "other Network Service Providers", which under the National Electricity Rules means other electricity networks. There is no mechanism for cross-fuel coordination with gas distributors, even though in Victoria the three gas and five electricity distribution territories do not align. The final rule should explicitly include gas distributors in the joint planning framework.
And the new data reporting framework should require reporting on gas connection density and electrification readiness at the spatial resolution that matters for network planning: not state averages, but data matched to distribution zones and substations, so that a distributor can identify which transformers face the earliest and largest heating load transfers.
These are drafting choices within the AEMC's existing remit. The larger institutional question of who plans and sequences gas network retirement remains unanswered. But making the electricity planning framework aware of the gas transition would be a start.
