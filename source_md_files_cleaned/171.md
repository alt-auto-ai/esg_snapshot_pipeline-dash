Gentailer joins consumer advocates in calls for the regulator to reject an unprecedented reopener application
By [Charis Palmer](
AGL executive Ralph Griffiths has delivered a scathing assessment of monopoly network owner Transgrid over its mismanagement of Australia’s largest electricity transmission project.
Project EnergyConnect, originally approved at a cost of $2.28 billion in 2021 but now running to more than $4 billion, has been beset by problems, including the failure of the original construction contract.
In February, Transgrid applied to the Australian Energy Regulator for an unprecedented reopener of its 2023-28 revenue determination asking for an additional $1.1 billion to be recovered from consumers.
At the time, Transgrid CEO Brett Redman said the reopener application was necessary due to “significant external factors outside Transgrid’s control” that impacted the contractor’s ability to deliver the project.

By not escalating oversight earlier, Griffiths said, Transgrid entrenched a weakened negotiating position and materially increased its cost exposure.
“This reflects a failure to translate emerging risk into timely control, not a loss of control itself. In these circumstances, the subsequent contract failure cannot reasonably be characterised as beyond Transgrid’s reasonable control for the purposes of the reopener rules,” Griffiths wrote.
He also called into question Transgrid pointing to the urgent need for the project for system security and reliability reasons. Project EnergyConnect has been included in the Integrated System Plan since 2020.
Griffiths said any risk to system reliability has been resolved by AGL’s September 2025 agreement with South Australia’s government to extend its Torrens Island gas-fired power station to June 2028.
Consumer advocates Louise Benjamin and Gavin Dufty warned the additional cost could add to energy bills “for decades” and when looked at cumulatively “might be the choice between a solar system or a battery”.
In a submission Benjamin and Dufty said the reopener would set an important and significant precedent for future overspends for major projects, and could send a signal that such projects are too big to fail.
Allowing the cost recovery “may embolden other contractors and guarantors on major transmission builds to threaten repudiation and run the risk that the terms of the original contract will not be enforced”.
Meanwhile, the Justice and Equity Centre said in its submission that the reopener rule does not apply where expenditure has already been undertaken, and as a result must be rejected.
The decision by Transgrid to continue with the project in 2023 without applying to the AER for increased expenditure “amounts to a bet, with consumers' money, that the project would be delivered at the original cost, despite all evidence to the contrary,” the JEC wrote.
Benjamin and Dufty, along with the Justice and Equity Centre say an ex post review would be a better approach to ensure fairer cost allocation.
This could include a portion of the costs being recovered by consolidated revenue, the Rewiring the Nation Fund or measures other than NSW electricity bills, Benjamin and Dufty wrote.
