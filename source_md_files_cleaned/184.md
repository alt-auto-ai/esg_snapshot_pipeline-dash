CEO Frank Calabria reveals talks with potential partners for its new Eraring Energy batteries, but warns on cost inflation for new wind and gas projects
By [Kate Burgess](
Origin Energy's Frank Calabria chats with the Australian Energy Council's Louisa Kinnear at the AEC's conference - Helene Cochaud
Origin Energy is in talks with potential data centre partners at its Eraring site, where two batteries are operational and a third is planned, with the 2880MW coal-fired power plant set to close in April 2029.
CEO Frank Calabria declined to name a partner as discussions were early, but said Origin would look to co-locate data centres near the Eraring batteries like it is also doing at Supernode in Queensland, in an interview with  _The Energy_ on the sidelines of the Australian Energy Council conference in Sydney.
”There's a lot of work going on right in that space right now. We've still got the Eraring batteries to come on and some at our Mortlake power station in Victoria, and they're all tracking ahead of schedule,” he said.
For Origin, batteries are the bright spots of its energy transition journey, with most projects coming in on time and on budget and in the case of Supernode, the option to purchase the electricity on a cheaper capital-lite model. More vexing has been getting large east coast wind farms like Yanco Delta, which has a Capacity Investment Scheme (CIS), through to financial close.
Origin was[ seeking equity partners]( for the 1.5GW project in the NSW Riverina as of April, [which it acquired in 2024,]( but Calabria declined to comment on whether those talks had resulted in a deal, allowing the project to reach financial close and move into construction.
In his speech to the conference, Calabria lamented that wind farm construction costs had risen 50 per cent since 2020 on a combination of supply chain disruptions from Covid, Trump trade tariffs and more recently, inflated fuel costs from the Middle East oil crisis.
> “The problem is that the economics of delivering large-scale infrastructure in Australia have become genuinely difficult. This is not a problem unique to energy, but it is showing up most visibly in our sector given the scale of infrastructure we’re trying to deliver.
> “Wind projects that were bankable a few years ago, are now finding it very hard to clear the hurdle. That is a problem, because wind is essential to the system we're building.”

The federal government has agreed to re-negotiate some of the 19 CIS contracts because they were awarded with feed-in tariffs that were priced when construction costs were lower, making them uneconomic at today’s costs. The projects have not been publicly named.
Calabria insisted Origin is keen to press ahead with Yanco Delta, which if built today would be nearly three times the size of Australia’s next largest wind farm, the 736MW Golden Plains Stage 1 in Victoria. Other early stage wind farms on the company’s slate include the Northern Tablelands and Skye Hill projects in the New England REZ, which must be sequenced along with the transmission buildout in the area.
Calabria highlighted the need to reform the East Coast gas market as among the most critical measures to expedite the energy transition.
While he supports the plan to force gas exporters to contract 20 per cent of their supply to the domestic market to avert shortfalls “at a high level..in terms of it providing the right signal to enable investment but achieve the outcomes [government] wants, I think there's a lot of discretion retained by ministers.
“What does it mean to be an oversupplied market? How does that work? How does forcing supply in work? You could see why they create quite a lot of uncertainty, and so the industry's engaging now to see if we can get that to work,” he said, referring to the consultation process that [closes in late June](
A consultation paper said the government was working toward having the market in a constant 110 per cent oversupply, which Calabria said was “ very difficult for suppliers to come to terms with”. He echoed concerns by other gas players that it could crowd out smaller domestic producers, and encourage exporters to direct future investment overseas.
“ I think if you looked at it today, people would be feeling uncertain about their ability to invest based on that scheme,” he concluded.
The spiralling costs of investing in the transition has led to heightened conflict over who should pay, with some campaigns such as the Electrical Trade Union’s [Sovereign Power]( calling for an increased role for government.
Because network costs and developer subsidies ultimately hit customer bills in a cost of living crisis, governments have been designing industry support like the CIS contracts to be funded directly from the budget so they are not passed onto consumers.
Speaking to  _The Energy_ at the same event, Alinta Energy CEO Jeff Dimery said he supported underwriting schemes, but criticised the CIS as “lacking transparency, because it is impossible to see how it is accounted for in the federal budget”.
Most energy retailers seek to use consumer energy resources (CER) like home batteries to avoid larger investments, but have struggled to attract loyal customers to engage with Virtual Power Plants, which Calabria put down to the difficulties in selling the benefits while meeting regulatory demands.
“Right now, we're leaving most of that value on the table. Only one in six Origin customers with a battery is currently connected to our VPP. That leaves the rest sitting idle from the grid's point of view,” he said.
“Upping the VPP participation rate is one of the cheapest and fastest ways to take pressure off bills and shore up reliability for everyone."
