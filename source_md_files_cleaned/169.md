Utility-scale batteries may be the poster child of the NEM, helping to flatten the dreaded duck curve, but there's one thing they can't fix
By [Kate Burgess](
Australia's National Electricity Market is transitioning, tipping the balance towards renewables - Open Electricity
Your personal facebook feed is not where you’d expect to find an 
Yet, in a brightly-hued tapestry of colours there it was - an area chart showing the times of day that the National Electricity Market’s growing battery fleet stepped up to set the price of electricity.
AEMO used this chart as its social media “hero Image” to headline how the NEM has been rapidly changing in the past quarter, with 4,460MWh added to the market’s storage capacity, bringing the addition to 11,219MWh over the year.
Batteries’ mushrooming contribution to the grid is best explained by the massive 1,115MW they collectively managed to shift from the daytime hours to the evening peak in Q1, as 872MW of extra capacity sprung into action during the quarter.
This can also be seen in Q1 pricing data, which show prices turning negative less of the time, in a move that one AEMO staffer described as “flattening the belly of the duck curve”, and helping to provide energy when consumers need it most.
But are batteries too helpful? Already, they are edging out gas peakers and pumped hydro, which traditionally fire up and dispatch when evening demand begins to rise. This pattern is partly seasonal, because batteries soak up more electricity on summery sunny windy days than in the winter months when solar irradiation is lower and there is a lull in wind speeds.
The fact that it is happening so much in the summer months when there is plenty of renewable generation, indicates that gas plants will increasingly have to rely on cap contracts - a type of derivative that energy retailers use to limit their exposure to price volatility - rather than revenue from physical generation to stack up economically.
In the eyes of Rystad analyst David Dixon, this is only going to become more prevalent over the coming months. “We've got 16GW roughly, of batteries that are either under construction in commissioning or fully operational.
“At the present time, only about a third or less than a third of that is fully operational. But because these projects don't take that long to build, all that capacity will largely be online in the next 12 months.”
While electricity demand is hitting new records thanks to increased electrification, peak demand and price volatility are not rising to the same extent. Eventually the market will not require both the gas peaker and battery fleets simultaneously, Dixon adds.
“So you'll just get time in the year where you're not going to need to switch on a gas plant in the evening, particularly if you've still got coal in the system, because the coal will still be on at that evening peak as well.”
As Dixon points out, batteries are less of an enemy to gas peakers than the ageing coal plant fleet that governments are very reluctantly allowing to exit the market.
“We obviously want to bring those gas plants online before the coal exits. But the problem is the market signal isn't there before the coal exits.”
The utilities and investors building batteries are now making unprecedented levels of arbitrage profits, helping to offset a decline in the value of frequency and ancillary control services (FACS) revenue, which has flatlined in recent months everywhere across the NEM except for Tasmania.
The profitability of batteries is now inextricably linked to arbitrage spreads between the price of power at which they charge and that at which they discharge later in the day when demand lifts. While the battery fleet made a record amount of revenue from arbitrage, their profitability was lower because arbitrage spreads narrowed during the quarter.
With more batteries constantly coming online, if the NEM continues to exhibit lower volatility in coming months, profitability from arbitrage activities will continue to fall.
Batteries are able to solve a lot of problems in the NEM, but one thing they can’t do is prevent solar and wind farms from having their output curtailed when they flood the grid with too much electricity, especially in South Australia where it is most prevalent.
Curtailment rose steadily throughout 2025, and continued to trend upwards during Q1.
