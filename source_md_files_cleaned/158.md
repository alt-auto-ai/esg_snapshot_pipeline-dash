Default offers were looking quite good until the latest network costs landed
By [Ben Potter](
The Australian Energy Regulator has set its network tariffs for the next financial year (2026-27) for National Electricity Market jurisdictions other than Victoria, which is in the final throes of a hard fought five-year reset for its distribution networks.
Network tariffs for NSW have been bumped by between 5.5 per cent and 11.5 per cent in NSW distribution network areas for 2026-27, the AER announced on Friday.
Based on network costs comprising roughly 40 per cent of retail bills for residential and small business customers, this should translate — all other ingredients remaining unchanged — into bumps of 3 per cent to 5 per cent in final Default Market Offer determinations due in four weeks.
Of course, the other ingredients are unlikely to remain unchanged. Just what change there might be in the other big component — wholesale cost of energy — in the aftermath of the US-Israeli attacks on Iran and closure of the Strait of Hormuz is unclear.
National Electricity Market Futures have been [impeccably behaved ]( they are below their level when the Draft DMO was set — but this may reflect the gas industry's superhuman efforts to ensure the domestic east coast market is well supplied and NEM Futures are not spooked.
The AER will have to decide if this paints a true picture of NEM prices that can be reasonably expected in 2026-27.
In the meantime, network tariffs — comprising distribution and transmission network costs — are going up, and that will have an impact on the final DMO.
The may yet be other moving parts in the final DMO calculation.
For example, one large NSW customer has just been advised that its transmission charges are jumping 42 per cent as a result of megaprojects such as EnergyConnect and Humelink, resulting [in an overall 19.2 per cent increase in network charges](
While this customer may be atypical, it shows the costs of large transmission projects are starting to hit customers' bills, and this could have an impact on the final DMO determination.
Green costs have been restrained but may also be bumped up by any federal budget initiative to kickstart wind projects which are struggling to win final investment decisions thanks to rising capital costs and deteriorating economics.
All of that means any respite from high energy bills the [Draft DMO issued last month]( may be short-lived, or at least not as generous as signalled in the draft determination.
The Draft DMO held out the very welcome prospect of cuts in benchmark total energy bills ranging from 2.4 per cent to 8.2 per cent for residential customers in NSW and 7.6% per cent to 21.2 per cent for small business customers in NSW (add 4 percentage points for real — after inflation — cuts).
Self-evidently, increases in network costs — including any increases in transmission costs still to emerge — will trim these eagerly anticipated bill reductions if not wipe out some of the smaller reductions.

The same approach with the same caveats can be used to estimate the impact of network tariff increases on final DMO determinations in Southeast Queensland (Energex) and South Australia (SA Power Networks).
Network tariffs are going up between 10 per cent and 12 per cent for residential customers in these two distribution areas, and by 4.4 per cent for small business customers in SA and 12.6 per cent to 13.2 per cent for small business customers in SE Queensland.
This translates to bumps in the final DMO of just 1.8 per cent to 1.9 per cent for small business customers in SA and 4.4 per cent to 5.2 per cent for residential customers in SA and both categories of retail customers in SE Queensland.
SA residential customers saw the slimmest reductions in the Draft DMO so they could be wiped out by increases in network tariff costs. Energex residential customers had a 10% reduction in the Draft DMO so they should be able to absorb the estimated increases from network tariffs and still come out ahead.
Small business customers in both jurisdictions had large reductions in the Draft DMO and should still come out ahead after the increased network tariff costs.
Remember though: these estimates are based on the network tariff determinations available now and other elements — such as increases to transmission charges and any impacts from the Solar Sharer tariffs — may emerge to alter the picture.
"This analysis looks static," said Gavin Dufty, national director energy policy and research at St Vincent de Paul Society.
"The information is based on what's available now and there are many moving parts that aren't settled yet before the final determination, but it indicates upward movement," he said
For example, Dufty added, wholesale cost are not settled; the new Solar Sharer and the “free time” is not settled (this has a feedback to other consumer groups and with network regulation under a price cap regulation the cost gets reallocated); and there will also need to be inclusions of the costs of various local jurisdictional schemes.
