Heavy industrial energy users are not like ordinary customers and shouldn’t be treated as such.
By [Alison Pennington](
Big industrial users such as the Tomago smelter need reliable, cheap, long-term power agreements - Google Earth
Geopolitical pressures are making sovereign industrial capability a top priority at the same time as energy costs are putting one heavy industry after another onto life support.
Research released by the [McKell Institute yesterday]( finds that the Commonwealth should step in to create Sovereign Power, a government entity with one simple job – build, own and deliver renewable electricity to power Australian industry, at cost.
Five industry emergency bailouts have occurred over the past two years, including the Tomago aluminium smelter in NSW and the Whyalla steel works in SA.
The common thread running through each is the cost of energy. Australian industry was built on long-term, low cost, public energy and the private electricity market does not have a suitable product for industrial users.
As the assets and contracts from the coal era expire, industrial energy users are discovering today’s market has nothing to replace them.
Private market contracts are too short-term, high-priced, and small for the needs of people making steel, aluminium, copper and critical minerals.
Government has a lower cost of capital and no equity investors demanding a payout. This means financing costs – which typically account for around half of the levelised cost of energy (LCOE) are much lower.
The LCOE spreads capital and operating costs over the lifetime output of an asset using a capital recovery factor based on a discount rate (WACC – weighted average cost of capital), capital expenditure, asset life and capacity factor.
Using government’s lower cost of capital at a real discount rate of three percent is significantly lower than the private sector’s discount rate of around seven percent. This produces a $26/MWh difference in power purchase agreement (PPA) price. The same factors affect firming costs, which our modelling shows Sovereign Power can deliver for $7/MWh less.
An operating margin supporting a cost-recovery model rather than a profit-seeking model delivers around $18/MWh in savings over private market pricing.
The result is a cost-recovery power price of about $66/MWh, compared with a prevailing average of $117/MWh in the private market.
Modelled over an indicative $23 billion investment in renewable generation and storage, the difference is stark.
While the construction, operation and maintenance costs are identical between Sovereign Power and the private market, the lower cost of capital, lower margin and cost recovery period produces a $30 billion difference between the two delivery modes.
Price is not the only factor preventing the private market from effectively serving industrial customers – the volume and horizon of private PPAs usually do not meet the needs of industrial energy users.
Sovereign Power does not compete with private market participants – it fills a gap where the private market fails, rather than reproducing the aims of derisking investment through the current Capacity Investment Scheme (CIS) or its replacement, the Electricity Services Entry Mechanism (ESEM).
Its design and enabling legislation must ensure it serves industrial customers who the private market cannot serve. That means it targets specific strategic customers – not the general market of energy users – who cannot obtain long-term, firmed PPAs at viable prices.
The pricing of Sovereign Power PPAs should be determined by a published methodology overseen by regulators. As is the case with most industrial PPAs, they would not include network costs, which would be paid separately by the customer.
It should be legislated as a statutory entity rather than a government-owned corporation like Snowy Hydro, as the latter is typically required to seek profits and demonstrate equivalent commercial returns to the private sector.
The purpose of Sovereign Power is not to compete with the private sector, but to coordinate the procurement and delivery of firmed renewable energy for strategically important industrial customers the private market has demonstrated it cannot serve.
The benefits of Sovereign Power go well beyond the viability of key industrial capabilities in a time of global uncertainty – though this is undoubtedly important.
As the world converges on electrification, energy systems become powerful economic determinants of business conditions, productivity, inflation, employment and investment.
Australia’s sensitivity to global supply shocks and price spikes in today’s non-electric energy sources has been demonstrated repeatedly since the pandemic.
Petrol and gas fuel prices have risen an average of eight percent a year since 2020, and high gas prices are the key driver of elevated electricity costs across the National Electricity Market.
This sensitivity would be compounded by a reliance on trade for key materials like steel, aluminium, copper and strategic minerals – all inputs for which we have ready raw materials.
Preserving and expanding our industrial capability to manufacture materials like these makes our economy more resilient to global events.
Doing so in a way that uncouples their manufacture from energy inputs like gas, that are themselves volatile and vulnerable to interruption, is the smartest way to harden our domestic capability.
