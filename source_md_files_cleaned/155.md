Despite in-principle support from the Federal Government, Australia’s green iron ambitions have not yet left the ground. A confluence of factors explains why
By [Matt Pollard](
An electric arc furnace at Georgsmarienhütte steel in Germany. - Georgsmarienhütte | Wikimedia
To date, Australia’s green iron ambitions have largely failed to get off the ground. We are sitting on the runway, seatbelts fastened but nowhere near airborne. High construction cost premiums in Australia’s iron ore regions have meant that the levelised costs of energy has stayed high. Coupled with higher than anticipated costs for electrolyser stacks, this has meant the economic viability of energy-intensive production of low-carbon iron in Australia faces significant headwinds.
The Federal Government’s flagship Future Made in Australia package has concentrated public support into supply-side incentives. Whilst this support provides a critical foundation, its time-limited nature fails to address the structural challenges facing green iron proponents in Australia. To kickstart a green iron industry in Australia, proponents must see a runway towards long-term demand growth, underpinned by an effective price on carbon in Australia and in our key trading partner economies in China, Japan, Korea and Southeast Asia.

This report highlights that for every step forward on an individual project or market-level, the broader investment pipeline has just as many case studies of project delay, cancellation, and restructure in the face of unresolved structural headwinds. In Australia, over the past 24 months, we have seen the delay of POSCO’s Port Hedland Iron, the Whyalla Steelworks, Quinbrook’s Gladstone green iron proposal, the Australian Renewable Energy Hub and the cancellation of Fortescue’s Gladstone electrolyser precinct. Overseas, US policy backsliding is also clearly undermining capital momentum.
A confluence of factors has meant the business case globally for low-emissions primary iron and steel produce using clean energy remains insufficient to attract capital.
These include low returns on capital, continued margin compression as a result of global overcapacity, a lack of consumer willingness to pay clean premiums as well as insufficient market intervention to surmount structural challenges in low-emissions energy and enabling infrastructure.
The economic realities of rising infrastructure costs, slower-than-anticipated deflation of electrolyser cost curves outside of China, incoherent and unstable enabling policy architecture, incumbent fossil fuel vested interests’ undermining, volatile and high fossil fuel prices globally and rising geopolitical tensions have necessitated a more considered approach to investment.
Nowhere is this misalignment more visible than in Europe. Despite billions in state aid, carbon pricing, and supply- and demand-side assistance, the European investment pipeline has slowed sharply. ArcelorMittal has cancelled green hydrogen direct reduced iron (DRI) proposals in Germany and France, returning €1.3 billion in German federal subsidies after failing to reach a final investment decision. HYBRIT's demonstration plant in Sweden has returned €113 million to EU funding bodies after permitting delays pushed timelines to 2029 and beyond. Stegra’s flagship Boden green iron and steel project came within weeks of running out of construction cash in early 2026 before securing a €1.4 billion rescue package.
The Middle East and North Africa (MENA) region is emerging as the most credible near-term supplier of lower-emission primary iron to Europe with existing DRI infrastructure, access to competitively priced renewable energy, lower-cost capital and less complex regulatory approval environments that provide a structural cost advantage over Europe. However, the renewed geopolitical instability in the Middle East highlights supply chain risks in the region.
These global headwinds mean that middle-power economies such as Australia and Canada now have the opportunity to capitalise on our strategic value as a geopolitically stable economic partner to emerging demand hubs for green commodities.
China, meanwhile, is not waiting. Although 90 per cent of its steel is produced by the blast furnace-basic oxygen furnace method, China is already the world's largest electric arc furnace operator by installed capacity and is leveraging its value to coordinate energy load with renewable energy supply. China is moving at speed and on a scale orders of magnitude higher than Australia. It is systematically building commercial-scale DRI, expanding its national emissions trading scheme to cover steel in 2026, and manufacturing electrolysers at costs a fraction of Western equivalents. When China determines the economics of green hydrogen-based DRI are viable, it will not need years to scale up the infrastructure.
The global acceleration of green iron and steel investment will depend on the ability of policymakers to synchronise technological, fiscal, and diplomatic levers across markets and borders to shift decarbonisation from a perceived cost into a central pillar of sovereign industrial strategy.
In this context, the opportunity remains for Australia to leverage its advantages and transform its world-leading position in shipping rocks of iron ore into leadership in the burgeoning global green iron and steel marketplace. This requires a significant change in political will, coordination and speed of execution, along with sustained investment in technology innovation. Equally important is a strategic approach to foreign investment policy and investment settings that capitalise on supply chain security risks in the Middle East, to attract bilateral and multilateral support for green iron production onshore.
While progress globally on steel transition is incremental at present, this remains a global race, and we need to be in it to win it.
