Biodiversity in Europe is under threat, with recent trends [showing declines]( in key habitats and species. Human practices in the past decades, including the rise of intensive agriculture, have been identified as significant factors in these declines.
To address this, the [Nature Restoration Regulation]( has been implemented in the EU as part of the EU’s [Biodiversity Strategy]( for 2030. This legally binds Member States to develop restoration plans as part of a collective effort to restore 20% of land and sea area by 2030. To help set restoration targets and design land management strategies that increase and maintain biodiversity, it is important to understand the socioeconomic trends of the past, and how they were related to the state of nature at specific points in time.
Following this idea, researchers set their sights on Medieval civilisation in what is now southwest Germany. The area was the heart of the Carolingian Empire, a precursor to modern France and Germany, and there is considerable primary evidence from the era. In this case, the scientists coupled analysis of data from six pollen cores – cylindrical sediment samples extracted from lakebeds containing fossilised pollen grains – with that from previous meta-analyses of plant materials found at archaeological sites around Lake Constance, a lake that borders modern Germany, Austria and Switzerland. They corroborated these biological data with documentary materials from the Monastery of St Gall, which rose to prominence under Carolingian rulers.
The study paints a picture of human activities and biodiversity in Europe at a time of considerable change. The scientists uncovered what they describe as a “profound change in the floristic landscape” around 500-1000 CE by analysing fossil pollen assemblages, which provide a widely used proxy for past vegetation diversity. They found a marked increase in plant diversity during the early Medieval period. The diversity of pollen types recorded in sediments rose from roughly 27 to about 40 taxa, and Shannon diversity – a standard ecological measure combining richness and relative abundance – increased from 2.15 to 2.65. Because pollen assemblages reflect the composition of surrounding vegetation, these shifts indicate a substantial diversification of plant communities in the region.
Since the study period, they noted that plant diversity estimates through pollen analysis have fluctuated, dipping significantly around 1350 CE, but then increasing again after 1450 and remaining relatively high through the 20th Century. However, this diversity never returned to pre-1350 levels.
The researchers linked the plant diversity trends to agricultural development at the time of using the archaeological plant materials. ‘Immigrant’ crop species arriving via continental trade connections, weeds associated with crop fields, grassland species, and ruderal plants (those inhabiting and colonising disturbed environments) all contributed to the rise in diversity. This was connected to the integrated regional economy centred around the Monastery of St Gall.
The study goes against conventional wisdom linking human population growth with biodiversity decline, showing that just the opposite is possible under the right conditions. The records from the time document a regional population increase, with new agricultural innovations supporting the establishment of new settlements. These included the three-field system, which saw more of the land planted and greater production of staples for human and animal consumption. New tools such as ploughs with iron ploughshares contributed to increased crop yields, while water mills could process crops on a large scale, and financial diversification brought increased income for both the monastery and peasants.
As well as laying down evidence for a concurrent rise in human populations and outputs with plant diversity, the study demonstrates that the pattern reversed when human populations collapsed following the Black Death. When disease killed significant fractions of human communities in the region and agriculture contracted in the period 1350-1400, plant diversity declined. When human populations rebounded in the following centuries, so did plant diversity.
Perhaps most critically, the work links the biodiversity peak at a time of sustained food production to the creation of a patchwork of ecosystems in the region, including farmed fields, pastures and forests. In much more recent times, there has been a shift towards industrial food production in the region, coinciding with a slight decrease in plant diversity in the studied area over the last half-century. Further analyses have found a net loss in plant diversity across Germany in the last 25 years.
The researchers concluded that European environmental policy would benefit from supporting [High Nature Value]( farming, which can be seen on [Common Agricultural Policy aims]( to improve both European farm biodiversity and competitiveness. This research indicates that these objectives are not necessarily at odds. With coordinated interventions, the researchers say, some regions of Europe could even build on the raised plant diversity Medieval civilisation left behind.
