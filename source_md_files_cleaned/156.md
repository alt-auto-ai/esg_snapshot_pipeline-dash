A huge surge in large batteries coming online in the past three months has continued to benefit the National Electricity Market at the expense of fossil fuels
By [Kate Burgess](
Shutterstock.
An explosion in new batteries added to the National Electricity Market in the first three months of 2026 and a larger share of renewables pushed coal and gas generation to fresh lows, 
The addition of 1,504MW / 4,460MWh of new batteries in Q1 brought the number added over the past year to 4,445MW / 11,219MWh. Combined, batteries generated 300 per cent more electricity totalling 872MW, soaking up excess solar and wind generation during the day, and helping to reduce the size of the "duck curve" which occurs when prices turn negative.
Working in concert, batteries and renewables - which surged to an all time high of 46.4 per cent of the energy mix, up from 42.5 per cent a year earlier - are progressively reshaping what AEMO terms the “intraday generation profile”. This is the relative amount that various generation sources contribute to the energy mix at different hours of the day.
“The trend of batteries shifting renewable supply into the evening peak will likely grow with more than 32 gigawatts of new battery capacity looking to connect to the NEM, up 60 per cent year-on-year,” AEMO executive general manager policy & corporate affairs, Violette Mouchaileh said.
Overall, batteries shifted 1,115MW of generation into the evening peak over the quarter, where they became the marginal technology at lower prices than ever before. Like the last three months of 2025, batteries continued their march to displace other capacity like coal, gas peakers and pumped hydro between 6 and 9pm.
The massive upswing in battery charging provided unprecedented arbitrage opportunities for their owners who earned a combined $96.9 million in revenue from arbitrage and FCAS, more than double that of Q1 in 2025. While arbitrage revenue grew 142 per cent to $93.9 million, revenue from providing frequency and ancillary control services (FCAS) plummeted 43 per cent to $3 million.
Battery owners are now making more than 95 per cent of their revenue from arbitrage, whereas even two years ago they made more than 60 per cent from FCAS.
While battery charging revenue was higher on the larger charging volume, arbitrage spreads - the difference between the price that a battery charges at versus the one it discharges at - narrowed during Q1. Partially offsetting this was the ability of some battery owners to charge at negative prices - this rose 146 per cent to $70.8 million.
The meteoric rise of batteries was matched by a precipitous slump in gas-fired generation across the NEM to an average of 712MW, its lowest level since Q4 in 1999. The decline was most marked in Queensland, where gas generation fell 28 per cent year-on-year to 131MW, its lowest quarterly level since Q1 2005.
While AEMO has written in its draft 2026 Integrated System Plan that the grid will need 13-15GW of new gas generation to act as a backstop on days with little wind and solar generation when batteries are charging, its declining use is leaving plant owners to rely on revenue from cap contracts, which are only profitable at times of high price volatility.
Despite the rise of batteries, curtailment of wind and solar plants [continued to be a problem]( nearly doubling to 296MW over Q1 compared to the first three months of 2025. This was mostly attributed to an increase in network constraints, although this includes both unexpected network congestion from too much generation and unplanned network outages, according to an AEMO spokesperson.
The greatest curtailment occurred in southern New South Wales where there are a large number of newly-connected solar farms. Overall solar plants across the NEM were curtailed as much as 7.8 per cent of the time that they were available, up from 4.4 per cent in Q1 2025.
The rising share of wind and solar helped to push emissions created across the NEM to an historic Q1 low 26.0 MtCO2-e although this was only 4.8 per cent lower compared to the same quarter a year earlier. The emissions intensity data only includes utility scale solar, and not rooftop PV so does not provide a full picture of the greening grid.
In an AEMO first, the Quarterly Energy Dynamics shows the progress of data centre connections across the NEM. During Q1, 11 projects with capacity above 5MW progressed in the connection queue. A spokesperson said these were just the projects applying to connect to the transmission grid, and not the distribution network which is where the bulk of smaller data centres connect.
Of the 11, seven projects were in the early stages and four in what the operator terms “proponent implementation” - when developers complete construction of their projects.
