AI expert Luis Gonzalez sees a future where energy equals intelligence, but if Australia is to be an AI hub without coal, our policy settings need to change
By [Charis Palmer](
Stage one of Quinbrook's Supernode battery near Brisbane is operational. The project includes a hyperscale data centre campus - Supplied [Get The Energy in your inbox](
Data centres in Australia could become “digital smelters” concentrated in Sydney and Melbourne pushing wholesale energy prices up by 26 per cent, or, if Australia gets it right, emerge as “managed green hubs” helping to moderate prices and keep Australia on track to net zero.
That’s the view of Professor Luis Gonzalez, chief data and AI officer at Philippine energy company AboitizPower.
Headlining last week’s Energy Consumers Australia conference, Gonzalez had a strong message: a lot of data people have no clue how the energy industry works and a lot of energy people don’t understand what data technology looks like.
“So we have to start talking to each other.”
Gonzalez says nobody truly knows how much energy data centres will be using in 25 years time, but forecasters have missed big structural changes in the past, and data is today’s fundamental structural change.
“AI coming to our economies is not just another technology.
“Data centres are not just going to be storage and availability and maybe processing of data. That's where they are today. That's where they're contracted to be 100% reliable, really low latency etc. They're becoming more and more what we call inference engines, and an inference engine is the ability to turn data into intelligence.”
To put this in perspective, Gonzalez says today only about 3 per cent of the world’s population have interacted with AI and only 0.3 per cent have a paid license.
“ So if you consider this in power terms, let's say that we are in 1940s and only 3 per cent of the population have access to electricity. What was the curve of adoption of electricity that changed the way our economy works? One of the biggest predictors of poverty is lack of electricity. We're in a moment like that again.
“Once this becomes 20 per cent of our population, the entire explosion of intelligence will drive the energy industry.”
Gonzalez argues this is the key to understanding that data centres today are not what data centres will be in the future.
“And forecasting of energy today cannot be done without forecasting data use, compute, inferences.”
This means the current global model of exponential data centre growth has to change, Gonzalez says, towards compute processes being more distributed. Perhaps even down to a community or household level.
In the meantime, he says, “we really do have to create an incentive for data centres to become a driver of green energy, not a bottleneck that would force us to go back to coal”.
Just what role Australia might play in this potential data-led future is debatable.
The energy market operator’s latest Integrated System Plan Step Change scenario sees [data centre energy consumption]( reaching around 6 per cent of the NEM by 2029-30, with a large margin for error based on to what extent AI is taken up.
But the Australian Industry Group is already 
“ There is a lot of importance being placed by all governments, and ministers beyond energy in not unduly constraining an important source of economic growth,” AiGroup climate change and energy director Tennant Reed recently told a member briefing.
“But there is some awareness of the possibility of some significant pressures and that it's worth ensuring this is managed beforehand and not too late.”
In his set of recommendations for Australian policymakers, Gonzalez says the government should align its National AI Plan with AEMO’s ISP, and that regulators should mandate the delivery of data on long-term data centre load and flexibility profiles.
In his “managed green hubs” ideal, pricing signals would steer data centres towards Renewable Energy Zones, rather than just to locations with proximity to fiber.
But Belinda Dennett, CEO of new lobby group Data Centres Australia, says 70 per cent of the energy use of data centres today is done through power purchase agreements “because we think that’s the best way to do it.”
“ Should it be co-located with renewable energy? Now data centre operators don't think they are good candidates to be operating energy plants…that's not what we want to do. We’re building on the urban fringe. That doesn't make a lot of sense,” Dennett told the ECA conference.
And, she says, the 24/7 nature of data centre energy demand means firming is vital.
“ So thinking you can just plug into the solar array in Dubbo and run a data centre without firming, that's not fair.”
Gonzalez recommends policymakers frame data not as a corporate byproduct, but as a strategic asset that requires localised digital infrastructure.
Dennett has been at pains to point out the large gap between the US data centre landscape (5,400 data centres, 54GW) and Australia’s (250 data centres, 1.4GW). “The pipeline is much smaller than what it looks on paper,” she says.
“The hyperscale, large language model data centres that you're probably reading a lot about in the US that Luis is talking about, we don't have those here and because of our copyright laws we're probably not going to have those here.”
The government has ruled out changing Australian copyright laws to enable AI firms to freely train their models using Australian news articles, books and films, but has been 
Training of AI models in Australia may not be common, but the hyperscalers are present in Australia and AI inference group Groq last year expanded its global AI infrastructure footprint to the Asia-Pacific region through its deployment in Equinix’s International Business Exchange data centre in Sydney.
The National AI Plan points to large investments by tech companies Firmus, Amazon and Microsoft and says Australia’s “abundant renewable energy potential, robust privacy protections and strategic Indo-Pacific location can make Australia an AI hub for the region”.
At the Groq and Equinix launch last year, assistant technology minister Andrew Charlton talked up the opportunity for Australia.
“The nations around the world that grab this opportunity, that successfully deploy AI, make AI, use it in their companies, instil those skills in their workers, they are the countries that will rise up the ranks of the world's most prosperous nations,” Charlton said.
“And those countries that do not, those countries that fall behind, that fail to grab this opportunity, they will fall down the list of the world's most prosperous nations.”
Reed says the network augmentation involved is being well covered, but that’s just one component.
“(It’s) well managed from what I can see in terms of guarding against the risk that should individual projects fall over, data centres not eventuate, that other electricity users don't wind up paying for infrastructure they don't need.
“That risk is being well managed. The risk that is still not obviously solved is that the data centres do show up and we don't have enough generation to supply them.”
