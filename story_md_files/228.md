Colombia is home to some of the most diverse plant and animal life on Earth. However, the local people living in these areas often see very little of the money that tourists bring in. In Latin America, it's estimated that local communities receive **less than 5%** of the total income generated by tourism.
In Colombia’s Chocó region, one of the richest ecosystems on Earth, this imbalance is especially visible.
A project called **CHECK -** a regenerative tourism and territorial innovation initiative - has been working since 2021 to change this balance. They want to show that travel can be used to protect nature and help locals earn a steady living.
### Learning while enjoying
Instead of traditional tours where visitors just look at the scenery and leave, CHECK embeds learning in biodiverse territories through locally led checkpoints. 
At these spots, the focus is on:
  * **Hands-on learning:** Travellers and locals learn about the environment together.
  * **Research:** Regular people help gather data on local wildlife (sometimes called "citizen science").
  * **Local leadership:** The projects are run by the people who actually live there, not outside companies.


Since its launch, CHECK has worked with **200 local operators** across **20 destinations** , involving more than **1,500 travellers and learners** in regenerative tourism practices. 
![People from CHECK stand on a boat in the see smiling at the camera](https://environment.ec.europa.eu/sites/default/files/styles/embed_large/public/2026-04/671761.JPG?itok=-vmQQt8q)
In Arusí, on Colombia’s Pacific coast, CHECK’s 'Jardín del Mar' or 'Sea Garden' checkpoint shows how this approach works on the ground. The area is famous for its coral ecosystems, sea turtle nesting sites, and humpback whale habitats, and the initiative brings together environmental education and community-led conservation.
More than **60 children** and **50 local volunteers** are involved in marine education and protection activities, learning about biodiversity, citizen science, and sustainable tourism. The programme has also created new income opportunities linked to responsible travel, helping communities benefit directly from protecting their natural environment. 
The model follows a structured process - from local co-design and implementation to impact measurement and community-led management - ensuring that projects are both effective and locally owned. 
### Building future skills
Beyond conservation and hosting tourists, CHECK focuses on strengthening **local skills** and **livelihoods**. By working with academia, NGOs, and private sector partners, the initiative supports training in sustainable tourism, environmental management, and entrepreneurship. 
This approach increases community participation in ecosystem protection while creating long-term economic opportunities. It also aligns with Colombia’s broader community tourism strategy, contributing to more inclusive and sustainable development in remote regions. 
The initiative demonstrates how tourism, when designed carefully, can provide the funding and the people-power needed to save endangered habitats while making life better for the people who call those places home.
