**With El Niño conditions expected to develop in the coming months, April 2026 saw the second highest sea surface temperatures (SSTs) on record for the extra-polar oceans – between latitudes 60°S and 60°N – as reported in the latest Climate Bulletin by the Copernicus Climate Change Service (C3S). The month saw record high SSTs across large parts of the tropical Pacific associated with strong marine heatwaves.**

> “April 2026 adds to the clear signal of sustained global warmth. Sea surface temperatures were near record levels with widespread marine heatwaves, Arctic sea ice remained well below average, and Europe saw sharp contrasts in temperature and rainfall; all hallmarks of a climate increasingly shaped by extremes.” Samantha Burgess, Strategic Lead for Climate at ECMWF
The average SST for April 2026 over the extra-polar oceans was 21.00°C, the second highest on record for the month, behind only April 2024, which was 21.04°C and coincided with the end of the 2023-2024 El Niño event. SSTs reached record highs for April across a wide stretch of the Pacific Ocean, from the central equatorial region to the west coast of the USA and Mexico. These SSTs corresponded to strong marine heatwave conditions – prolonged periods of unusually warm ocean temperatures that disrupt marine ecosystems and fisheries and can affect coastal economies.1
Throughout the month, the daily average SST over the extra-polar oceans gradually moved toward the record values observed in 2024, reflecting the transition from ENSO-neutral towards El Niño conditions now expected in the coming months.2

##  Third-warmest April globally, regional contrasts in Europe 
With a global average surface air temperature of 14.89°C – 1.43°C warmer than the estimated 1850-1900 pre-industrial baseline – the month was the joint third-warmest April on record, tied with April 2016 and April 2020. This finding follows the fifth-warmest January and February and fourth-warmest March. April’s higher ranking reflects increased SSTs globally, in particular warmer sea surface temperatures in the Pacific. The warmest April occurred in 2024.

Europe experienced its tenth warmest April, although this relatively low ranking masks sharp regional contrasts. While southwest Europe saw much warmer than average conditions and parts of north Spain and south France experienced their warmest Aprils on record, much of east Europe was colder than average.
There were also significant contrasts in temperature anomalies across the Northern Hemisphere in April, although these were less pronounced than earlier this year.

##  Second-lowest Arctic sea ice extent for April 
In April, the average sea ice extent in the Arctic was 4.8% (0.7 million km²) below average, the second lowest on record for the month, behind only April 2019, which was 5.7% below average. Sea ice extent in the region has ranked among the three lowest on record for the corresponding month every month since November 2025.

In the Antarctic, the monthly sea ice extent was 9.7% below average for the month, making it the 11th lowest on record for April. Sea ice cover within the region was much below average in the Bellingshausen Sea, which remained virtually ice–free. By contrast, it was above average in the neighbouring Amundsen Sea.

##  Dry conditions in much of Europe, wet conditions and floods in southwest Asia 
West and central Europe experienced drier than average conditions in April, with particularly pronounced conditions over France, parts of Switzerland and the west Balkans.

![](https://climate.copernicus.eu/sites/default/files/styles/image_dropdown/public/2026-05/C3S_Bulletin_hydro_202604_Fig1_map_anomalies_April_europe_0.png.jpg?itok=bllXGFsw)

In contrast, much of easternmost and southeast Europe, along with Iceland, west Scotland and much of Ireland, parts of south Spain, the Pyrenees, regions of peninsular Italy, the Maghreb coast, and the Caucasus saw above average rainfall and soil moisture. The North Caucasus region was particularly impacted by flooding, as reported by the media.3
Beyond Europe, northeast and central USA, Canada, north Mexico, the Arabian Peninsula and Afghanistan, south China, Japan, parts of Brazil, south Africa, and New Zealand experienced wetter than average conditions. Flash flooding hit much of the Arabian Peninsula, while parts of Iran, Afghanistan, Saudi Arabia and Syria experienced widespread flooding and landslides, claiming lives, per United Nations reports and media accounts.4
