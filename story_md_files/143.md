# Australian startup tackles global grid security issue
A company spun out from Monash University research is solving grid problems before even industry experts know they have a problem
By [Sara Phillips](https://theenergy.co/person/sara-phillips)
When new renewable energy generators connect to the grid, the control systems of the inverters can sometimes interact with the network in ways that create instability. But now a spin-out from Monash University has produced software that can decode the frequencies from inverters and allow better harmonisation.

But inverters come in all shapes and sizes and from manufacturers around the world who consider the contents of their ‘black box’ to be proprietary technology. So Bahrani and colleagues developed technology — Impedance Based Stability Analysis — that, like a bat sonar, effectively pings the inverters and grid and interprets the response to build a profile that helps engineers detect whether the connection is stable or at risk of oscillations.
“You can do stability checks, understand which IBR may be problematic in a larger network, if there is oscillations happening between them, if they're not working properly, if there are adverse interactions and things like that,” says Bahrani.
GridZync was formed from a research question that had the Monash team first approach the Australian Renewable Energy Agency (ARENA) in 2019. The engineers wanted to understand why renewables might become unstable, and whether they could be made more stable. Bahrani says that the work made the team realise that it was possible to develop a tool that could actually solve some of the problems that they identified. So they went back for a second round of ARENA funding to pilot the tool.
“We worked quite significantly with industry, particularly AEMO, to get their feedback and understand how exactly the tools should be developed,” says Bahrani, adding that the Monash Innovation team pitched in to help found a company to commercialise the idea.
“This has been a founder-led effort,” says Andrea Huggins, Vice President of Innovation and Commercialisation at Monash. While Monash has provided support to connect the researchers with industry and investors, she credits Bahrani’s team for taking the initiative and showing “a strong commitment to seeing it through.”
Having demonstrated the viability of the tool, the team then applied for a third round of ARENA funding, which commenced earlier this year, with the plan to have finessed the product to a point where it can be brought to market by the end of 2026.
Already Rezonance is being trialled by industry partners, who are providing feedback to GridZync on what improvements and embellishments would make the tool more useful.
Rob Orange is an electrical engineer at Niko, a New Zealand-based consultancy who is one of the industry partners trialling Rezonance. He says that using the tool for modelling new projects has provided insight into issues they didn’t even realise were going to be issues.
In the past, “we just had to connect to projects, and then you'd see what resonated. Now we can anticipate those issues.” He says that Rezonance's ability to interrogate the black box inverters and its connectivity with coding platform Python has allowed the Niko team to usefully deploy it in their workflow. He says that while it's working well, he can see plenty of scope for the tool to expand, for example Orange would like to see its Python integration extended to more features of the software.
To date, ARENA has provided just shy of $1.5 million to GridZync and its prior research project. ARENA General Manager of Grid and Storage, Carl Christensen says this is funding that would have been difficult to raise from venture capitalists because they tend to look for projects with a large potential market. “While GridZync has potential for a large, positive impact in managing grid stability, its market size is limited,” he says. The ARENA funding aimed to de-risk GridZync in order to develop the technology and attract capital from other investors.
Bahrani says that back when they first applied for ARENA funding, they didn't have the credibility to attract VCs and couldn't show a clear pathway to commercialisation. But he hopes to have convinced some of his trial participants to convert to paid licences by the end of the year, and so be able to show to VCs that the project is commercially viable. More funding would allow more features, such as those that Orange would like to see, and a better user experience. Time is of the essence — Bahrani says “a lot of problems are happening right now in the grid and a tool like this is desperately needed”, not just in Australia, but around the world as IBRs come to dominate. “It's a very, very good time to push and to scale and to come up with more features, a better tool, a robust tool.”
