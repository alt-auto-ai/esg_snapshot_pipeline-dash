#  Australia and UK strengthen partnership on safe and secure AI
The signed memorandum of understanding (MoU) recognises the importance of cooperation between the 2 countries and their AI safety and security institutes.

The new MoU will offer a framework for technical cooperation and promote the safe and responsible use of artificial intelligence (AI). 
The MoU strengthens collaboration between the Australian AI Safety Institute, which is currently being established, and the UK AI Security Institute.
Under the MoU, both parties aim to: 
  * share information and expertise on emerging AI capabilities and risks, and best practices for testing AI systems
  * conduct joint research, including approaches to measure, test and manage risks
  * support the multilateral work of the International Network for Advanced AI Measurement, Evaluation and Science.


The MoU supports the Australian Government’s National AI Plan. The plan aims to keep Australians safe by strengthening international cooperation on AI risks and safety. Australia can respond to the fast pace of AI development by working with our trusted partners.
This partnership will help both countries keep pace with change and ensure communities share the benefits of AI. As AI systems become more powerful and widely used, governments must act quickly to keep Australians safe and maintain public trust.
This agreement highlights the shared commitment of Australia and the UK to ensure AI remains safe, trustworthy and beneficial for everyone.

# Energy ministers cook up smooth transition to new energy underwriting
Victorian Energy Minister Lily D’Ambrosio says the Electricity Services Entry Mechanism will click into gear "when the last CIS auction concludes” so that project developers won’t be left hanging
By [Ben Potter](https://theenergy.co/person/ben-potter)
![](https://theenergy.co/uploads/transforms/Articles/_hero/19525/Goyder.avif)
Project developers are looking to the ESEM to be ready once the Capacity Investment Scheme expires in 2028. Picture: Goyder wind farm in South Australia
State and federal energy and climate ministers are developing a mechanism for a seamless transition to the proposed Electricity Services Entry Mechanism (ESEM) to support long-term investment in generation from the current — and struggling — Capacity Investment Scheme.
Victorian Energy Minister Lily D’Ambrosio said on Tuesday the mechanism would click into gear “when the last CIS auction concludes” so that project developers won’t be left hanging without clarity about funding gaps and financial support.
“We cannot afford to have pauses in the industry's confidence and investment decisions about the pipeline of projects that we need to actually get built,” D’Ambrosio told the Clean Energy Council’s Australian Wind Industry Forum.
“So the clarity and the locking away of the ESEM mechanism is a really important thing to achieve next year.”
The ESEM — a system of long-term contracts underwritten by a federal agency to support investment in new bulk renewable energy, intra-day “shaping” energy such as storage and long term firming energy such as hydro and gas peaking — was devised by an independent panel headed by industry veteran Tim Nelson last year.
![](https://theenergy.co/uploads/transforms/Photos/_hero/19951/1779765702993.avif) “The clarity and the locking away of the ESEM mechanism is a really important thing to achieve next year”: Victorian climate change and energy minister Lily d'Ambrosio (right) in conversation with Clean Energy Council CEO Jacqui Trad.
It will replace the CIS, which has underwritten 18 gigawatts of wind, solar and battery projects towards a 40GW target by 2028, but on such terms that just 6GW — across two wind and two solar projects — has achieved final investment decisions, CEC CEO Jacqui Trad told the forum.
The idea is that the ESEM will overcome this problem by providing targeted support for the latter years of a project in the form of contracts for years five to 15 of a project’s life. The Nelson review found commercial buyers such as large retailers favoured short-term contracts of two to three years over longer contracts, leaving developers facing a “tenor gap” that put off financiers.
D’Ambrosio faced off against Victorian opposition energy spokesman David Davis, who she said presented an “existential threat for the renewable energy industry and investor confidence in Victoria” that risked a repeat of the Coalition’s last term in government from 2010 to 2014 when no new wind or solar projects were approved.
Davis said the Coalition could win the election in November and backed the goal of replacing ageing coal-fired power stations such as Yallourn with renewables and “low emissions technology” and would hold a "short, sharp assessment" of the state's massively over budget transmission projects — Western Renewables Link and VNI West — and move quickly on.
### 'Clobbering communities' counterproductive
Asked by Trad if a Coalition government could cause a sharp slowdown in renewables investment like that in Queensland under the Liberal-Nationals, Davis said he wasn’t going to answer for Queensland but “we will not let the grass grow on this”.
“We want to be very quick, and we want to do this independently, and we want to make that decision and move forward, because we know that low-emission technologies do need to be brought forward, and doing so requires certainty and security.”
Davis was critical of changes to environmental and planning approvals processes that the Allan government has brought in to speed things up and bring them in within 18 months — down from three to five years in some past contentious wind farm cases.
![](https://theenergy.co/uploads/transforms/Photos/_hero/19955/1779765702680_2026-05-26-070223_svbb.avif) Speeding up planning is critical but “not at the expense of local communities”: Victoria coalition spokesman on energy David Davis in conversation with Clean Emergy Council CEO Jacqui Trad at the Australian Wind Industry Forum
He said the Coalition agreed that speeding up planning was critical but “not at the expense of local communities” because “clobbering communities” and stripping their rights “has actually had a counterproductive effect.” A Coalition government would open up exploration of conventional gas resources in Victoria “more broadly” than the government and a proposed 2 kilometre “setback” for renewable energy projects would be ”rebuttable”.
D’Ambrosio agreed gas would have a critical role but it had become both expensive and scarce thanks to export projects and it was just as important to reduce reliance on gas because that saved households and businesses money on their bills.
Victoria’s revived State Electricity on Tuesday launched a platform — [Easy Electricity SEC](https://theenergy.co/article/sec-lends-clout-to-vic-gas-switching-goal) — to help households switch from gas to electric appliances.
D'Ambrosio warned against resorting to “dumb politics” because “the only interest that that serves are people who don't want to save people money on their bills, but want to lock people into very expensive gas bills for decades to come — that doesn't serve Victoria's interests at all”.
In a subsequent panel, Peter Cowling, head of wind at Envision Australia, said Victoria’s transmission projects would unlock “enormous investment” in renewable generation which could “all go to NSW” if transmission was held up any longer.
