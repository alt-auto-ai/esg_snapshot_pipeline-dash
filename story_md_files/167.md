# Developers slow to commit to ratings scheme
Voluntary scheme has lukewarm support as projects ramp up
By [Charis Palmer](https://theenergy.co/person/charis-palmer)
A new rating scheme for renewable energy developers expected to be launched in March remains in "testing" as developers decide whether to sign on.
The Renewable Energy Developer Rating Scheme was one of the primary recommendations of the Andrew Dyer-led independent 
But more than eight months since the start of a pilot by credit reporting agency Equifax and two months since it was formally opened to developers, few have publicly committed to it and the 
A spokesperson from the Department of Climate Change, Energy, the Environment and Water said: “The Developer Rating Scheme opened to industry on 6 March for businesses to commence the assessment process - it is in the final testing stages before formal launch soon.”
In 2023 the Energy Infrastructure Commissioner recommended the scheme be opt-in, but current Commissioner Tony Mahar told  _The Energy_ he thinks it should be mandatory.
“ But let's start with the voluntary scheme and see how it goes,” Mahar said, adding that while it’s “not a silver bullet that’s going to solve everyone’s problems” it would be a valuable tool if the community has confidence in it.
“It’s busier than ever in terms of projects and the scale of them,” Mahar said, with the biggest issue he's seeing at the moment being a lack of coordination or collaboration.
“ Who's making sure that the roads are used in a sequential, responsible way? If there's 10 projects in a region all trying to use the road, all trying to use the tip, all booking out the local accommodation, there needs to be some coordination.
“The community can't cope with that, the councils can't manage it. So it is desperately needed, that coordination mechanism within government and definitely within industry.”
Given the scheme was supported by all governments when conceptualised, Mahar said it was now essential state-owned organisations got on board.
“ State governments need to get their entities like EnergyCo, Powerlink, VicGrid, PoweringWA and TasNetworks. They all need to be signatories and put their money where their mouth is.”
A spokesperson for VicGrid said the group was engaged throughout the design of the scheme.
"The Developer Rating Scheme is intended for project developers and transmission builders, it isn’t something VicGrid would sign up to directly. We encourage Victorian developers to participate in the scheme," the spokesperson said.
Participation in the scheme is understood to cost developers $10,000 a year, and few private developers  _The Energy_ contacted were willing to comment publicly on whether they would formally commit beyond the pilot phase. Changes were made to the scheme following feedback from developers in the pilot.
Erin Coldham, Chief Development Officer at offshore wind developer Southerly Ten said the group welcomed initiatives like the Developer Rating Scheme, adding that Southerly Ten had held 88 community information sessions to date as part of its commitment to community and landholder involvement in projects.
“We look forward to seeing how the Developer Rating Scheme evolves and participating in future stages,” Coldham said.
A spokesperson for TagEnergy said the scheme was "an important step forward for the industry," and that they were supportive of initiatives "that aim to strengthen standards, transparency and accountability across renewable energy development.
"As it has only just been made public, we are still in the early stages of reviewing what is required."
Wagtail Collective director Martine Holberton said in the two years since the scheme was recommended, the industry has lifted its approach to community engagement.
“While poor operators exist in any industry, they are not representative and rarely last. The reality is that many developers are already meeting, and exceeding, the intent of the proposed rating scheme.
"The question now is whether additional layers of assessment will genuinely add value or whether they risk overlooking the progress already being made on the ground," Holberton said.
