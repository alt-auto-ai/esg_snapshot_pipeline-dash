# CSIRO readies grid to value flex
The CSIRO has unveiled a long-awaited plan to model how EVs, solar hot water heaters and industrial heating systems will play a bigger role in the power grid
By [Kate Burgess](https://theenergy.co/person/kate-burgess)
![](https://theenergy.co/uploads/transforms/Articles/_hero/20377/CSIRO-Energy-Centre-at-Newcastle.avif)
CSIRO's Energy Centre at Newcastle, New South Wales where FlexCost research leader Chris Dunstan and his team work.
The CSIRO has kicked off a new approach to forecasting energy costs with a study that takes new distributed energy resources (DER) into account for the first time.
In a [report](https://www.csiro.au/en/research/technology-space/energy/Electricity-transition/FlexCost) detailing its  _FlexCost_ method, the national science agency views electricity markets in an entirely new way - focusing on net supply (expected supply minus expected demand) and wholesale energy costs, and assumes that the balance of supply and demand changes at multiple points during a given day.
The rise of rooftop solar generation has made expected supply and demand more variable, giving forecasters a tougher job of deciding which energy resources to marshall, explains energy strategy leader Dr Chris Dunstan. The new approach was first [publicly flagged](https://theenergy.co/article/federal-energy-department-dives-into-demand-side) at last week’s Energy Efficiency Council conference.
”The type of constraints that we're likely to see in future are different from those that we've seen in the past, with a system based largely on fossil fuel generation, it was very much dependent on when peak demand is. But now that we’re moving to a more renewable-based energy mix with storage, it's going to be a mix of both demand and supply conditions that set those constraints.
”You're looking at the capability of these demand measures to operate in any given moment,” Dunstan added.
DER includes the ability of large power users to conserve demand at times of constrained supply or high cost - smelters and certain other industrial facilities already do this to some extent.
Sometimes neglected among larger, shinier projects, energy efficiency has been described by the International Energy Agency as “the unambiguous first and best response to simultaneously meet affordability, supply security and climate goals”.
It is hoped that by bringing more DER into the energy system, that it will reduce overall demand, and require fewer expensive new large pieces of generation and transmission kit, reducing the cost of the energy transmission while helping to meet federal 62-70 per cent emissions reduction targets.
Home batteries are already helping to flatten out demand in the evening peak when they collectively discharge, aided by the launch of Solar Sharer default offers in July when consumers can shift their usage to peak supply periods during the day.
But the capacity of EVs, solar hot water heaters, industrial heating and cooling systems and eventually larger data centres to discharge their stored electricity into the grid is growing. Customers are opting into arrangements via VPPs or similar plans with their retailers, but not at the levels that retailers have expected them to.
![](https://theenergy.co/uploads/transforms/Photos/_hero/20382/2NEM_total_demand_heatmap_smoothed_avg_2020_2025_2026-06-03-070332_xbwt.avif) A heatmap showing total demand in the National Electricity Market from 2020 to 2025.= - CSIRO
The four types of supply constraints are shown in the above diagram, with Acute Constraints coloured yellow, Peak Constraints coloured light purple and low constraints (Bulk Energy) appearing in dark purple. Yellow periods are typically in evening peaks in the cooler months of the year.
### Levelised costs
The goal of FlexCost - like the CSIRO’s GenCost - is to determine a metric for how much DER is available, in MWh terms. The market will be sorted into four periods by the amount of generation constraints, where the supply gap is greatest and prices are highest, ranging from Acute Constraint to Bulk Energy (very few constraints).
DER assets ranging from heating appliances to installations that reduce electricity use like insulation, will all be assigned a cost, based on the asset’s cost and the network costs. Some economic and “free-rider costs” when other consumers benefit from demand resources that are owned by a single person, are excluded.
When run the model will produce a Levelised Cost of Demand-side Technology (LCODT), similar to the popular Levelised Cost of Energy (LCOE), expressed in MWh terms.
Customers typically need to be offered incentives to offer their appliances to the grid for collective use. FlexCost has termed this a “facilitation cost” and keeps it separate to the basic cost unit.
Dunstan’s team will undertake FlexCost for the next several months and will feed the results of their analysis into AEMO’s work with the Department of Climate Change, Energy, the Environment and Water’s landmark new study on DER costs, the Demand Side Statement of Opportunities.
Final reports are expected before the end of 2026, spokespeople from the respective agencies said.
