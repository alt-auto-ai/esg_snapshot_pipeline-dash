# Meeting the moment: a budget towards energy sovereignty
As much as the fuel crisis in Iran is dominating the economic outlook, urgent action to address it must not compromise our long-term energy strategy
By [Dani Alexander](https://theenergy.co/person/dani-alexander)
![The Strait of Hormuz in 2021, as captured by NASA satellite.](https://theenergy.co/uploads/transforms/Articles/_hero/19414/iss064e046338large.avif)
The tensions in the Strait of Hormuz have shown up Australia's energy vulnerabilities. - NASA, JPL
The fuel crisis in the wake of the Iran War has uncovered our energy sector’s vulnerability. The harsh reality is that we have never met the International Energy Agency’s recommended 90 days of stockpiled fuel.
The risk is felt keenly since more than 40 per cent of our economy’s energy consumption is still fuelled by oil. This oil is used for personal road transport, which has been at the epicentre of the public debate, but it also is a significant source of energy for our agricultural and mining sectors that currently underpin our economic prosperity.
But in a moment of crisis, urgent action must not compromise our long-term strategy.
For tonight's budget, we need to see action towards low-regret short-term options and ambitious investments in future technologies that we know we need. Both will alleviate the risk to supply in a way that supports our economic and environmental objectives.
The easiest and most fruitful action is supporting a national approach to electrification. We need to send a strong signal to Australians, across all levels of government, that electrifying as much as possible is a route to energy that is more affordable, more reliable and secure, and lower emissions — because it is. To ensure that all signs point to electrification, we need to maintain momentum on successful programs and implement new policies that expand the opportunity to electrify to a wider group of Australians.
To electrify more transport, we need to invest strategically in public charging infrastructure and introduce policy measures that promote solar soaking by EVs. For example, ANU researcher Bjorn Sturmberg recently released an [analysis](https://theenergy.co/article/record-curtailment-threat-or-opportunity), being increasingly curtailed.
Beyond transport, policy should focus on electrifying the commercial and industrial sector to complement the Cheaper Home Battery Scheme’s success for households. We must keep in mind that industry’s energy usage is more than four times as much as the residential sector. International examples such as New Zealand’s Government Investment in Decarbonising Industry Fund, which invested just over NZ$100 million to avoid 437,900 tonnes of CO2 annually, have shown that targeted efforts can deliver significant impact quickly.
Continued support for electrification can also underpin new sources of export income including green materials, metals and commodities.
To support energy users that cannot electrify, we need to develop new technologies that use renewable energy to produce liquid fuels, molecules and gas. For this reason, it was disappointing to see the Australian Government discontinue funding for Australia’s Economic Accelerator (AEA) that has, in less than three years, supported 400 research commercialisation projects.
We need significant and extensive support for deep-tech ventures to traverse the so-called valley of death between idea and commercial product. Without this, exciting technologies like those being pursued by Hysata, PlasmaLeap and HydGene Renewables will face an uphill battle.
[energy R&D in Australia, as a proportion of GDP, has halved over the last decade](https://theenergy.co/article/energy-ambition-stuck-in-yesterdays-r-d-settings).
In addition to supporting new technology development, we can also do more to make sovereign renewable energy projects bankable. Professor Elizabeth Thurbon and former head of the Clean Energy Finance Corporation, Oliver Yates, have proposed a Clean Commodities Trading Initiative to provide revenue certainty for projects and address the “green premium” in fiscally responsible ways. As an example, they estimate that the sustainable aviation fuel (SAF) challenge could be addressed for less than $2 per airline passenger.
I fully appreciate that this will be a challenging budget and fiscal responsibility is top of the agenda in globally uncertain times. But we must continue to invest in the long-term strength of our economy and environment if we are to emerge stronger out of this crisis.
And if we need a saving to make that investment perhaps we should look at the more than $16 billion that has been directed towards fossil fuel subsidies. We could redirect that funding to electrify as much as possible now, including those sectors receiving that subsidy, and invest in the sovereign renewable energy technologies we know we need.
