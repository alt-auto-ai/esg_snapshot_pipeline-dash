# Tips for New South Wales on cutting approval times
The Clean Energy Investment Group has called for the state’s new major projects body to fast-track clean tech projects
By [Kate Burgess](https://theenergy.co/person/kate-burgess)
![](https://theenergy.co/uploads/transforms/Articles/_hero/20327/20260203149890750966-minihighres.avif)
NSW Planning Minister Paul Scully debates bills in the Upper House. - AAP/Bianca de Marchi
New South Wales’ new Investment Delivery Authority (IDA) should be given enough resources to quickly identify and sequence the state’s most critical renewable energy projects to speed up project delivery approvals, according to the Clean Energy Investor Group.
In advice for state planning Minister Paul Scully and Climate Change and Energy Minister Penny Sharpe prepared with an HSF Kramer survey, the industry body wants planned new Priority Energy Projects to include solar farms, batteries and pumped hydro for the first time.
NSW has had its State Significant Development (SSD) and related infrastructure (CSSI) planning pathways in place for years, but to date only the SSD pathway has applied to renewable energy projects, and not the infrastructure category, which is seen as faster-moving.
The CEIG argues that by adjusting the thresholds for inclusion under the infrastructure category, more critical priority projects can be fast-tracked by the IDA, the state’s new mega project overseeing body.
> #### Expert view
> ”The one thing holding the New South Wales budget out of recession is investment in clean energy. Of the 16 projects that IDA selected to push ahead with, 13 were renewables-based,”

However, the bar for these is high, with a project value for more than $1 billion, and despite recent planning reforms passing through the NSW Upper House and the federal Environment Protection and Biodiversity Conservation Act (EPBC), approval times for all types of renewable energy remain stubbornly longer than in any other state.
The CEIG’s latest figures show that while approval timeframes for all three technologies is shrinking, it takes an average of 1,205 days to approve a wind farm, with solar energy at 1,116 days and standalone batteries at 665 days. These are the longest for any Australian region, with Victorian onshore wind projects taking 1096 days to approve, and no other state or territory taking longer than 400 days to approve a solar farm.
As of last month, 48 wind energy projects are seeking approval in the state, with 32 at the environmental impact stage, and eight awaiting their final assessment. Three have been referred to the IPC, due to a conflict between project stakeholders.
Thirty-eight solar projects are wriggling their way through the system, of which 21 are at EIS stage and two are stuck in the IPC. Meanwhile, of the 68 batteries, 48 are at EIS stage and nine awaiting final assessment, with no IPC listings.
### Slow progress
The [prioritising renewable energy bill](https://www.parliament.nsw.gov.au/bill/files/18899/Second%20Print.pdf) is currently before the NSW Parliament and seeks to elevate renewable energy to a critical planning status alongside housing, energy transmission and other resources projects. Other changes involve giving Planning Minister Scully more discretion to make decisions on contested projects that reach the IPC stage.
These include an option for the planning minister to hold a public hearing for projects that have lost an appeal and providing the planning minister the power to determine the amount of land to be dedicated free of cost.
HSF Kramer Partner Peter Briggs said that while these were not major changes, it was important to balance the level of community input needed to ensure social licence with the need to expedite important projects through the system.
> #### Expert view
> “ There's a number of problems with the state significant development (SSD) pathway, but the infrastructure (CSSI) pathway is much more suited to energy projects. A 2024 set of guidelines called for large wind farms with 700MW capacity to be considered, but it never eventuated.
> “Now with the new Priority Energy Project status, this is back on the table, with discretion split between the Energy Minister and the Planning Minister.”

Major state transmission projects have been routinely -declared under existing laws, such as TransGrid’s HumeLink connection to Snowy Hydro and Project EnergyConnect.
Under the new rules, gas-firming projects would be eligible for CSSI status, but coal and nuclear energy projects have been specifically excluded.
The Prioritising Renewable Energy bill has been read twice in the NSW Upper House, but Labor needs to seek support from other parties as it has a six seat minority, and Minister Sharpe has been suspended from the chamber on four occasions.
A number of other members have also been suspended for at least two months, which has meant that no bills can be voted on. With 28 bills awaiting a vote, Senators are seeking urgent solutions to resolve the impasse before Treasurer Daniel Mookhey hands down the state budget on 23 June, according to a spokesperson for Minister Sharpe.
