# Offshore wind industry puts VicGrid on notice
A coalition of local Gippsland council groups has released a strategy to coordinate Victoria's emerging offshore wind industry
By [Kate Burgess](https://theenergy.co/person/kate-burgess)
Victorian transmission planner VicGrid should expedite its plan to deliver the first 2GW of offshore wind capacity expected to come online in Gippsland by the end of 2029, according to a first of its kind sector plan.
The Gippsland Offshore Wind Alliance 
VicGrid released an offshore wind transmission strategy in August 2025 and in October shortlisted bidders to deliver the Gippsland Shoreline Renewable Energy Zone, which will connect the first 2GW of capacity.
Southerly Ten’s 2.2GW Star of the South is the most progressed of the nine projects that have been awarded feasibility licences, having lodged its Environmental Impact Statement to the federal government last December.
Iberdrola is currently preparing their Environmental Effects Statement (EES) for Victoria in parallel with their EIS for their 3GW Aurora Green project.

Orsted, JERA and Nex bp’s Blue Mackerel’s EIS guidelines, the precursor to the assessment process, are open for community consultation until May 11, according to the EPBC public portal.
The nine projects, with a total capacity of 18.5GW, were awarded offshore feasibility licences in mid-2024, with Australia’s Offshore Infrastructure Regulator (OIR) approving the projects’ feasibility stage management plans at the beginning of 2025.

The OIR should have a larger involvement in the planning approval process at a federal level, including responsibility to undertake federal environmental assessments, the report stated.
Other recommendations include the development of a joint federal-state O&M hub, and for TAFE Victoria to deliver a regional training strategy and financial support package to re-train and support workers leaving other industries, including coal mining and power generation.
The state’s energy department has said it is working with the federal government to have offshore wind farms included in the new Electricity Service Entry Mechanism (ESEM) contracts that are currently under development.
Victoria is leaning on the fledgling offshore wind sector to help it meet its Net Zero by 2045 target, which involves generating 95 per cent of its electricity from renewable sources by 2035 and includes 4GW of offshore wind by 2035 and 9GW by 2040.
The report states that the offshore wind projects may potentially be developed within 14 years of being granted their feasibility licences, but that depends to a large extent on whether VicGrid can roll out the new transmission lines according to this timeline.
The Port of Hastings, located to the east of Melbourne is being upgraded to allow for the delivery of wind towers and turbine equipment in time, and in late 2025 began preparing an EES for the state government and an EIS for the federal government.
