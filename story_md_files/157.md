# How big can batteries get? Why safety, not chemistry, sets the limit
As the size of batteries becomes grid-scale, the lack of mandatory, nationally harmonised safety standards needs to be addressed
By [Yasir Arafat](https://theenergy.co/person/yasir-arafat) and [Asma Aziz](https://theenergy.co/person/asma-aziz)

Which raises a natural question: how big can batteries actually get? Is there a fundamental chemical or technical limit?
The surprising answer is that chemistry is not the limiting factor. Safety risk management is.
### **There is no chemical ceiling**
From a chemistry standpoint, there is no inherent upper size limit to lithium-ion battery systems. Voltage and capacity are fundamentally constrained at the individual cell level by electrochemical and material limits. Each cell has a defined voltage window, energy density, and current capability dictated by its chemistry.
But grid-scale batteries are not giant electrochemical devices. They are vast assemblies of identical small cells.
A single lithium-ion cell behaves the same whether it sits inside a laptop, an electric vehicle, or inside a container at a grid-scale storage site. Its internal electrochemical reactions do not change because the overall system is larger.
Scaling happens through repetition. For example, Lithium iron phosphate (LiFePO4 or LFP) cells are assembled into modules, modules into racks, racks into containers, and containers into power stations. A 1GWh battery is simply millions of small electrochemical cells connected in series and parallel to deliver the required voltage and capacity.
There is no chemical rule that says lithium-ion technology stops functioning at 500MWh or 1GWh. The electrochemistry does not “break” at scale.
This is where safety — not chemistry — becomes the real constraint on size.
**The risks is thermal runaway**
Thermal runaway remains the defining safety risk in lithium-ion systems. Even under normal operation, batteries generate heat during charging and discharging. As systems age, internal resistance increases, leading to greater heat production. If that heat is not properly managed, a cell can exceed its critical temperature threshold.
Thermal runaway may be triggered by internal short circuits, overcharging, mechanical damage, cooling system failure or malfunction of control electronics such as the battery management system.
Once initiated, the reaction becomes self-sustaining. The heat generated inside the cell accelerates decomposition of the electrolyte and electrode materials. Flammable gases are released, internal pressure rises rapidly and, if ignition occurs, temperatures can spike within seconds.
Extinguishing visible flames does not necessarily stop the reaction inside the cell. Thermal runaway is driven by stored chemical energy, and even after surface flames are suppressed, internal reactions can continue. In some cases, cells may re-ignite hours later. This makes suppression complex and often prolonged.
**What happens as batteries get bigger?**
When you move from thousands of cells to millions, even a tiny failure rate starts to matter. Scale also makes thermal management more demanding. In large containerised systems, keeping temperature uniform is critical. A difference of just a few degrees can accelerate degradation unevenly and increase the risk of failure.

**Thermal runaway mitigation strategies**
Modern battery energy storage systems rely on layered risk management. Protection is built into design, monitoring, containing, cooling and physical configuration. Thermal management is the first line of defence.
Utility-scale batteries use liquid, controlled air cooling or a hybrid to keep cells within safe temperature limits and prevent conditions that could trigger thermal runaway. The goal is simple: prevent cells from ever approaching thermal runaway conditions.
Battery management systems continuously monitor voltage, temperature and imbalance. If abnormal behaviour is detected, affected strings can be isolated, modules shut down, or the system disconnected from the grid. Early gas detection provides another warning layer, allowing automated shutdown before ignition.
Because eliminating every defect is impossible, containment is essential. Large systems are segmented into cells, modules, racks and containers, each layer providing another barrier to prevent failure from spreading. In practice, sacrificing a single container may be acceptable if it protects the entire site.
Finally, if mitigation and containment fail to stop fire propagation, suppression systems such as water mist or aerosol agents come into play to control flames and reduce escalation.
Notably, suppression does not necessarily halt thermal runaway inside a failing cell. Its primary role is to limit fire spread and protect adjacent equipment.

But here is the catch. Transmission standards regulate electrical performance, not internal chemical safety.
They do not address what happens inside a container if a lithium-ion cell fails. Thermal runaway, electrolyte combustion, gas release and cell to cell propagation are different from the risks transmission rules were designed to manage. A transformer fire and a battery thermal runaway event are fundamentally different phenomena.
This leads to a broader issue. Australia does not currently have a mandatory, nationally harmonised safety standard specifically tailored to utility scale battery systems. Smaller batteries are covered by established standards, but large projects are typically assessed using a mix of international guidelines (for example IEC 62619), project specific fire engineering studies and negotiations with local authorities.
