# Developers view government offtakes with rose coloured glasses
Project developers say the void that erstwhile long-term state government power purchase agreements left has not fully been filled by the Capacity Investment Scheme
By [Kate Burgess](https://theenergy.co/person/kate-burgess)
![](https://theenergy.co/uploads/transforms/Articles/_hero/15950/1000032288.avif)
Potentia Energy CEO Werther Esposito addresses the Clean Energy Investor Group conference [Get The Energy in your inbox](https://theenergy.co/subscribe)
Renewable developers are nostalgic for the 15-year power purchase agreements (PPAs) that state governments used to bring forward wind and solar projects a decade ago.
Potentia Energy CEO Werther Esposito acknowledges that the Albanese government’s Capacity Investment Scheme was a “great initiative to support renewables”, but told the Clean Energy Investor Group conference in Melbourne that “it isn’t enough support”.
“It doesn’t fully replace PPAs that state governments used to write. This is one of the key upcoming challenges of the transition.”
Potentia is a JV between Italian utility Enel and Japanese oil and gas explorer INPEX. It owns foundational solar projects including the Clare, Royalla and Bungala solar farms, and is developing the Quorn Park solar hybrid project.
The CEO of New South Wales government’s storage financing agency Energy Security Corporation, Paul Peters, told the conference that the Electricity Services Energy Mechanism (ESEM) contracts that were a key outcome of last year’s Nelson review into the National Electricity Market only partially bridges the gap left by state-backed PPAs which were awarded by reverse auction tenders.
“The underwriting schemes were never designed to fully underwrite projects. They partially underwrite projects,” Peters said.
“For any lender, there's a whole bunch of things that are needed to to get bank finance into a transaction and the equity into a transaction. Offtake is one of those. The government took a view that there is a gap in the offtake market and that it can be filled with a partial underwriting solution.”
The ESEM would be structured as a contract for difference to support revenue in projects from years 8 to 15 of operation, a period known as the “tenor gap” where they are unable to strike offtake agreements with electricity retailers or large users.
Peters said it was difficult for project developers to sign off ake contracts for two-thirds or more of their output at the time of financial close, “because you’re still three years out from being operational, and the retailers or whomever you’re off aking from don’t have a good picture of what the market will look like.”
The Energy Security Corporation is looking to bridge funding gaps for storage projects where commercial finance is unwilling to lend, either due to individual project economics, the cost of senior debt or the absence of an off taker.
“We know the economics of getting that hundred per cent offtake three years out from operation is too hard right now.
“So we've flexed that offtake challenge and some of the other project challenges and said, let's offer a thin layer of mezzanine capital - it might be 10-20 per cent of the, the value of the project to fill the gap that we may not fill through the offtake market today.”
The ESC's strategy is to offer mezzanine finance earlier in a project lifecycle, before final investment decision, to help make projects more bankable to commercial lenders who would provide senior debt.
FTI Consulting managing director Claire Stewart said one way to break the PPA drought would be for data centre operators to sign PPAs with renewable energy projects.
The Department of Climate Change, Energy, the Environment and Water is pressing ahead with its ESEM contract design and later this week through ASL will issue a call out for industry participants to join a reformed contract co-design working group.
