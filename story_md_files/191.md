# The data centre energy paradox
Here's why energy experts are 'alert but not alarmed' over coming energy demand from data centres
By [Ben Potter](https://theenergy.co/person/ben-potter)
![](https://theenergy.co/uploads/transforms/Articles/_hero/18628/20260226170936303100-original.avif)
NEXTDC has been expanding its portfolio of data centres in Sydney - Steven Markham/AAP
Data centres are the paradox of the energy system.
Scarcely a week goes by without a fresh and increasingly dire warning about the huge strain these Tardis-like facilities are going to place on the grid when the massive pipeline of proposals gets built and seeks a connection.
Yet there is no sign of a looming demand surge in either current wholesale prices in the National Electricity Market, the default market offers for the coming financial year which are down in all states bar South Australia, or in the ASX 24 electricity futures prices.
March quarter NEM prices were at their lowest since 2022, June quarter prices continue to track below prices for the corresponding quarters of the past few years, and ASX 24 NEM futures for the coming quarters and years are on their best behaviour too.
Futures may not tell the whole story of course, but the project pipeline may not tell the whole story either.
### **Market operator 'alert, not alarmed’**
It’s a case of being “alert but not alarmed”, says Daniel Westerman, CEO of the Australian Energy Market Operator.
An AEMO slide pack for the agency’s forecasting reference group is the latest update to be seized upon as evidence that a wave of data centres is going to overwhelm — or “break” — an east coast grid that is slowly gearing up for the deluge.
![](https://theenergy.co/uploads/transforms/Articles/_hero/20153/State_DC_Demand.avif) ![](https://theenergy.co/uploads/transforms/Articles/_hero/20154/DCForecast.avif) AEMO Forecasting Reference Group meeting May 2026
The top chart shows average monthly historical data centre demand in the NEM doubling over the past four years from around 300 megawatts to about 600MW.
The bottom chart shows consumption forecast to almost triple in the central Step Change scenario from about 5 terawatt hours to just under 15TWh by 2030, and increase fivefold to 25TWh by 2035 (and 35TWh in the Accelerated Transition scenario).
​These projections look dramatic at first blush, but there are a few factors that might make them seem less scary.
​AEMO derives its forecast from two approaches — project-based, which looks at a pipeline of project proposals; and economics-based, which looks at underlying economic demand for data centre services.
​The former comes up with an ambitious 40TWh, and the latter with a more modest number — barely more than 10TWh by 2035. AEMO weights them equally to come up with its final forecasts of 25TWh (central) and 35TWh (accelerated).
### **Price response?**
These are scary enough for grid operators. If they are real, you might expect a visible price response, especially as data centre developers bang on so much about the urgent national imperative of getting them connected ASAP.
But so far there is nothing to see in published short or medium term NEM prices. (We focus on the NEM — specifically NSW and Victoria — here because the lion's share of data centres target those states.)
​Near and medium term prices are being inexorably pushed down by the wave of large and small batteries entering the market, but they're probably too soon to be relevant.
![](https://theenergy.co/uploads/transforms/Articles/_hero/20157/Settlement1.avif) Baseload futures, May 2026 settled prices for delivery in 2028-2029, NSW - ASX Energy ![](https://theenergy.co/uploads/transforms/Articles/_hero/20158/Setttlement2.avif) Baseload futures, May 2026 settled prices for delivery in 2028-2029, Victoria - ASX Energy
The farthest dated visible electricity contracts in the NEM are the fiscal 2029 contracts.
In the case of NSW, prices are barely higher than current prices, despite the scheduled closure of the giant Eraring power station falling just months prior to delivery. In Victoria, 2029 prices are higher, but from a low base, and the closure of the Yallourn power station falls the year before delivery.
ASX Energy futures are an imperfect predictor — liquidity is poor for longdated contracts, and the market may be in a state of flux pending finalisation of the new long term contracts system (the Electricity Services Entry Mechanism) in 2028, says UNSW senior research associate Dylan McConnell.
Developers also have the option of buying over-the-counter (OTC) futures contracts, or Power Purchase Agreements (PPAs).
But the details of OTC contracts are not made public, and the [PPA Tracker](https://www.erm.com/ppa-tracker-australia/) published by consultancy ERM is still dominated by Rio Tinto and Transport for NSW, with cameos from data centre operators Amazon and Equinix.
![](https://theenergy.co/uploads/transforms/Articles/_hero/20161/ERM_PPA.avif) ERM PPA Tracker
## **Rubbery figures**
Some developers may be biding their time while policymakers work out what the federal government's "expectations" of the renewable and firming energy new data centres must bring to the table will mean in practice.
Local data centre operators and global "hyperscalers " are known to be actively seeking large supply contracts to meet their 2030 commitments and beyond, in spite of the policy uncertainty.
But they've become wary of announcing PPAs until the underlying clean energy projects are well advanced because of the propensity for some renewable project developments to fall over for financial reasons or the difficulty of securing a grid connection.
Data centre projects are rubbery too. There are lots of aspiring developers hoping to cash in on the data centres boom. Quite a few of them drop out.
The next chart shows that even committed projects are withdrawn or regress at a rate of nearly 50% from one year to the next. From these drop out rates, AEMO derives completion probabilities of 0-20 per cent of proposed projects, 21-50 per cent of anticipated projects and 51-100 per cent of committed projects.
![](https://theenergy.co/uploads/transforms/Articles/_hero/20162/AEMO_DC_commitments.avif) Nearly 50% of data centre projects are either withdrawn or "regress" from year to year - AEMO Forecasting Reference Group meeting May 2026
Westerman told the [Energy Users Association of Australia national conference](https://theenergy.co/article/key-transmission-line-all-but-finished-aemo-cautiously-positive) last week that developers take more options on a grid connection than they will ever build to increase their chances of getting connected.
AEMO estimates data centre utilisation at between 27% and 37% based on current average demand of just under 600MW and estimated capacity of 1.4GW to 1.7GW (Data Centres Australia, DC Byte) to 2.2GW (network service providers).
Still, the agency is mulling bumping its forecasts up a little more, Westerman said. It has 4.1GW of data centre connection applications in its current pipeline, 1.3GW in the "implementation" stage and none in commissioning.
**Tennant Reed** , Director of Climate Change and Energy at the Australian Industry Group, says there is duplication on the demand side too as online search and business process firms defend their turf against AI challengers.
These uncertainties leave grid operators and policymakers scrambling to determine real data centre demand.
### **Empty promises**
US energy guru **Jigar Shah** , who ran US government loan programs under the Obama and Biden administrations, told the [Prof G Markets podcast on 29 April](https://www.erm.com/ppa-tracker-australia/) that supply constraints on high performance graphics processing units (GPUs), plain vanilla central processing units (CPUs) and memory meant developers could not by 2030 build more than 50GW of the 500GW of data centre connection requests "creating havoc" across America.
"They are making empty promises to people across the country to disrupt their communities when they don't have the GPUs to fill it," Shah said.
Reed says AEMO and VicGrid, the state-owned grid planner for Victoria, may not be tracking GPUs and CPUs but VicGrid's estimate of serious data centre connection inquiries of 2.5GW is a similar fraction of the 23GWs of inquiries received to Shah's.
Even the most excited data centre enthusiasts concede there's some bubbliness in the market, Reed says. Developers can increase their chances of timely connection by acknowledging the potential of some data centres duties to be flexible in their demand too.
"The outlook for data centre electricity demand and its impact on the electricity market is really uncertain", Reed says.
So far the increase in data centre demand is just replacing the shortfalls in demand from earlier hydrogen production and electrification forecasts, says McConnell.
![](https://theenergy.co/uploads/transforms/Articles/_hero/20163/Deamnd_changes_McConnell.avif) Increased "business" demand forecast for 2030 — which includes data centres — is largely offset by reductions in forecasts for hydrogen production and electrification - Dylan McConnell, UNSW
Even so, policymakers can't count on that remaining the case in the coming years. Forecast data centre demand of 25TWh to 35TWh in 2035 is substantial compared to current NEM demand of around 200TWh, Reed points out.
If the forecasts are realised, and the industry doesn't build enough renewable energy and storage to both meet the demand and replace the output of retiring coal power stations, the outcomes for electricity prices, reliability and emissions won't be pretty.
That's a risk the nation can't afford to take, and recent progress in the energy transition is not encouraging. The Clean Energy Council reported last week that just [2.3GW of projects reached financial close in calendar 2025](https://theenergy.co/article/lack-of-investor-certainty-drags-renewable-rollout-cec), down from 4.4GW in 2024.
### **All stops**
Fortunately, state governments in NSW and Victoria are pulling out the stops to accelerate projects and seeing results.
Some very large projects are being expedited through planning — such as the 1500 MW Warracknabeal Energy Park wind and battery farm and Meadow Creek 332MW solar and 250MW, four hour battery project in Victoria, and the 1500MW wind, 1500 Yanco Delta wind farm, with an 800MW battery, and 943MW Valley of the Winds wind farm, with a 320MW, two hour battery.
Such projects still need final investment decisions, but the increased determination is palpable (as is the lack of progress in Queensland, where the Crisafulli government has shoved the transition into reverse, and the continued hostility towards transmission, especially in Victoria).
The sheer momentum in batteries — both existing lithium ion and emerging chemistries such as sodium ion — is another cause for optimism, because batteries enhance the viability of both wind and solar farms, says Reed. New wind tower technologies such as those pursued by Fortescue Metals Group in the Pilbara also offer the prospect of reduced costs.
Reed thinks these developments are bringing the government's controversial 82 per cent renewables target by 2030 back into view, if not the plausible range.
"The rate of build of big batteries and additions of small batteries is really fast, and it does suggest that we are going to more rapidly lift some of the key constraints to hitting the 80s in renewable penetration," Reed says.
"[Climate and energy] Minister [Chris] Bowen might wind up in a few years saying, 'oh, well, 82 that was a motivational number, and the 70 something that we've done was magnificent', and 'no, no harm, no foul'."
McConnell wants to see more project FIDs but agrees getting into the ballpark of 82 per cent would be a win.
"You need to have a target to have accountability and something to work towards, but if we end up getting to, you know, 75 per cent renewable energy by 2030 — that's pretty bloody good."
