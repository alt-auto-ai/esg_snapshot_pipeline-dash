**On 6 June 1944, the world bore witness to the largest amphibious invasion in history, D-Day. Also known as the Normandy landings, the operation saw the air, sea and land forces of the allied armies descend upon northwest France, laying the foundations for the liberation of Western Europe. However, stormy conditions in the run up to that fateful day put the whole mission at risk.**[**Weather Replay**](https://weather-replay.climate.copernicus.eu/)**, the latest app released by the Copernicus Climate Change Service (C3S), allows us to recreate the weather on this poignant day. Doing so reminds us of the importance of forecasting for safety, health, military success and global security, historically and in the present day.**

Saturday 3 June 1944 was a Saturday unlike any other for meteorologists. On that day, meteorological advisor Group Captain J.M. Stagg presented his overall assessment of weather forecasts from three teams of scientists1 to the Supreme Commander of the Allied Forces in Europe, General Dwight Eisenhower, which led to the postponement of the landings and aerial assault, key parts of Operation Overlord. That forecast, confirmed in the early hours of 4 June, predicted that stormy conditions in the English Channel would have put the entire operation under threat on 5 June, the day the mission was initially planned for. C3S’ modern tools enable us to see that their information was accurate, with a threatening storm forming west of Britain.
As is often the case with military operations, favourable weather was not just important for D-Day's success – it was a fundamental prerequisite. The allies required little cloud cover, light wind, low seas and a low tide. However, the forecast teams predicted the next occasion when tides were favourable was almost two weeks later. Postponing until then would have likely had hugely damaging consequences for the war effort.
With the fate of around 160,000 allied troops on their shoulders, the teams of scientists regrouped later on 4 June to make another forecast. Within that prediction, they identified an alternative window of quieter weather on 6 June that would allow the operation to proceed, which led to military orders for it to go ahead on the following day – 24 hours later than originally planned – being issued, and resulted in a key military success for the allies.
Released late last month in the US, the film ‘Pressure,’ directed by Anthony Maras, shines a light on the courageous scientists tasked with forecasting conditions for the Normandy landings in the critical 72 hours leading up to the operation. The film has raised awareness of operational weather forecasting all over the world, with expert scientists at the American Meteorological Society recently hosting a [webinar to discuss the science behind it](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=fV5GEwbyjN4) with the Director.
In 2014, the European Centre for Medium-Range Weather Forecasts (ECMWF) scientist Adrian Simmons wrote an [in-depth article for the ECMWF website analysing D-Day weather](https://www.ecmwf.int/en/about/media-centre/news/2014/ecmwf-revisits-meteorology-d-day-period) based on the European Reanalysis of Global Climate Observations (ERA-CLIM) collaborative research project. Many of its findings are still valid, despite subsequent improvements in reanalysis and collections of additional historic observations.
##  Recreating historical days 
The Weather Replay app enables anyone to easily access and use Copernicus’ newer ERA5 data to reconstruct the weather experienced by those in the air, at sea and on the beaches during D-Day, see the conditions they faced, and better understand the importance of forecasting for the outcomes of that operation. It demonstrates the power of the ERA5 reanalysis dataset and the meteorological archive of the ECMWF.

While the weather conditions predicted for the morning of 6 June – mainly sunny with a north-westerly wind at Force 4 – were better than on the previous day, they were some way off meeting the original military requirements.
On D-Day, strong wind gusts of up to approximately 40 kilometres an hour and rough seas, with maximum wave heights off the Normandy beaches increasing above 1m during the morning, made beach obstacles harder to navigate by bringing the tide in more quickly than anticipated. Regardless, the weather conditions were good enough for the landings to succeed on 6 June, and follow-up activities on 7 and 8 June took place in weather that was better than Stagg had anticipated. Find out more about the conditions throughout this crucial period [here](https://www.ecmwf.int/en/research/projects/era-clim/d-day-analyses).

Notably, the unusually poor weather at that time was preceded by exceptionally warm conditions, with the UK experiencing its joint highest May temperature on record on 29 May 1944.* A record that was only broken a couple of weeks ago during a heatwave that both affected most of Western Europe and showed forecasts are still fundamental to security, health and climate resilience across the continent and beyond, with predictions saving lives.
The Weather Replay app enables anyone to revisit the weather anywhere on the globe, hour by hour, from January 1940 right up to a few days before the present. Thanks to the ERA5 reanalysis, a cloud-ready ARCO archive system, and the powerful architecture of the ECMWF Data Stores, its simulations are available in seconds. They are also produced with roughly the same tools used for the ECMWF weather forecast.
The app’s data can be explored freely by playing around in it and the “share” function empowers users to obtain a link to the view that they have selected. As well as exploring individual past events, the app can be used to compare them, such as previous heatwaves from the past eight decades.
[**Read about how to use the app to reproduce historical weather conditions, its features and what can be monitored with it.**](https://climate.copernicus.eu/weather-replay-your-time-machine-revisit-past-weather)
C3S is implemented by the ECMWF with funding from the European Commission.
