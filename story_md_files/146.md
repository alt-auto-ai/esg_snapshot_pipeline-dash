# Budget funds new CER body
The 2026-27 federal budget lays the groundwork for increased rooftop solar and home batteries on the national grid
By [Kate Burgess](https://theenergy.co/person/kate-burgess)
![](https://theenergy.co/uploads/transforms/Articles/_hero/19473/20260511164580161264-original.avif)
Finance Minister Katy Gallagher, Prime Minister Anthony Albanese and Treasurer Jim Chalmers put the finishing touches on the 2026-27 federal budget. - AAP/Lukas Coch.
A new regulator for consumer energy resources funded in the 2026-27 federal budget will coordinate an effort that the government claims could slash $7 billion off the cost of the electricity system by 2050.
The National Technical Regulator will champion a regulatory framework to integrate rooftop solar and home batteries, and eventually vehicle-to-grid into the National Electricity Market, at a cost of $25.3 million over four years, the budget papers show.
The new CER overseer is part of a roadmap costing $97.3 million over the four-year forward estimates period.
Low-carbon fuels will take a slight step forward, with the government promising to investigate a “new market measure to help drive demand” for low-carbon liquid fuels, under the previously announced $1.1 billion Cleaner Fuels Program. This could involve a liquid fuel standard, whereby industrial users must source a certain percentage of their fuels from low-carbon sources, in line with similar measures used overseas.
A department of energy and climate change official said the government would first consult with industry on what demand-side measure to go with.
### Gas reservation
The budget revealed the $26.6 million cost over four years to implement the 20 per cent gas reservation policy, in line with Climate Change and Energy Minister Chris Bowen’s announcement last week.
This will include $2.6 million over the coming financial year for the Australian Energy Market Operator (AEMO) to develop the capability to oversee the gas reservation policy. “AEMO will monitor the delivery of supplied gas to the market to ensure that reserved gas is made available under open, transparent and accessible terms to domestic users,” according to a budget overview document.
### NEM Review
Much of the work to modernise the NEM is being undertaken alongside the respective states that the system touches as part of the government’s response to last year’s NEM Review.
Budget documents confirm that as agreed at last week’s Energy and Climate Change Ministerial Council (ECMC) meeting, work would continue on Electricity Services Energy Mechanism (ESEM) contracts and the Market Making Obligation, which will require large generators to sell more of their capacity to the market.
### Planning reform
Costings are tabled for the new National Environment Protection Agency, which will open on July 1, as part of the reformed Environment Protection and Biodiversity Act and cost $250 million over the next two financial years.
This adds to the $65.6 million that Environment Minister Murray Watt has already flagged will be spent on inking new bilateral agreements with the states to enable simultaneous assessments, as well as bioregional plans that will establish development “go” and “no-go” areas.
### Australian refining
The budget also flags $10 million over the next financial year “to develop feasibility studies to expand our domestic refining capacity,” which a departmental official said would look at building new refineries that would be co-funded alongside state governments.
It also contained an obtuse reference to EV trucks with a promise to “accelerate heavy vehicle reforms through National Competition Policy … to support the uptake of zero emissions heavy vehicles."
### And the rest...
Among the pre-announced budget items was the $7.5 billion fuel and fertiliser facility and $3.2 billion to boost the Minimum Stockholding Obligation (MSO) to increase reserves of diesel and jet fuel to 50 days.
And as previously flagged, the EV tax discount will transition to a permanent 25 per cent discount on fringe benefits tax on models costing over $75,000 from April 1, 2027, expanding to all EVs two years later.
Other energy-related budget line items include:
  * $387.4 million for the CSIRO
  * $1 billion in interest free loans through the National Reconstruction Fund’s Economic Resilience Program for manufacturers and logistics groups affected by the fuel crisis
  * $55 million in 2026-27 for a Transport Resilience and Capacity Kickstart pilot program to incentivise more freight to move via trains and cargo ships.


The government also used the budget to flag that it would consult further on reforms to the controversial super performance test, which many argue disincentivises super funds from investing in infrastructure assets, including renewable energy.
The government managed to find $2.2 billion in savings over 14 years from 2025-26, by redirecting uncommitted funding through a range of Department of Climate Change, Energy, the Environment and Water programs including $1.3 billion over 10 years from the Battery Breakthrough Initiative and Solar Sunshot, and reducing the amount of funding available for Round 2 of Hydrogen Headstart to $1.0 billion.
Savings from other uncommitted grant funding included from the Powering the Regions Fund, the National Water Grid Fund and the Regional Hydrogen Hubs Program.
### The bottom line
The budget forecasts a $31.5 billion deficit for the next financial year, $2.8 billion lower than the previous forecast. Gross debt is predicted to stand at $982 billion at the end of June.
Treasury is forecasting oil prices to remain sticky at around $100/bbl until the end of June, reducing to $80/bbl by the end of the 2027 financial year. One of its scenarios sees oil prices peaking at $200, before taking three years to return to pre-crisis levels.
Higher oil prices are expected to boost inflation to 5 per cent by the end of June. This will push GDP growth from 2.25 per cent in 2025-26 to 1.75 per cent in 2026-27.
