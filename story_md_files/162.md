# Victorians to see new lower retail prices
Victorian homes and businesses have been spared from a price hike in their latest retail energy price release
By [Kate Burgess](https://theenergy.co/person/kate-burgess)
[Get The Energy in your inbox](https://theenergy.co/subscribe)
Victorian Energy consumers may have breathed a sigh of relief this morning after discovering that their retail electricity bills might fall somewhat over the coming year.
The Victorian Default Offer released by the Essential Services Commission, allows retailers to offer contracts that will be on average 3 per cent for houses and 5 per cent for small businesses.
This will come as welcome news to many amid fears of oil and gas price spikes and shortages due to the ongoing conflict in Iran, which is now into its second week after US missiles hit Iran on February 28.
Commenting on the news, Grattan Institute Deputy Head of the Climate Change and Energy program Hamish McKenzie said lower “environmental costs” were behind the downwards move, as they fell from about 7 per cent to about 4 per cent of the indicative electricity bill.
> ### Expert view
> Notable was the stability in wholesale and network costs, which have fluctuatedsubstantially in the past few years. After wholesale spikes of up to 80 per cent year on year in the early 2020s, costs have now stayed within a range of $10 for the past three VDOs, suggesting that some of the fluctuations in prices attributed to the energy transition might be smoothing out.

> [Hamish McKenzie](https://theenergy.co/person/hamish-mckenzie)
> Deputy head of Climate and Energy Program, Grattan Institute
As the name suggests, the VDO applies only to retail consumers in Victoria and is separate from the Default Market Offer (DMO) used as a benchmark price elsewhere in the National Electricity Market. It will be released by the Australian Energy Regulator on Thursday March 19.
A curious omission from the VDO is pricing that would apply under the Solar Sharer deal. This is expected to be offered to other states in the DMO. Solar Sharer will allow participating households to get free solar electricity for three hours during the day when the sun typically shines and turns electricity prices negative.
Solar sharer is due for release on July 1, but the exact details of the pricing is still being debated and is not firmly set. There are reports that there is a variable network charge in the usage tariff offered that consumers would need to pay even if prices turned negative for a few hours, but an AER source who declined to be named said that the rules on that had not been firmed up.
"Victorians will still be able to get three free hours of solar from one of the retailers that offer them. Most Victorians are paying far more than they need to for electricity, and most won’t benefit from these cost reductions unless they search for and sign up to a better deal," McKenzie added.
The VDO is not the cheapest energy deal that Victorian customers have - it is only the one they fall back on if they do not shop around for a cheaper deal, or install solar panels to generate their own electricity.
McKenzie said that it was important for electricity consumers to shop around, even though the VDO had fallen when compared to previous years.
“The message is clear: Victorians should use the Victorian Energy Compare tool to find a better, cheaper plan.”
The ESC is due to make the final ruling on its draft pricing in May.
