# Rethink gas use with biogas and hydrogen: Grattan
A leading energy think tank is urging Canberra to boost incentives for the fuels that will replace fossil gas so they can compete on cost
By [Kate Burgess](https://theenergy.co/person/kate-burgess)
![](https://theenergy.co/uploads/transforms/Articles/_hero/20216/shutterstock_2606176195.avif)
The role for gas in Australia's energy future remains under question - Shutterstock
Australia’s future gas strategy forecasts a large role for gas, both as a domestic fuel and for export, but domestic and global demand has been falling and the fuel will need to play less of a central role in power generation and industry if Australia is going to meet its 2050 net zero targets.
This choose-your-own-adventure future has prompted the Grattan Institute to call for more investments in the fuels that may replace or complement gas to make them cost-competitive. The Australian Energy Market Operator forecasts gas demand to drop more than 40 per cent on the east coast, primarily due to steep declines in the residential and commercial buildings industry as they seek electrification solutions.
Solutions involve moving industrial customers to electrification or renewable fuels that can travel through gas networks, depending on the size of the business and what feedstock it uses.
Grattan Institute Climate & Energy deputy program director Hamish McKenzie said that industries that use gas as a feedstock for higher heat, such as steel and aluminium smelting will mostly need to find new fuels that are like methane and can be “dropped in” to the same network infrastructure.
> #### Expert view
> ”The Australian economy will be a mostly electric economy in 2050. Most users of gas will switch to electricity to heat their homes, cook their food, run their factories, and generate their electricity. There is a rump of users, though, who do not have ready electrification options.
> “There are some industrial users of gas that don't have alternatives. That is why we will need some amount of gas in the future.”

The government has been busily investing in biomethane - made from animal and sewage products and chemically identical to natural gas - and hydrogen produced by renewable energy to bolster natural gas supplies while helping to reduce emissions.
Biomethane can cost up to $20-$30/GJ, depending on which feedstock has been used, far in excess of the $10-12/MJ that domestic gas currently costs.
“The challenge with biomethane is trying to find someone who can produce meaningful volumes and bridge the cost gap,” McKenzie explains.
### Hydrofail
Hydrogen, meanwhile, usually involves a facility investing in new equipment so it can be used as a fuel, such as a cement, steel or fertiliser factory using it to make green ammonia for the first time. Its current cost is around $7.50/kg, compared to the $2/kg it would need to compete against existing fuels.
“ There has been billions invested into green hydrogen over the last couple of years in Australia and there's been a very public wave of failed projects. Of the operating hydrogen electrolysers in Australia, they're producing about 1PJ of hydrogen. So we're still very much at the niche stage,” McKenzie explains.
ARENA has heavily invested in hydrogen through the $1 billion Headstart program, which has resulted in funding for the Murchison green hydrogen project and the Hunter Valley hydrogen hub, but some of the funding for other projects such as that from Origin Energy was clawed back because the companies decided not to progress them.
McKenzie said that more investment to support demand for biomethane and hydrogen was needed, once projects were commercially developed and injecting the fuels into gas networks. The government has taken this step with low-carbon liquid fuels like sustainable aviation fuel and renewable diesel, once the products were in a later stage of development.
Unlike some pundits, he isn’t forecasting an immediate decline of the global LNG industry, because exporters will continue to meet demand from overseas buyers, particularly during the Middle Eastern fuel crisis.
Longer term, this could look very different, however. “Demand for Australian liquefied natural gas (LNG) exports is forecast to decline faster than domestic demand, but the LNG sector is still forecast to be four times the size of the domestic gas sector by 2045,” McKenzie added.
These apparent contradictions highlight the difficulties policymakers have when choosing which projects and industries to back, and what the role of the gas network is in supporting them.
McKenzie believes that the transmission network will be needed to connect large industrial gas users to supply sources, and that this will step up economically, but that as more residential customers drop off distribution grids, the cost of supporting industrial customers connected there must be spread over a larger number of individual users.
“ Residential customers provide a disproportionate amount of revenue to the gas networks. The decline of residential gas consumption creates an existential threat for industrial gas users being able to access the network,” he says.
“The Jemena network in New South Wales has one and a half million residential customers and four hundred industrial customers, and they each consume about half of the gas on the network. The residential customers consume half the gas, but they contribute about 85 percent of the annual revenue for Jemena.”
The Institute’s  _Out of Gas_ report is urging the government to step up planning and funding for biomethane, hydrogen and the work of managing the future of the gas transmission and distribution networks so they remain cost-effective for their consumers.
“ One of the recommendations is that instead of the networks themselves doing their demand forecasting, we should get AEMO to do a central gas demand forecast so that we actually understand where industrial and electrical demand for gas will be in the future,” Hamish noted.
Careful attention should be paid to policy design in the [gas reservation program](https://theenergy.co/article/gas-reservation-design-three-flaws-and-a-tightrope), which will give gas companies incentives to develop biomethane projects and the safeguard mechanism, which covers large industrial gas users but doesn’t currently provide incentives for smaller industries to switch to electricity or use renewable fuels.
“ The safeguard mechanism is not hugely successful in driving reductions in gas use so far. Our recommendation is that the government should use the upcoming review of the mechanism to sharpen the incentives for electrification, but also to drive the facilities to start purchasing renewable gases,” McKenzie concludes.
