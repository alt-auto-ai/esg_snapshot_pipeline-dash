# What those who envisage in-home medical care have missed
A bright future of in-home aged care is envisioned by companies developing medical technology, but without proper consideration of future energy constraints
By [Fareed Kaviani](https://theenergy.co/person/fareed-kaviani) and [Kari Dahlgren](https://theenergy.co/person/kari-dahlgren)
![An illustration of an elderly man sitting with a robot friend, while a holographic nurse hovers in the background](https://theenergy.co/uploads/transforms/Articles/_hero/18985/Care-2.avif)
The medtech industry envisages a raft of home-care innovations, but these rely on reliable power. - Fareed Kaviani
The future, we are told by medical technology companies, will involve frictionless, personalised and preventive healthcare products and services delivered directly into our homes. Ageing populations and overstretched health systems can be supported by a seamless integration of digital infrastructure. But beneath these projections lies a largely unspoken assumption: that homes will have reliable, affordable electricity and uninterrupted digital connectivity. In fact, climate and energy reports predict the opposite: a future defined by disruption, household electrification inequities and escalating risk.
These expectations are not trivial. Australia’s care economy is projected to double in coming decades, and industry actors position AI and digital health as essential to managing this growth. The home is reimagined as a node in a distributed healthcare network integrated with sensors, connected devices and data flows.
Wearable sensors will continuously stream biometric data to clinicians. Smart medication systems will track adherence. At-home diagnostic kits will detect disease early. Domestic robots will assist with mobility, cleaning and companionship. Predictive analytics will anticipate health risks before symptoms arise.
In these visions, older adults are supported to live independently for longer. Health systems become more efficient. Travel is reduced. Workforce shortages are offset through automation and digital triage. Care is proactive, rather than reactive.
At the same time that healthcare is moving into the home, Australia is undergoing what has been described as the biggest transformation of the National Electricity Market since its formation.
Decarbonisation is accelerating the electrification of households. Rooftop solar, home batteries, electric vehicles and smart appliances are expected to become standard features of domestic life. This transition is occurring under intensifying climate pressures.

This is not to say that AI-enabled diagnostics and remote care will not offer genuine opportunities to improve access and outcomes, particularly for people in remote or underserved areas. Rather, we suggest this future needs to be in dialogue with climate and energy forecasts.
Climate change and the energy transition are not background conditions; they are constitutive forces shaping how life will unfold. Designing AI systems for ageing populations without accounting for heat stress, insurance retreat, grid instability and energy affordability risks creating brittle infrastructures.
What would it mean to design AgeTech with climate disruption as a certainty rather than an afterthought? Devices could be built with integrated battery backups. Platforms could prioritise low-energy operation modes. Care models could incorporate offline contingencies and community-based support networks. Energy planners and digital health designers could collaborate to identify households dependent on life-sustaining technologies and ensure resilience planning through keeping accurate life support registers.
More fundamentally, we might ask whether technological optimisation alone is sufficient to address the social challenges of ageing.
AI-enabled health scoring systems, for example, are envisioned in some reports as empowering individuals to manage their wellbeing. Yet, they also raise the prospect of differential insurance premiums, employment advantages and new forms of surveillance.
When climate change is added to this picture, inequalities might deepen. Lower-income households already experience lower levels of digital inclusion. Rural and regional communities face connectivity constraints. The cost of energy, insurance and device maintenance may compound vulnerability for those most reliant on home-based care.

As socio-technical visions of the future shape investment and policy, the values embedded in these scenarios matter. Efficiency and productivity cannot be the only metrics guiding the future of care.
The promise of healthcare at home is compelling. But without confronting the material realities of climate change and energy instability, the vision of seamless AI-enabled care risks creating more harm than good.
The future of ageing will not unfold in a vacuum of uninterrupted connectivity. It will unfold in homes exposed to heat, storms, market volatility and infrastructural strain. Designing for that future – honestly and collectively – is the task ahead.
