The new study used Fitbits and EEG sensors1 to measure heart rate and brainwave activity in 58 participants as they sat in front of, or near, green walls and roofs. Researchers also asked participants about their mental state and perception of temperature and recorded the actual cooling effect of the green infrastructure on the immediate environment.
This is the first research to investigate green infrastructure’s combined impact on perceived and actual heat stress, psychological health and physiological responses such as heart rate, say the researchers. Its findings come amid rising concern about the urban heat island (UHI) effect. Cities worldwide are experiencing increases in artificial surfaces like tarmac and concrete, while green space is declining. As a result, urban temperatures can rise up to 12°C higher than in rural areas, significantly increasing heat exposure.
Extreme heat is known to cause thermal stress, psychological distress and physical illness. Although green space has been shown to reduce these effects, expanding traditional parks and vegetation in crowded cities is challenging due to a lack of available land. Vertical and rooftop greening offers a promising alternative but it has not been widely evaluated.
The [2013 EU Green Infrastructure Strategy](https://environment.ec.europa.eu/topics/nature-and-biodiversity/green-infrastructure_en) describes green walls and roofs as “no-regret” nature-based solutions for mitigating heat islands and enhancing biodiversity, and they are promoted again in the [EU Green Deal](https://commission.europa.eu/strategy-and-policy/priorities-2019-2024/european-green-deal_en). However, specific evidence on their physiological and psychological benefits has been limited.
Recent technological advances have made it possible to move beyond self-reported wellbeing to record objective physiological responses. By using heart rate data and EEG patterns, this study delivers a more robust and holistic understanding of how green infrastructure affects health and how it can be designed and implemented effectively.
The researchers installed three types of green infrastructure on a rooftop in Seoul, South Korea: a live plant green roof, a live plant green wall and an artificial plant green wall. Participants were university students aged 19-34, all native Korean speakers. Over 11 days, each participant experienced five conditions: viewing a bare wall front-on, viewing a live green wall front-on, sitting with their back to a live green wall, viewing an artificial green wall front-on, and sitting in front of the green roof.
In each setting, researchers collected mood and thermal perception surveys alongside heart rate and EEG readings. Environmental variables such as wind speed, air temperature and humidity were also recorded. Air temperature data were used to calculate the Wet-Bulb Globe Temperature (WBGT) – a key index for heat stress – in the area by the green wall or roof. The WBGT accounts for temperature, humidity, sunlight and wind and is used to manage heat risk during outdoor work and activities.
Results showed that every green infrastructure setting significantly reduced heat stress, measured by WBGT, compared with the bare wall. The largest reduction occurred in front of the live green wall, followed by the artificial green wall and then the live green roof. Participants’ perceptions of thermal comfort aligned closely with these objective measurements, indicating that the cooling effect of greenery is both physically real and psychologically noticeable.
The plant species used for the green walls released more water vapour during the daytime than the species used on the roof, giving the walls an advantage in reducing daytime heat stress. Surprisingly, the green wall made of artificial plants also had a measurable cooling effect, likely due to the thermal properties of its structural materials.
Physiological responses reinforced environmental findings. When participants sat near live greenery, their heart rates dropped significantly compared to the bare wall condition, suggesting that natural elements provided physical relief and relaxation. EEG data further revealed that visible greenery elicited brain activity patterns associated with increased wakefulness, arousal, relaxation and attention. These neural responses mirrored participants’ self-reported reductions in emotional distress, which were strongest when they were directly facing green surfaces.
Importantly, the study found that visual exposure to greenery played a more powerful role in delivering psychological benefits than the physical cooling itself. This included artificial greenery, indicating that even where live plants cannot be installed, well-designed artificial green infrastructure may still deliver meaningful mental health improvements.
By integrating environmental measurements, physiological data and psychological surveys, the study provides a useful assessment of how rooftop and vertical greenery support human health in hot urban environments. It confirms that green walls and roofs offer multiple, interacting benefits – reducing heat stress, improving mood, lowering heart rate and positively influencing brain activity.
The findings strengthen the case for expanding such green infrastructure, especially in cities where land is scarce. The researchers also call for further research on how green roofs and walls perform in different climates, with varied vegetation, soil conditions and spatial configurations.
