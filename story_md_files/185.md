### $20k awarded to circular economy innovators
  * Circular economy
  * Funding
  * May 25, 2026

![](https://www.greenindustries.sa.gov.au/images/news/260525-CIA-awards.jpg)
Green Industries SA (GISA) recently joined Collab4Good and program partners at Lot Fourteen for the Circular Impact Accelerator 2026 Pitch Night.
Now in its fifth year, the Circular Impact Accelerator supports emerging circular economy ventures to test, validate and refine business ideas that tackle some of our most pressing waste and resource recovery challenges.
Six teams completed the intensive 6-week program which culminated in a pitch night, where each team presented their business ideas to a live audience and a panel of judges.
GISA awarded a total of $20,000 in business development funding on the night, with judging based on the clarity of the circular problem being solved, the viability of the business solution, team capability and passion, and the strength of the pitch.
Jeremy Dossor from Waste Not Site Management was awarded the $12,000 major prize, as well as the $1,000 People’s Choice Award, as voted by the audience on the night.
Kris James from Adelaide Electrical Salvage was awarded the $7,000 second prize.
Read on to learn more about this year’s teams.
### Jeremy Dossor – Waste Not Site Management
Waste Not Site Management works to reduce construction waste on building sites by enabling builders to understand their waste, recover all valuable materials, and reuse it in its highest value form.
### Kris James – Adelaide Electrical Salvage
Adelaide Electrical Salvage turns e-waste into a parts supply chain, recovering working components from discarded electronics and using them to repair appliances and equipment, and also making them available for sale online – giving repairers and DIYers across Australia access to affordable parts that would otherwise go to waste.
### Sarah Whittaker and Callum Unger – Pressed Planet
Pressed Planet turns plastic waste from Adelaide eateries and events into reusable flat-pack drink trays. Each product is made locally, and once worn out can be returned to Pressed Planet to be remade.
### Hayley Polyichanin – The SA Repair Network
The SA Repair Network is working to bring together South Australia’s repair sector by supporting local businesses to grow, and advocating for government policy and programs that enable repair, and educating consumers on the value of repair.
### Hayden Nguyen and Cherry Nguyen – BEXT Solutions
BEXT Solutions takes unwanted agricultural waste and turns it into affordable and biodegradable fertilisers.
### Amanda Porter – Little Earthlings
Little Earthlings is a second-hand clothing website, selling quality pre-loved clothing for children from birth to teens, that allows parents to ‘op shop’ from home. Little Earthlings’ future goal is to create meaningful and flexible employment opportunities for people with disabilities, carers and parents looking to return to work.

# Australians ready to bypass solar and battery barriers
Australians’ love for new technology and desire for energy savings means the solar and battery boom could be about to enter a new phase
By [Charis Palmer](https://theenergy.co/person/charis-palmer)
![Solar panels on a balcony](https://theenergy.co/uploads/transforms/Articles/_hero/19856/shutterstock_2304835429.avif)
Germany's adoption of balcony solar took off after the Ukraine war - Shutterstock
The first job for the Clean Energy Regulator’s [newly funded](https://theenergy.co/article/budget) technical arm for consumer energy resources should be to get the tick for plug-in devices like balcony solar and small batteries, says Rewiring Australia CEO Francis Vierboom.
“That will deliver some real results to people, and it's not a hard ask when you've got such a strong international precedent, a 230 volt advanced economy that's got nearly a million and a half of these things installed already,” Vierboom tells  _The Energy_ , pointing to Germany’s [balcony solar boom](https://avanzaenergy.substack.com/p/1-million-balcony-solar-panels-zero).
His comments come as [new analysis](https://energyconsumersaustralia.com.au/news/half-australians-facing-barriers-solar-battery-revolution-new-report-finds) from Energy Consumers Australia shows at least half of Australian households face structural barriers to adopting solar and batteries, with many still interested in the technology.
![](https://theenergy.co/uploads/transforms/Photos/_hero/19851/PQpOm-room-to-run.avif)
Despite one in three households already having rooftop solar in Australia, a further 15 per cent are considering it and among households with solar, 16 per cent already have a battery (as at end 2025) and 47 per cent are researching or considering one, showing the consumer energy boom still has room to run.
The findings are in line with AEMO’s draft 2026 ISP forecast that 47 per cent of households in NEM regions will have rooftop solar by 2035, and around half the households with solar will have supporting batteries by 2050.
What has shifted is the size of those batteries and the speed with which they have been taken up in response to subsidies.
AEMO recently confirmed in a forecasting workshop it would be updating its forecasts for home batteries in the 2026 ISP in response to stakeholder feedback and the Cheaper Home Batteries Program.
On the other side, it will be [revising down](https://theenergy.co/article/fresh-reality-check-for-virtual-power-plants) anticipated participation in virtual power plants.
![](https://theenergy.co/uploads/transforms/Photos/_hero/19547/VPPForecast.avif) The market operator is revising down expectations of VPP participation - AEMO Forecasting Reference Group meeting May 2026
### **Motivation matters…**
The new ECA analysis shows that the biggest precursor to solar and battery adoption is how engaged people are with the energy system - meaning whether they are willing to be involved in choosing between different tariffs, monitoring energy use, or managing imports and exports.
Households that are considering adoption are twice as likely to want an active relationship with the energy system (60% compared to 30%), according to the ECA survey.
“A lot of people living in apartments say they are interested in solar and batteries. Now, you could say maybe a lot of them sadly can't have it, but there is a really strong interest,” said ECA executive manager for advocacy and analysis Ashley Bradshaw.
![](https://theenergy.co/uploads/transforms/Photos/_hero/19850/ILS9R-engagement-with-the-system.avif)
Even among those facing a barrier to adoption such as having a low income or living in an apartment or rental, around one in five still want an active relationship with their energy provider, and one in 10 are considering solar and/or battery technology.
### **…but understanding isn’t strong**
One surprising finding from the ECA analysis is that even among home battery owners there is still confusion about the retail energy offering, be it of how bills work or of feed-in tariffs and smart meters.
![](https://theenergy.co/uploads/transforms/Photos/_hero/19852/Kmte3-what-s-a-feed-in-tariff.avif)
Less than half of those who already have a battery know what a feed-in tariff is, which helps to explain why so few households are getting on board with helping the grid. ECA surveys show the main motivator for home battery adoption remains reducing bills, with consumers having a strong desire to maintain control.
With the introduction of Solar Sharer from July, all eyes are on how consumers will respond and whether it will push more households, including those without space for an electrician-installed device, to consider a home battery.
If the regulator can smooth the way for safe plug-in battery and solar options for renters and apartment dwellers, which Vierboom says is [technically possible](https://rewiringaustralia.substack.com/p/enabling-portable-energy-devices), Australia’s consumer energy revolution could break even more records.
