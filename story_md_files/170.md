# Investors rail against mooted CGT change
The peak body for clean energy investors has warned of price hikes and energy security fears if Treasurer Jim Chalmers follows through with proposed changes to capital gains tax
By [Kate Burgess](https://theenergy.co/person/kate-burgess)
![](https://theenergy.co/uploads/transforms/Articles/_hero/19241/20260504168554324550-original.avif)
Treasurer Jim Chalmers has surprised the clean energy sector with a proposal to levy CGT on the sale of foreign-owned assets - AAP/Lukas Coch.
Renewable energy investors have implored Treasurer Jim Chalmers to exempt those domiciled overseas from its proposed capital gains tax regime until the government’s 82 per cent renewables target has been met.
Voicing their angst through the Clean Energy Investor Group, the investors are seeking transitional arrangements over and above the four-year 50 per cent CGT discount outlined in a consultation paper released last month.
“Four years, as proposed, risks a fire sale of assets. Those investors in a position to do so, are likely to exit Australia,” the CEIG wrote in its submission, obtained by  _The Energy_.
"[A transitional arrangement] will provide targeted support for the construction of new power plants as capital gains are key to the business models of greenfield developers.”
The reforms are designed to correct a loophole in existing tax law that meant foreign investors only pay 30 per cent CGT on sales of land, and not on assets or businesses that sit on the land. Besides renewable energy, they include heavy mining equipment, ports and electricity transmission and distribution networks.
But Treasury’s revelation of how the change might be enacted immediately put investors on the defensive, because the original rules date from 2006, meaning that they could technically apply to all investments with a foreign party made for the past 20 years.
Clean Energy Regulator and InfraLogic figures show that more than three-quarters of the wind, solar and battery projects that reached financial close in the three years to March 2026 involved foreign capital, while domestic investors accounted for 11.9 per cent and government entities 11.5 per cent.
The CEIG submission warns of multiple dire consequences that would stymie investment in the sector, before it has garnered enough critical mass to emerge as the backbone of the National Electricity Market, as government policy intends it to.
Price hikes resulting from cancelled projects and less supply-side competition, curtailed greenfield project investment and an inability for overseas-based developers to recycle capital from operational projects into riskier early stage ones, and heightened sovereign risk are all likely to emerge if foreign investors are charged CGT on assets they choose to exit, the submission warned.
The CEIG urged the government to rethink its stance on applying the tax to existing investments. “Retrospective changes on sunk capital is fundamentally unfair and would deter future investment more broadly,” the submission noted.
The new regime should sunset when existing net zero targets are met, and following this a 15 per cent “post-transition” CGT rate should apply, it also stated. Foreign investors should be able to access concessional tax rates “on equal terms” as their domestic counterparts.
“This aligns all investors - domestic and global. Performance-based sunset supports capital recycling and avoids permanent concessions that distort investment decisions and erode the base.”
In 2024, CEIG modelling found that a CGT change could push residential power prices up by 4 per cent, or $100 a year. CEO Richie Merzian said the group intended to re-run the modelling exercise, but pointed out that the current consultation period, which closed on April 24, was too brief to commission the work.
The group wants the government to undertake more work on its proposal before attempting to pass legislation. “At a minimum, such analysis should investigate the impacts on economic activity, tax revenue, overall investment, sector specific investment from domestic and international sources, and Commonwealth and state-level policies created to attract highly mobile global capital.”
