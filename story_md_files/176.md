Labor is backing better facilities for grassroots sporting clubs.
Minister Spence today visited Lionheart Reserve in Taylors Lakes to announce applications are open for the next round of the Local Sports Infrastructure Fund (LSIF).
All Victorian councils and Alpine Resorts Victoria are eligible to apply in partnership with sport and recreation clubs, sport associations and leagues.
Funding up to $500,000 is on offer for projects that boost participation.
It’s all about getting more Victorians into sports, so they can have fun, get fit and spend quality time with family and friends.
Projects funded under previous rounds include:
  * $500,000 towards a new youth recreation precinct at Monterey Reserve in Frankston North
  * $40,000 for the Horsham Aquatic Centre to help community members get more active
  * $150,000 towards the installation of 350 lux LED lighting for six synthetic tennis courts at Maffra Lawn Tennis Club


We are proud to fund initiatives like these because they also create jobs and boost the economy.
The _Victorian Budget 2026/27_ includes $24 million to continue providing new and improved community sports infrastructure, including through the LSIF _._
It’s part of more than $2.4 billion Labor has invested in community sport and recreation infrastructure.
To learn more or apply, visit [www.sport.vic.gov.au](https://sport.vic.gov.au/funding/local-sports-infrastructure-fund).

_“Community sport is the heartbeat of local communities, and we are proud to support it.”_
_“We are making sure clubs have modern and accessible facilities, making it easier for players, coaches, umpires and supporters of all abilities to get involved.”_
**Quotes attributable to Member for Sydenham Natalie Hutchins**

# Renewables will comprise two-thirds of global energy spending this year: IEA
War driving energy market disruption, but not like that of the 1970s
By [David Braue](https://theenergy.co/person/david-braue)
![](https://theenergy.co/uploads/transforms/Articles/_hero/17114/Fatih-Birol_NPC.avif)
International Energy Agency executive director Fatih Birol is warning policymakers not to underestimate the size of the current energy crisis - Lukas Coch/AAP
Spending on renewable energy infrastructure will account for 65 per cent of the $4.8 trillion (US$3.4 trillion) global energy market this year, the International Energy Agency has predicted in releasing its latest [ _World Energy Investment 2026_](https://www.iea.org/reports/world-energy-investment-2026) report – which found the disruptions of the Middle East crisis are pushing governments to dial up renewables, grid strengthening, expansion of electrification initiatives and acceleration of energy efficiency overhauls.
Some $3.1 trillion (US$2.2 trillion) in renewable energy infrastructure expenditure will go to grids, energy storage, low-emissions fuels, nuclear power, renewables, efficiency and electrification this year, the IEA said, with executive director Fatih Birol [predicting](https://www.iea.org/news/impacts-of-middle-east-conflict-set-to-reshape-energy-investment-plans-as-disruptions-put-focus-on-security) that the disruption “will reshape investment strategies globally, with parallels to the major changes the energy world witnessed after the oil shocks of the 1970s.”
Yet where the shocks of the 1970s drove a resurgence in gas and oil exploration, the response this time around will look far different, with oil singled out as a clear loser this year: despite the conflict pushing crude oil’s value to dizzying heights – and many warning it could go higher still – oil investment is set to decline for the third year in a row as energy systems are progressively diversified to avoid the concentrated risk that got us to this point.
Another fossil-fuel stalwart, however, is seeing a resurgence – with investment in coal set to increase to $252 billion (US$180 billion) this year, largely led by the all-hands energy push in China, which is simultaneously leading the world in solar PV rollouts by a country mile. Many of the countries rediscovering their love for coal are in Asia, where the conflict-driven drought of oil has hit particularly hard.
Nuclear, an old-school favourite, is also surging this year as governments pour over $112 billion (US$80 billion) into a nuclear pipeline that has over 80GW of new capacity underway in 15 countries.
And gas, which has become a political whipping boy in Australia, shows no signs of stopping at the global level – with orders for new gas-fired power plants reaching a 25-year high last year, driven particularly by exploding data centre demand.
### But who will pay for it all?
Behind the headline numbers, the IEA noted that the uncertainty created by the ongoing Iran conflict “is complicating the prospects” for energy project finance – with market volatility slowing investment decisions and increasing financing costs that are already being hit by interest rate creep.
A new Pitchbook [analysis](https://pitchbook.com/news/reports/q1-2026-launch-report-oil-gas) has highlighted the investment gap such uncertainty is creating, noting that the “structurally underinvested” oil and gas sector requires around $1.7 trillion (US$1.2 trillion) in annual investment over the next decade – 20 per cent more than the average investments over the past decade – to replace the more than 7 million barrels per day necessary just to maintain supply.
The new IEA market outlook, however, predicts that much will be spent on oil, gas  _and coal_ this year – which, if you subtract the forecast spending on coal, leaves just $1.3 trillion (US$948 billion) for oil and gas investment.
And that, if you follow Pitchbook’s argument, means actual spending is 31 per cent lower than it needs to be just for the oil and gas sector to keep up with demand.
Little wonder Pitchbook said the supply side of the energy equation is “the defining investment dynamic of the coming decade,” arguing that private equity investment will be crucial to boosting upstream spending by 25 per cent to 30 per cent in order to offset “accelerating natural production declines”.
Convincing jittery investors to see energy as a safe bet, then, remains a key challenge in a market where diversification is increasingly recognised as crucial but everybody has their own favourites.
Rising long-term finance “could disproportionately affect capital-intensive energy technologies,” the IEA report warns, with an updated [dashboard](https://www.iea.org/data-and-statistics/data-tools/investment-data-explorer) of investment data confirming that equity investments have flatlined in recent years while debt finance has continued to grow.
Indeed, in 2022 debt accounted for 76 per cent as much finance as equity did – but by last year this had grown steadily to 92 per cent, beating pre-COVID levels for the first time as escalating energy demands exceed investors’ liquid resources.
Significantly, the IEA also noted private financiers comprise the lion’s share of investment, delivering US$2.4 trillion last year compared to US$802 billion from public sources and just US$16 billion from ‘international public’ sources.
### Grow supply, modify demand
Even as the Iran conflict drives pushes supply-side investors to reconsider their risk appetites, the IEA report also [notes](https://iea.blob.core.windows.net/assets/4fda38df-523c-46f5-ae75-49481abdc8fc/WorldEnergyInvestment2026.pdf#page=105) the increasingly important role of electrification initiatives that are reshaping demand-side dynamics – helping generation infrastructure meet demand somewhere in the middle.
“Rising demand for energy services is influencing energy investment trends more profoundly than any single price cycle, policy shift or technology breakthrough,” the report notes, calling energy efficiency “a central component of energy investment, in conjunction with electrification.”
Efficiency and electrification investments are particularly important during periods of geopolitical uncertainty, the IEA said while noting the complexities that come from having to scale end-use investments across millions of assets, technologies and decision makers.
With investment in heat pumps, EVs, and electrified industrial processes increasing at around 15 per cent annually, the IEA welcomed “prospects for scalability and technological maturity” – while warning that “progress is uneven, accelerating when infrastructure, prices and finance align, and slowing when affordability, grid readiness or technology risk barriers persist.”
This uneven progress had left public, not private, sources as the “central driver” of energy-related investment in buildings, the IEA said while [noting](https://iea.blob.core.windows.net/assets/4fda38df-523c-46f5-ae75-49481abdc8fc/WorldEnergyInvestment2026.pdf#page=110) that public electrification initiatives had become “more focused instruments” over time.
Such is the sprawling, dynamic and rapidly changing global energy market this year, which the IEA calls “a transformative moment for world energy investment”.  
  
“The conflict is not over, and the precise contours of this new energy investment landscape will become clear only over time,” the report says. “But the way that global oil and gas supply – and large parts of the global economy – can be disrupted by blocking a 50-km wide waterway will not be quickly forgotten.”
