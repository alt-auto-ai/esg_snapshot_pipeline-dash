The EU’s customs authorities play an important role in protecting the EU’s external borders and citizens from threats such as counterfeit goods, drug trafficking, and other illicit activities. The import control system (ICS2) is a mandatory, EU-wide IT system that supports these efforts through early collection and analysis of information on goods entering the EU. 
The complete transition to ICS2 is part of the safety and security requirements established by the EU’s Union Customs Code (UCC). The system has been fully operational across all modes of transport since this year, marking an important milestone in the EU’s efforts to improve its risk management capabilities using advance data analytics. The successful transition has been prepared in close collaboration between the Commission, Member State customs authorities and business. This success bodes well for future progress on the EU’s ambitious Customs Reform. 
ICS2 is already providing concrete benefits for customs authorities on the frontline, protecting citizens and the EU economy. 
Here are**examples of ICS2 in action in 2025** : 
  * At **Luxembourg airport** , customs authorities used ICS2 to seize nearly 2 tonnes of cannabis and more than 360 kg of cocaine, worth 100 million in street value.
  * A merchant vessel and its entire cargo were seized by **Italian authorities** in Brindisi after arriving from Russian territorial waters with a cargo of 33,000 tonnes of ferrous material. Initially, the transaction appeared legitimate, but further investigations due to the loading port indicated in the ICS2 data revealed inconsistencies and falsifications in the ship's documentation, including a denied port of departure and altered navigation records. The vessel had loaded cargo at a Russian port subject to EU sanctions and had switched off its Automatic Identification System to evade geolocation and monitoring.
  * Following a successful cocaine seizure in air freight traffic in Germany, **real-time ICS2 cooperation between Germany, Belgium and the Netherlands** led to the seizure of a further over 130 kg of cocaine. 
  * Through **ICS2 cooperation with Germany** , **Dutch customs** seized almost 7.9 million untaxed cigarettes from a sea freight container. The cargo was disguised as disposable bowls. The smuggled cigarettes were intended for the French market. This prevented a potential loss of over EUR 3 million in tobacco tax, plus the additional VAT evasion. In another case, Germany intercepted roughly 3.5 million cigarettes thanks to real-time ICS2 collaboration with the Netherlands.
  * **Spanish customs** seized more than 2 tonnes of cocaine, coming from Ecuador, at the Valencia port, at the beginning of November 2025; almost 800 kg of marijuana, coming from Thailand, were seized at the Barcelona port, at the end of May 2025, thanks to ICS2.
  * **Danish customs** uncovered a suspected alcohol duty evasion scheme triggered by the absence of an ICS2 Entry Summary Declaration.
  * **Swiss customs** seized around 1.1 million cigarettes at Geneva airport based on information received from the United Kingdom. The smuggled cigarettes were disguised as products from a well-known watch manufacturer and destined to Northern Ireland. 


These are only a few examples of how the system is used by customs authorities to analyse safety and security data, identify high-risk consignments and take targeted action to prevent them from entering the EU internal market. While ICS2 has already been proving itself on the ground, the European Commission and national customs authorities continue to improve the system to sustain its effectiveness against illicit and fraudulent activities. 
## How ICS2 works 
Economic Operators who bring goods into or transition them through the EU, must declare safety and security data to ICS2 through an Entry Summary Declaration (ENS). Customs authorities then run a safety and security risk analysis on the basis of the ENS, which allows them to identify and stop potentially high-risk goods before they enter the EU. Economic operators are also essential in this process, as they need to provide accurate, timely and complete information about their goods. Their cooperation has been instrumental in ensuring the smooth operation of the system. 
## A broader vision to modernise customs 
The EU is committed to developing more modern and efficient customs processes. The implementation of ICS2 is just one part of the EU’s broader actions to make customs fit for the future. 
The EU has already put forward proposals for the **most ambitious and comprehensive reform of the EU Customs Union since its establishment in 1968**. The measures proposed present a world-leading, data-driven vision for EU Customs, which will massively simplify customs processes for business. Embracing the digital transformation, the reform will cut down on cumbersome customs procedures, replacing traditional declarations with a smarter, data-led approach to import supervision. At the same time, customs authorities will have the tools and resources they need to properly assess and stop imports which pose real risks to the EU, its citizens and its economy.
