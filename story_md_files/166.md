# Bowen keeps one in reserve
The government continued its pre-budget announceables spree with more detail on the gas reservation policy
By [Kate Burgess](https://theenergy.co/person/kate-burgess)
Buyers of Australian exported gas with existing contracts will be exempted from new rules requiring project owners to set aside 20 per cent of production volumes for domestic users.
More than four months after being first flagged, Climate Change and Energy Minister Chris Bowen, Resources Minister Madeleine King and Industry Minister Tim Ayres fronted media to unveil the finer details of the gas reservation policy, which is aimed at shoring up supplies ahead of a predicted shortage in east coast gas markets by mid-2028.
"We've created a buyer's market where those producers, to get that export approval, have to compete among themselves for the contracts to supply - to actually supply not just offer - to the entire domestic consumption market," Resources Minister Catherine King said.
While the fresh details came as welcome news for the energy industry, which [had been craving certainty](https://theenergy.co/article/bowen-maintains-reserve-on) since the policy first arose from last year's gas market review, the announcement carried little fine print about how the mechanism would operate in practice.
The energy and resources ministers will jointly administer the Domestic Supply Obligation Powers in conjunction with the industry minister, the three said in a statement. It must be legislated before it can come into operation.
As previously flagged when the policy was first announced last December, the mechanism will replace existing gas supply policy including a heads of agreement with exporters and the Australian Domestic Gas Security Mechanism, will apply to spot cargoes only and come into effect on January 1 of next year.
"We think it's just unacceptable for Australia to continually have those gas shortfalls forecast because that provides uncertainty for the industry. In relation to infrastructure, it's a fair question because there are constraints on infrastructure, you're right," Bowen said.
"What we will do is work with industry to best ways for them to develop that infrastructure so that they can meet their obligations. We think this supply obligation is not just about making gas available, it's about selling gas and that also involves getting gas to where it's required."
The policy news adds to a shopping list of pre-budget announcements made this week, including a new 25 per cent flat EV discount and a $10 billion fuel reserve to boost Australia's minimum holdings of petrol, diesel and jet fuel to 50 days, plus a measure to secure fertiliser supplies.
The government will undertake a further round of consultation before releasing full policy details and tabling the draft bill in parliament.
Grattan Institute senior fellow Tony Wood warned of multiple market distortions once the policy comes into full effect.
> #### Expert view
> "When this whole thing was announced just before Christmas, the government said [the reserved volume] could be 200 petajoules a year. Now the shortfall in domestic market has never been anywhere near 200 petajoules a year. So if they did that, then it wouldn't just be a slight oversupply as Minister Bowen has been arguing, it would be a huge over supply.
> If you provide more gas to the market, obviously the chances are prices will fall, and the market will respond and supply will adjust.
> What that logically means is the domestic supply, because there are plenty of companies who already supply the domestic market, who are not LNG exporters, they would then be squeezed out, which is not the objective of this policy.
> The only way out of that is the government has to introduce some mechanism by which to give exemptions or waivers. And you can imagine how this could be gamed.
> This is not what everybody was hoping for in the first place. This is a market in which the government's going to be intensely involved trying to sort out how they make sure that that gas actually gets to the market without destroying the domestic market."
