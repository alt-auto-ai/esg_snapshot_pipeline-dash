# Progress in the face of adversity: Cali style
The chair of California's Energy Commission is navigating a fraught route between federal climate policy oblivion and meeting the state's ambitious climate targets
By [Kate Burgess](https://theenergy.co/person/kate-burgess)
![](https://theenergy.co/uploads/transforms/Articles/_hero/16177/David-MP-Bowen_2026-03-09-102058_wuoe.avif)
California Energy Commission chair David Hochschild greets Climate Change and Energy Minister Chris Bowen on the sidelines of Climate Action Week Sydney - Supplied [Get The Energy in your inbox](https://theenergy.co/subscribe)
When the chair of California’s Energy Commission accepted a speaking slot at Sydney’s Climate Action Week, he couldn’t have foreseen that his country’s president would have ignited a global energy crisis with an attack on Iranian soil just days prior.
The irony of proselytising on California’s wildly successful clean energy transition to an Australian audience as Donald Trump carries on dismantling US federal climate policy is not lost on David Hochschild.
He is the first to admit that Trump’s second administration has derailed progress and does not deny that the roadblocks erected for clean energy investors are real.
“There is a very strong sense in the state that the future of the country does happen in our state first. It’s obviously been a dispiriting year for the nation,” he tells  _The Energy_ on the sidelines of the CAW opening event in Sydney.
But for a state whose identity is so intrinsically linked with the environment, health and innovation and, like Australia, is already witnessing the most horrific consequences of weather extremes in its summer wildfires, he is adamant that California’s politicians and industry are by no means admitting defeat.
Pointing to an extremely strong “collective resolve” and favourable economics - it’s cheaper to build a new solar farm than a gas-fired power plant - Hochschild maintains that the states yield the upper hand to set the policies that drive the transition in a federal policy vacuum.
“ It's an interesting legal posture they're taking because by pulling back to the extent that they are and saying the federal government has no role here. That actually creates a legal opportunity for states to then regulate and actually be even stricter," he says, referring to the federal government’s repeal of the Environment Protection Agency’s Greenhouse Gas Endangerment Finding contained in a 2009 lawsuit that underpins the agency’s ability to regulate greenhouse gas emissions.
“We're saying there's no ability for the federal government to regulate anything around our climate. The more forward-thinking states could then ask, where would that logically start? Would it start with transport? It could start with cars, it could start with power plants.”

### Lofty ambitions
California - which is targeting 60 per cent of zero-carbon electricity by 2030 and 100 per cent by 2045 to help it reach net zero emissions by that same year - has historically played a leadership role among US states in climate policy and the transformation of energy and transport systems required to achieve its vision.
The state’s current flagship project is the Darden clean energy project, a US$5.5 billion, 1.15GW solar and storage project, that Google’s parent company Alphabet bought in December as part of its acquisition of its developer Intersect.
One of the US’ largest renewable energy projects to go ahead without a now-defunct renewable electricity production tax credit, Darden is among the first projects approved under the CEC’s opt-in certification program, a form of planning and environmental approval fast-tracking.
Hochschild, himself a former solar advocacy campaigner and solar panel manufacturer executive, is also talking up the next wave of electrification that California will need to fully transition its industrialised economy.

Of all the state’s myriad energy policies, Hochschild is particularly proud of an unlikely hero - a tweak in the construction code to require minimum energy efficiency standards in all new commercial and residential buildings - which he claims has amped up the electrification of homes because natural gas appliances can’t meet the new more stringent regulations.
“We build 120,000 new homes a year in California. Those are all-electric.”
When Climate Change Authority chair Matt Kean introduced Hochschild as part of a panel discussion on global climate action, he alluded to the importance of action from what he termed “sub-national” governments at times when national governments were not willing to play a leadership role.
Kean, a former Liberal politician who revitalised New South Wales’ energy and emissions policy suite with legislated climate targets and an ambitious electricity infrastructure roadmap during his time as state energy minister, evidently identified with Hochschild’s plight as a member of a climate-forward policy apparatus at a time of federal indifference.
