## The commercialisation challenge
[ Link copied ](https://www.industry.gov.au/news/cynata-therapeutics-developing-new-cell-therapies-treat-chronic-disease-and-acute-conditions#the-commercialisation-challenge "Copy link to this section")
Cynata Therapeutics is a clinical-stage biotechnology company developing next-generation mesenchymal stem cell (MSC) therapies through its proprietary Cymerus™ platform. 
MSC therapies shows significant potential in treating various medical conditions. However, manufacturing these therapies consistently and at commercial scale is a challenge when using traditional methods.
The Cymerus™ platform enables Cynata to mass-produce high quality MSC therapies consistently and at scale from a single donor. It enables them to preserve the potency of the therapies even if they are producing large quantities of them. 
Cynata is using this platform technology to develop MSC therapies that can:
  * treat acute graft-versus-host disease
  * treat diabetic foot ulcers
  * treat osteoarthritis
  * help prevent the rejection of transplanted kidney.


They are aiming to deliver these therapies to millions of people around the world. 
## Support from BioScience Managers and the Biomedical Translation Fund
[ Link copied ](https://www.industry.gov.au/news/cynata-therapeutics-developing-new-cell-therapies-treat-chronic-disease-and-acute-conditions#support-from-bioscience-managers-and-the-biomedical-translation-fund "Copy link to this section")
The Biomedical Translation Fund (BTF) played a vital role in enabling BioScience Managers to invest in Cynata Therapeutics. The support is helping Cynata to advance multiple clinical-stage programs to further develop the MSC therapies that can help treat the medical conditions mentioned.
The BTF provides companies with venture capital through licensed private sector fund managers. It helps to develop biomedical discoveries into tangible products, services and outcomes. The program contributes around half of the investment capital for funds to invest in biomedical discoveries that commercialise health and medical research outcomes.
Our department is delivering the BTF on behalf of the Australian Government Department of Health, Disability and Ageing. A total of $501.25 million is allocated for the program between government and private sector funding.
As a co-investment model, the Australian Government has provided a way for venture capital firms to invest in cutting-edge biotechnology at an earlier stage.

# Key powerline all but built, AEMO ‘cautiously positive’ on transition
Market operator sanguine in the face of delayed projects that are crucial to the transition
By [Ben Potter](https://theenergy.co/person/ben-potter)
![Project Energy Connect towers near Dinawan, NSW, substation](https://theenergy.co/uploads/transforms/Articles/_hero/20003/EnergyConnect-towers-at-Bundure.avif)
Transgrid says the NSW section of Project EnergyConnect is 97 per cent complete - Supplied
Project EnergyConnect, the new South Australia-NSW interconnector and first in a wave of controversial multibillion dollar transmission lines, is 97 per cent built and in the “pre-energisation” phase, a senior Transgrid executive said.
Jennifer Hughes, the NSW transmission monopoly’s head of delivery, told the Energy Users’ Association of Australia’s national conference the project had had some big problems, kicking off as Covid lockdowns struck and supply chain costs blew out.
But she said it was a critical project for Australia’s energy transition, which Australian Energy Market Operator Daniel Westerman told the same event he was now “cautiously positive” about thanks to a 33 gigawatt pipeline of big battery projects seeking grid connection, growing momentum in solar and wind projects and full gas storages.
![](https://theenergy.co/uploads/transforms/Photos/_hero/20015/DSC06185_SilasHarris.avif) Silas Harris
“PEC [Project EnergyConnect] is sitting at almost 97 per cent constructed. We are currently now in pre-energisation phases, so there’s a lot of testing happening. Line five is energising online, and line two is close to completion,” Hughes said. The project links renewable energy projects in southwestern NSW and SA to large demand centres and is especially important for the poorly interconnected SA grid.
She said Transgrid, which is seeking regulatory approval to add another $1 billion to the NSW share of PEC’s regulatory asset base, which would mean it could charge tariffs based on a guaranteed return on investment, is applying the lessons learned from the project to its other big transmission projects.
These are the $4.9 billion Humelink, which will connect the giant Snowy 2.0 pumped hydro project to the greater Sydney main grid, and the NSW half of VNI West, connecting heavily curtailed renewable energy in western Victoria to NSW and Snowy Hydro, which has seen its combined (Victorian and NSW) budget triple to at least $7 billion.
Alistair Parker, CEO of VicGrid, said no-one could argue that the Victorian part of VNI West or Western Renewables Link — another troubled Victorian project — had got off to a good start but the agency, which assumed control of the state’s grid planning last November, was re-engaging with communities to try to get them moving.
### 'On time and in full'
![](https://theenergy.co/uploads/transforms/Photos/_hero/20017/DSC06085_SilasHarris.avif) AEMO's CEO Daniel Westerman is now "cautiously positive" about Australia's clean energy transition - Silas Harris
Westerman said the transmission projects were part of a slate of renewable generation, storage and transmission projects that “must be delivered on time and in full” for the energy transition to proceed without outages and other reliability and system security issues.
He said the addition of 12GW of wind and utility solar, 9GW of rooftop solar and 6GW of big batteries to the grid since the horror winter of 2022 when the NEM was suspended, as well as the way in which the grid was meeting the challenges thrown up by the Strait of Hormuz closure, gave him confidence for the coming winter.
Westerman told _The Energy_ on the sidelines that outages of ageing coal power stations remained “the biggest risk on our grid”, and there were still challenges with transmission costs and securing final investment decisions for wind projects and gas plants, but “momentum is building ... and the industry is delivering the projects that are needed”.
“But am I sanguine and positive about the next few years? Yeah, I think I am.”
This extends to a possible increase in forecasts of data centre power demand, which AEMO is working through, although the proof would be when the Yallourn and Eraring coal plants shut down in 2028 and 2029, taking a combined 4.3GW out of the NEM.
![](https://theenergy.co/uploads/transforms/Photos/_hero/20019/DSC06018_SilasHarris.avif) Former Energy Security Board chair Kerrry Schott: "There's no way that gas plants are feasible without having some sort of allowance to make an availability charge." - Silas Harris
### Gas needs 'availability charge'
Kerry Schott, the former chair of the disbanded Energy Security Board, agreed that gas plants were still critical for so-called “dunkelflautes” — wind and solar droughts during southeastern winter freezes — because batteries could not provide such "long-duration" storage.
“For the moment, that demand must be met by gas, and there's no way that gas plants are feasible without having some sort of allowance to make an availability charge for the insurance that they would provide just by sitting there and being able to turn on when you need them.
“It's particularly important, I think, for large users who must have continuous supply and must have continuous heat at particular temperatures.”
She also took aim at AEMO’s Integrated System Plan, which she said was “less than useful” because of unrealistic assumptions” such as that Victoria would have 6GW of offshore wind by 2035.
