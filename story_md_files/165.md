# Back to the drawing board on consumer pricing
What value does the consumer electricity market really offer? Until this question is truly addressed, any attempt at reform can only be piecemeal writes Ron Ben-David in this guest post
By [Ron Ben-David](https://theenergy.co/person/ron-ben-david)
![](https://theenergy.co/uploads/transforms/Articles/_hero/16170/20240515186752362685-original.avif)
The consumer energy market is complex, and becoming more so - Jono Searle/AAP [Get The Energy in your inbox](https://theenergy.co/subscribe)
Despite being about more than just network charges, the Australian Energy Market Commission’s self-initiated pricing review has ignited a [firestorm](https://theenergy.co/article/rule-maker-defends-pricing-review-amid-fixed-charges-backlash).
It was reckless of the rule maker to propose a shift to greater fixed charges without first establishing a clear analytical [framework](https://theenergy.co/article/pricing-reform-needs-stronger-foundations) for identifying and solving the problem it’s claiming to address.
The AEMC draft report proposes an assortment of largely unrelated interventions. The report does not systematically peel back the many layers of complexity, behaviours, constraints and other realities that constitute the consumer energy market. Instead, it relies on sweeping assumptions about markets, competition, choice, consumers and price signals.
Consumers' best hope now is that governments establish a truly independent inquiry with a broad mandate to investigate the design, structure and regulation of the consumer-facing energy market.
The AEMC’s draft report has emboldened one group of people and enraged another without offering a clear conceptual or evidentiary framework for resolving the argument.
On one side of the brawl, we find Team C. It supports the current approach where a proportion of networks’ sunk costs are recovered through variable consumer charges. Team C considers it is fair that high users of the grid should contribute more to its cost base via variable charges.
A faction within Team C also considers higher variable charges help motivate consumer investment in distributed energy resources (DER). And let’s face it, there is also a sub-faction of consumers who are anxious to preserve the value of their sunk investments in solar panels and batteries.
Team E takes the alternate view. It supports higher fixed charges because it considers equity is better achieved when consumers contribute more equally to network costs. Adherents of this view consider current pricing arrangements allow consumers who invest in solar panels and batteries to avoid contributing to the network’s sunk costs, even though they continue to rely on the network.
Aligned with Team E are proponents of electrification as a key priority for the energy transition. This faction supports lower variable charges as a way of encouraging consumers to replace their gas appliances and switch to electric vehicles.
The AEMC’s only response to the flare up has been to limply suggest a long (possibly 10-year) transition to higher fixed charges.
Let’s think about that for a moment.
A long transition will certainly help avoid bill shock for the most affected customers, and that is probably good, but it leaves consumers waiting for ten years for the outcome the AEMC considers to be most equitable.
That’s a very long time to ask consumers to wait. Across the country there could be over 20 elections in that time.
Worse still, where does a ten-year phase-in leave the DER versus electrification debate? For most of the ten-year transition period, price signals will be mathematically watered down to produce a melange of tariffs that serve neither objective.
### Unasked questions
There are too many fundamental questions not even asked by the AEMC in its pricing review. For example, why do consumer-facing electricity prices contain a fixed charge in the first place?
Suppliers throughout the economy face a mix of fixed and variable input costs but they do not pass these costs on to customers through a mix of fixed and variable charges. For example, petrol stations do not charge drivers a fixed entry fee separate from the price of fuel.
Elsewhere competition forces suppliers to bear the risk of converting fixed and variable costs into either variable (or occasionally fixed) prices only. So, why doesn’t this happen in the supposedly competitive retail energy market? Without getting to the bottom of this question, the AEMC’s contemplation of higher fixed charges is adrift in a sea of conflicting opinions about pricing.
Another key question not asked is: What ‘thing of value’ is being priced? The draft report misses this question entirely.
The AEMC appears to have convinced itself that the question of pricing-for-value is a matter resolved between competitive service providers and consumers. Fair point. But if that’s what the AEMC believes, then why has it even self-initiated this review?
Let’s then ask the question the AEMC does not: What value does the consumer electricity market offer? Well, it’s not electricity.
The reliability and quality of electricity do not depend on the number of service providers in the market, the variety of energy plans on offer, how often or effectively someone shops around, or how much they pay for their electricity.
In reality, consumers are shopping for a pricing structure that best reflects their actual (or hoped-for) electricity profile. Consumers bear the financial risk of getting this calculation wrong. Put simply, the value offered by the consumer energy market is the opportunity to manage this financial risk.
For consumers, the electricity market is more akin to a financial market (or perhaps a gambling market) than a standard product market. What does this mean for pricing? I’m not sure, but the draft report does not even recognise the question exists.
There are so many other unasked questions.
So, where to from here?
Unfortunately, the way forward is not going to come from the completion of the rule maker’s self-initiated pricing review.
No-one wants another energy market review, but an open-minded and multidisciplinary inquiry with a broad mandate to look across market design, structure and regulation as it relates to energy consumers would be the best way to create a market that supports all consumers through the energy transition.
